Gross Pollutant Separators

Prepared by Harvey H. Harper, Ph.D., P.E. · Environmental Research & Design, Inc. · January 2026

Overview

Gross pollutant separators (GPS) are engineered stormwater treatment devices designed to intercept and remove coarse solids, vegetation debris, litter, and settleable particles from urban stormwater runoff before it enters receiving water bodies. They are installed inline within stormwater conveyance systems — typically at pipe inlets, manholes, or junction structures — and require periodic maintenance to remove accumulated materials.

This module examines the principal GPS device categories used in Florida urban stormwater management, presents field monitoring data from a comprehensive study conducted in the Casselberry/Altamonte Springs area of Seminole County, and evaluates the performance and cost-effectiveness of these systems with respect to total nitrogen (TN), total phosphorus (TP), and total suspended solids (TSS) removal.

Key Concept

GPS devices are specifically designed for gross solids removal — litter, leaves, gravel, and coarse to medium sand. They provide only limited removal of dissolved and fine-particle nutrients, which distinguishes them fundamentally from wet detention ponds and other treatment BMPs.

Phosphorus Release from Organic Matter

A critical design consideration for all GPS devices is the behavior of captured organic material — particularly leaves and vegetation debris — once it enters an aquatic environment. Research has demonstrated that leaves and vegetation exhibit a rapid nutrient release upon contact with water, driven by microbial decomposition and leaching of intracellular nutrients.

The practical implications of phosphorus release from captured organic matter are significant for GPS system operation:

  • Timely removal of accumulated vegetation and debris is essential to prevent re-release of nutrients back into stormwater flows.
  • Organic material stored in a dry condition (above the water line) releases substantially less phosphorus than material held submerged in the sump.
  • GPS systems designed to keep captured vegetation dry between storm events — such as the Suntree Nutrient Separating Baffle Box — are engineered specifically to minimize this effect.
  • Anoxic conditions that develop in submerged sumps can promote the release of previously sorbed phosphorus from settled sediments.

Device Types

GPS devices evaluated in Florida stormwater management programs fall into three principal categories: baffle boxes, swirl/vortex separators, and inlet baskets. Each employs a different physical mechanism for solids removal and has distinct advantages and limitations with respect to flow capacity, maintenance requirements, and pollutant removal effectiveness.

Suntree Nutrient Separating Baffle Box

The Suntree Nutrient Separating Baffle Box is a second-generation baffle box design developed specifically to address the phosphorus re-release problem associated with submerged organic material. The system was designed and patented by Suntree Technologies and has been widely deployed in Florida stormwater management programs, particularly in Seminole County.

Structural Configuration

The unit is a precast concrete structure containing three primary chambers accessible via surface hatches. The structural depth is deliberately limited — the bottom of the concrete structure is only 4 feet below the invert pipe elevation — to facilitate maintenance access and solid material removal.

Key internal components include:

  • Filtration Screen System: A horizontal screen assembly mounted above the static water elevation. During storm events, incoming flow carries vegetation and litter onto the screen surface, where coarse material is retained while water passes through to lower chambers.
  • Boom: A floating containment boom positioned at the outlet side of the screen zone to capture floating debris.
  • Deflector: A submerged baffle plate in the lower chambers that redirects flow to promote gravitational settling of heavier sediment particles.
  • Skimmer: An outlet skimmer that draws water from below the surface, reducing the discharge of floating material.

Operational Design Advantages

The key engineering innovation of the Suntree design is the physical separation of captured organic material from the permanent water pool. Because vegetation and litter accumulate on the screen above the static water elevation, these materials dry out between storm events. This prevents the anaerobic decomposition and nutrient leaching that would otherwise occur if organic matter were submerged continuously. The screen system hinges to one side during maintenance, providing unobstructed access to the sediment-laden lower chambers.

EcoVault Unit

The EcoVault (also referenced in field documentation as the Ecosense unit) is an underground vault system designed for installation in rights-of-way and other space-constrained urban sites. It employs a combination of sedimentation, screening, and optional media filtration to achieve stormwater pollutant removal.

System Components

The EcoVault configuration includes several integrated treatment elements:

  • F.O.G. Baffle Wall: A primary baffle at the inlet zone that attenuates incoming flow velocity and promotes the separation of floatables, oils, and grease from the primary flow stream.
  • Ported Baffle Wall: A secondary baffle with openings near the base, directing flow through the settled solids zone while continuing to reduce turbulence in the main chamber.
  • Bottom Solids Screens: Removable screen panels installed horizontally on the vault floor. These screens facilitate access during maintenance by hinging open to expose accumulated sediment below.
  • Leaf/Debris Screen: An inclined screen assembly positioned to intercept coarse organic material and prevent it from reaching the outlet filter media.
  • Outlet Filter with Aluminum Silicate Media (Baffle Buddy Filter): An optional filter cartridge containing aluminum silicate, a reactive media capable of adsorbing dissolved phosphorus. This component significantly enhances total phosphorus removal beyond what is achievable by sedimentation alone.
  • Vault-Ox Equipment: An aeration device that introduces dissolved oxygen to the vault environment, counteracting the anoxic conditions that promote phosphorus release from settled sediments.

Swirl Separators

Swirl separators (also called vortex separators or hydrodynamic separators) use centrifugal force generated by a tangential inlet to separate settleable solids from the main flow stream. Two commercial designs — the CDS Unit and the Stormceptor — represent this technology category in the GPS field study.

CDS Unit

The Continuous Deflection Separation (CDS) unit uses a non-blocking perforated separation screen in combination with a balanced, continuously circulating flow to cause natural separation of solids from the stormwater flow. Storm water enters tangentially through the inlet, creating a swirling motion within the cylindrical body. The continuous low-velocity flow over the separation screen keeps the screen from blocking. An optional sump basket at the base of the unit captures separated solids for periodic removal.

High flows that exceed the design capacity bypass over a diversion weir, preventing scouring of previously accumulated materials during peak storm events.

Stormceptor

The Stormceptor is an in-line precast concrete structure in which stormwater enters through a top-mounted inlet pipe. The inlet configuration generates a swirling motion within the central treatment chamber, directing settleable solids to a lower sump region while treated water exits through an outlet riser. Floatable materials are retained by a surface seal created by the outlet riser arrangement.

Performance Considerations

Important Limitation

Published pollutant removal efficiencies for swirl separators are typically based on performance at or near the unit's design flow capacity. At lower flow rates, the swirling motion that drives solids separation is proportionally reduced, resulting in diminished removal efficiency. Because a large fraction of annual runoff volume occurs during smaller, more frequent storms — well below design capacity — actual annual average performance may be substantially lower than laboratory or design-capacity test results suggest.

An additional consideration for swirl separators with permanent wet sumps is the anoxic environment that develops in the submerged solids storage zone. Under anaerobic conditions, previously sorbed phosphorus can be released from settled sediments back into the water column, potentially converting the device from a net phosphorus sink to a net phosphorus source during low-flow periods between storm events.

Inlet Baskets

Inlet baskets (also called curb inlet baskets or inlet inserts) are relatively simple devices installed within existing curb inlet structures. They consist of a screen basket that intercepts coarse debris before it enters the storm drain system, and a shelf bracket system that holds the basket in place while allowing bypass flow during high-intensity events.

Suntree High-Capacity Curb Inlet Basket

The Suntree high-capacity inlet basket design employs a tiered filtration approach within a standard concrete catchbasin structure. The shelf system supports a filtration basket containing three screen zones arranged from coarsest to finest (Coarse / Medium / Fine), allowing progressively finer particle removal while a bypass pathway accommodates flows that exceed basket capacity. The underlying concrete catchbasin provides additional sediment settling capacity below the basket assembly.

A critical limitation of inlet basket systems is their applicability: they provide meaningful pollutant removal only in watersheds with significant vegetation debris loading — primarily residential areas with substantial tree canopy that generates seasonal leaf fall. In areas without this debris input, inlet baskets accumulate relatively little material and contribute negligibly to pollutant load reduction on an annual basis.

Drainage Basin Characteristics

The study sites were served by residential drainage basins with varying pre-treatment conditions. Understanding the differences in watershed drainage configurations is important for interpreting the monitoring results, as the degree of pre-treatment influences the pollutant concentrations and particle size distributions entering each GPS unit.

Ecosense Baffle Box Drainage Areas (G-Series)

The Lake Hodge and Gee Creek baffle box sites were served by two distinct sub-basins representing different residential drainage systems:

  • Sub-basin G-1 (20.98 acres): Curb and gutter drainage from a residential area with no stormwater pre-treatment. Runoff from impervious surfaces travels directly through curb-and-gutter conveyances to the baffle box inlet without passing through any vegetated treatment areas.
  • Sub-basin G-2 (29.98 acres): Roadside swale drainage from a residential area with swale pre-treatment. Runoff flows through vegetated roadside swales prior to reaching the baffle box, providing some gravitational settling and filtration before the GPS device.

EcoVault, CDS Unit, and Inlet Insert Sites (H-Series)

Three sub-basins served the EcoVault baffle box, CDS unit, and inlet insert monitoring sites. All three were residential curb-and-gutter systems without pre-treatment:

  • Sub-basin H-3 (21.37 acres): Residential area with curb and gutter drainage and no runoff pre-treatment.
  • Sub-basin H-4 (2.71 acres): Smaller residential drainage area with curb and gutter drainage and no pre-treatment.
  • Sub-basin H-5 (4.90 acres): Residential area with curb and gutter drainage and no pre-treatment.

Monitoring Equipment Configuration

Each study site was equipped with flow measurement devices and automated stormwater samplers to collect time-proportional or flow-proportional composite samples from both the inlet and outlet of each GPS unit during storm events. Monitoring configurations varied by device type and site access conditions.

Suntree Baffle Box Monitoring — Lake Concord Site

The Lake Concord Suntree Baffle Box site provided access to both an internal cutaway model view and field photographs of the installed unit. The Lake Concord installation receives discharge from an 18-inch reinforced concrete pipe (RCP) and discharges to Lake Concord. The unit's three access hatches on the top surface provide maintenance access to the screen assembly and lower sediment chambers.

Gee Creek EcoVault Monitoring Equipment

The Gee Creek EcoVault site was equipped with autosamplers housed in covered enclosures accessible via hatches built into the vault access structure. The monitoring installation included:

  • Dual automated compositing samplers positioned on a wooden support platform within the access chamber
  • Leaf/debris screen assembly visible within the vault interior
  • Outlet filter cartridge containing aluminum silicate media, positioned to intercept the treated effluent stream prior to final discharge

Lake Hodge EcoVault Monitoring Equipment

The Lake Hodge EcoVault site was a higher-profile installation requiring a more extensive monitoring infrastructure. The site included a fenced compound containing the EcoVault unit and an adjacent autosampler shelter. Key monitoring features included:

  • Insulated equipment shelter housing inflow and outflow automated samplers
  • A 52-inch by 64-inch concrete outflow structure visible in the field
  • The EcoVault unit incorporated both a leaf/debris screen and an outlet filter with aluminum silicate media at this location

Maintenance and Cleanout Procedures

GPS devices require regular maintenance to remove accumulated solids, prevent re-suspension and nutrient release, and maintain hydraulic capacity. The frequency of required maintenance is a direct function of watershed solids loading, the percentage of tree canopy cover in the drainage area, and seasonal leaf fall patterns.

Baffle Box Cleanout — Lake Hodge Site

Cleanout operations at the Lake Hodge baffle box illustrate the typical maintenance sequence for baffle box GPS devices. The four-step process documented during field monitoring included:

  • Vegetation removal from screen: Accumulated leaves, branches, and organic debris are manually removed from the filtration screen assembly. The collected material includes both recognizable plant debris and miscellaneous litter items such as beverage containers.
  • Water pumping from sump: Standing water is pumped from the lower sediment chamber to reduce the volume of material requiring vacuum removal and to expose the accumulated sediment layer.
  • Solids vacuum removal: A Vactor truck (combination jet/vacuum maintenance vehicle) is used to vacuum the accumulated sediment slurry from the lower chambers through the access hatches.
  • Post-cleaning screen inspection: Following cleanout, screens are inspected for damage and structural integrity before being returned to service.

Baffle Box Cleanout — Lake Howell Site

The Lake Howell baffle box cleanout sequence follows an equivalent procedure, with the same four operational steps: vacuum truck mobilization to the site, pumping of standing water from the bottom chambers, vacuuming of accumulated solids from the chambers, and post-cleaning screen inspection and assessment.

Solids Characterization

Analysis of the material removed from GPS units during monitoring-period cleanout events provides important information about the composition of captured solids and the relationship between particle size and nutrient content.

Visual Character of Removed Material

Solids removed from the four baffle box study sites had a consistent visual character: a dark gray to black mixed organic and mineral matrix containing fibrous plant material, fine sand, and silt. The material from all four sites (Lake Hodge, Gee Creek, San Pablo, and Lake Concord baffle boxes) appeared comparable in composition, reflecting the similar residential land use and vegetation types in the Seminole County study area.

Key Finding

GPS devices are highly effective at removing large particles with low nutrient content. The fine particle fractions (<75 μm) that carry the highest phosphorus concentrations (up to 20,000 μg/g) represent less than 2% of the captured mass but would contribute disproportionately to nutrient loads if they could be captured. GPS hydraulics and sedimentation physics favor removal of the coarser, less nutrient-rich fractions.

Interpretation of Performance Data

Several patterns emerge from the monitoring data:

  • TSS removal is consistently high across all GPS device types, ranging from 73–94%. This reflects the primary design function of these units — physical separation of settleable and screenable solids.
  • Total nitrogen removal is minimal (2–14%), consistent with the predominantly dissolved and fine-particle-bound form of nitrogen in urban stormwater runoff. GPS devices do not provide meaningful nitrogen removal.
  • Total phosphorus removal is highly variable and strongly dependent on the presence of the aluminum silicate outlet filter. The Lake Hodge EcoVault with outlet filter achieved 57% TP removal at a life-cycle cost of $1,755/kg — the most cost-effective configuration. Without the outlet filter, TP removal drops to 7–12% across all other GPS types, with life-cycle costs exceeding $15,000–$25,000 per kilogram removed.
  • Present worth removal costs for nutrients are extremely high, typically 1–2 orders of magnitude greater than for wet detention ponds, which achieve comparable or superior nutrient removal at costs of $100–$500/kg.

Conclusions

Gross Pollutant Separator Performance Summary

GPS devices effectively remove litter, leaves, gravel, and coarse-to-medium sand from stormwater. However, their nutrient removal performance is limited:

  • Total N: 10–12% typical removal
  • Total P: 8–12% typical removal (without reactive media filter)
  • TSS: 30–60% typical removal

Life-cycle removal costs for GPS units are 1–2 orders of magnitude greater than wet detention for equivalent nutrient mass removal.

Appropriate Applications

GPS devices are suited for deployment in specific contexts where gross solids represent a meaningful fraction of the total pollutant loading and where the operational and aesthetic benefits of debris removal justify the cost:

  • Residential areas with large tree canopy that generate significant seasonal leaf fall and organic debris loads in stormwater
  • Urban areas with documented litter issues, where gross pollutant management has aesthetic, nuisance, or regulatory significance
  • Locations where downstream infrastructure protection from coarse debris is required
Design Guidance

GPS devices should not be used as the primary or sole BMP for nutrient removal projects. For sites where total phosphorus or total nitrogen load reduction is the regulatory objective, wet detention ponds, stormwater wetlands, or other treatment-volume BMPs are substantially more effective and cost-efficient. GPS devices should be considered as pre-treatment or supplemental controls for gross solids only, not as substitutes for properly sized treatment BMPs.

EcoVault with Aluminum Silicate Filter — Exception

The EcoVault configuration with the aluminum silicate outlet filter represents an important exception to the general GPS performance characterization. At the Lake Hodge site, this configuration achieved 57% total phosphorus removal at a present worth cost of $1,755/kg — substantially better than other GPS configurations and approaching the range of some less-efficient conventional BMPs. However, this performance depends on periodic replacement of the reactive filter media, and the long-term cost-effectiveness of media replacement must be factored into project planning.
