Section 01

Concentration-Based Method

Fundamental Relationship
Annual Loading = Runoff Concentration × Annual Runoff Volume
evaluated for each land use category

The concentration-based method calculates pollutant mass loadings by multiplying the event mean concentration (EMC) of a constituent in runoff by the total annual runoff volume generated from each land use category. Summing across all land uses in the watershed yields the total annual pollutant load entering the stormwater management system.

The following example illustrates the method for a mixed-use catchment with three land use types and Total Phosphorus as the constituent of interest:

Land Use Total P Conc.
(mg/L)		 × Runoff Volume
(ac-ft/yr)		 = Total P Mass
(kg/yr)
Single Family Residential 0.30 × 120 = 44.4
Low Intensity Commercial 0.15 × 160 = 29.6
Industrial 0.31 × 64 = 24.5
Totals 344 98.5
Section 02

Calculation of Pollutant Loadings

The general form of the annual pollutant loading equation, incorporating unit conversion factors to yield results in kg/yr, is presented below. This formula accounts for area, annual rainfall depth, the runoff coefficient (ROC), and the event mean concentration for each land use category.

Annual Load Formula — Unit-Dimensional Form

Load (kg/yr) = Σ from i=1 to n of:

(Ai) × 43,560 ft²/acre × R × CVi × 1 ft/12 inches × 7.48 gal/ft³ × 3.785 liter/gal × Ci × 1 kg/10⁶ mg
Ai= area of land use category i (acres)
n= number of different land use categories
Ci= concentration of runoff constituent in land use category i (mg/L)
R= annual rainfall at site (inches/yr)
CVi= ROC (runoff coefficient) value for land use category i (dimensionless)

Key Principle: Annual Loading = EMC concentration × Annual Runoff Volume. The unit conversion chain in the formula above converts from mixed English/SI units (acres, inches, gallons) to a final mass result in kilograms per year.

Section 03

Example Calculation — Site Characterization

The following worked example demonstrates the complete runoff loading calculation process for a 100-acre single-family residential development evaluated at three Florida locations representing different rainfall zones.

Step 1 — Land Use

Site Composition
  • 90 acres of single-family residential development
  • 5 acres of stormwater management systems
  • 5 acres of preserved wetlands

Step 2 — Ground Cover and Soil Types

Hydrologic Parameters
  • Residential areas will be covered with lawns in good condition
  • Soil types in Hydrologic Soil Group (HSG) D

Step 3 — Impervious and DCIA Areas

Residential areas will be 25% impervious, with 75% of the impervious area classified as Directly Connected Impervious Area (DCIA).

Impervious Area Calculation
Impervious Area = 25% × 90 ac = 22.50 acres
DCIA Area Calculation
DCIA Area = 22.50 acres × 0.75 = 16.88 acres
% DCIA of Developed Area
% DCIA = (16.88 ac / 90.0 ac) × 100 = 18.7% of developed area
= 18.7% DCIA
Section 04

Post-Development Annual Runoff Generation

Note on Area Deductions: The post-development loading reflects the loading discharging to the stormwater management system from the watershed. The area of the treatment system itself is not included, and the preserved wetland area is excluded since it is the same under both pre- and post-development conditions.

Step 4 — Post-Development Runoff Volumes by Location

The 90-acre residential development area is evaluated at three Florida project locations representing distinct rainfall zones. The Curve Number (CN) value for the post-development condition is 81.4, derived from the impervious fraction and HSG D lawn areas using TR-55 methodology.

Project Location Area (acres) Impervious Areas DCIA Non-DCIA CN Value Annual Rainfall (in) Annual ROC Value Runoff (ac-ft/yr)
% acres acres %
Pensacola 90 25 22.5 16.68 18.75 81.4 65.5 0.304 149.3
Orlando 90 25 22.5 16.68 18.75 81.4 50.0 0.253 94.8
Key West 90 25 22.5 16.68 18.75 81.4 40.0 0.266 79.8

The results demonstrate that despite identical site characteristics, annual runoff volumes vary significantly across Florida's rainfall zones — from 79.8 ac-ft/yr in Key West to 149.3 ac-ft/yr in Pensacola — reflecting the strong influence of geographic rainfall distribution on stormwater loading.

Section 05

Generated Loading to Stormwater Pond

Step 5 — Event Mean Concentration Values

Mean EMC values for total nitrogen (TN) and total phosphorus (TP) in single-family residential runoff are applied to the post-development runoff volumes to calculate mass loadings entering the stormwater pond.

Total Nitrogen — EMC 1.87 mg/L
Total Phosphorus — EMC 0.301 mg/L

Step 5a — Pensacola (Zone 1) Worked Calculations

The following unit-dimensional calculations use the Pensacola runoff volume of 149.3 ac-ft/yr:

TN Load from Single-Family Area — Pensacola
(149.3 ac-ft/yr) × (43,560 ft²/ac) × (7.48 gal/ft³) × (3.785 L/gal) × (1.87 mg/L) × (1 kg/10⁶ mg)
= 344 kg TN/yr
TP Load from Single-Family Area — Pensacola
(149.3 ac-ft/yr) × (43,560 ft²/ac) × (7.48 gal/ft³) × (3.785 L/gal) × (0.301 mg/L) × (1 kg/10⁶ mg)
= 55.4 kg TP/yr

Post-Development Loading Summary by Location

Location TN Loading (kg/yr) TP Loading (kg/yr)
Pensacola (Zone 1) 344 55.4
Orlando (Zone 2) 219 35.2
Key West (Zone 3) 184 29.6

Pre-Development EMC Values — Weighted Composite

Since the CN values for both land cover types are identical, annual runoff volumes are proportional to cover percentage. Composite runoff concentrations are calculated on a weighted basis using cover fractions as weights. The weighted EMC values for Total Nitrogen and Total Phosphorus are derived as follows:

Step 6a — Pre-Development Worked Calculations (Pensacola)

Using the Pensacola pre-development runoff volume of 79.9 ac-ft/yr and weighted composite EMC values:

TN Load from Pre-Developed Areas — Pensacola
(79.9 ac-ft/yr) × (43,560 ft²/ac) × (7.48 gal/ft³) × (3.785 L/gal) × (1.07 mg/L) × (1 kg/10⁶ mg)

Section 07

Required Removal Efficiencies

Step 7 — Calculate Required Removal to Achieve Post ≤ Pre-Development Loadings

The final step calculates the pollutant removal efficiency that must be achieved by the stormwater management system so that post-development mass loadings do not exceed pre-development levels. Required removal efficiency is calculated as:

Required Removal Efficiency
Required Removal (%) = [(Post-Load − Pre-Load) / Post-Load] × 100

The table below summarizes pre- and post-development loadings and required removal efficiencies for both Total Nitrogen and Total Phosphorus across all three project locations.

Project Location Total Nitrogen Total Phosphorus
Pre-Load (kg/yr) Post-Load (kg/yr) Required Removal (%) Pre-Load (kg/yr) Post-Load (kg/yr) Required Removal (%)
Pensacola (Zone 1) 105.4 344 69.3 2.56 55.4 95.4
Orlando (Zone 2) 54.9 219 74.9 1.33 35.2 96.2
Key West (Zone 3) 52.3 184 71.6 1.27 29.6 95.7

Key Observation: Total phosphorus removal requirements exceed 95% at all three locations. This reflects both the low TP concentrations in pre-development natural vegetation runoff (0.026 mg/L weighted composite) and the relatively higher TP concentrations from developed residential areas (0.301 mg/L). Total nitrogen requirements range from approximately 69–75%, driven by similar dynamics. These high removal requirements underscore the importance of properly designed and maintained stormwater BMPs.
