Section 2

What Is a Retention BMP?

Regulatory Definition

"Retention" means a system designed to prevent the discharge of a given volume of stormwater runoff into surface waters in the state by on-site storage. Examples are systems such as excavated or natural depression storage areas, permeable pavement, rain gardens, exfiltration, swales, and others.

Sources: Applicant's Handbook, Volume 1, Section 2 — Definitions and Terms, June 28, 2024; and Harper and Baker, Evaluation of Current Stormwater Design Criteria within the State of Florida, 2007.

Retention systems differ fundamentally from detention systems in that their goal is to prevent stormwater from entering surface waters entirely — through infiltration, evapotranspiration, or re-use — rather than simply attenuating the timing or rate of discharge. This on-site storage and volume reduction mechanism makes retention a critical tool for achieving nutrient load reductions, particularly where Outstanding Florida Waters (OFW) or impaired water body standards apply.

Common retention BMP types include:

Excavated or natural depression storage areas (dry retention basins), permeable pavement systems, rain gardens, exfiltration trenches and vaults, and grass swales. Each operates through the same core mechanism: capturing a defined volume of runoff and allowing it to infiltrate, evaporate, or otherwise be retained on-site rather than discharged.

Section 3

Retention Effectiveness — A Function of Runoff Volume Capture

The effectiveness of a retention BMP is primarily a function of the fraction of annual runoff volume it captures. This capture fraction depends on the treatment depth (TD) relative to the rainfall and runoff characteristics at the site, and critically, on the rate at which the stored volume recovers between storm events.

Recovery Rate and Annual Effectiveness

In fast-recovery retention systems — such as exfiltration into limestone formations or systems with pump-assisted recovery — annual effectiveness is meaningfully higher than in systems relying on slower soil infiltration (Hydrologic Soil Groups A and B). Two recovery scenarios bracket the design space:

Systems with recovery times less than 3 hours achieve higher annual volume capture for a given treatment depth, while systems with recovery times up to 72 hours capture less annual volume because storage fills before recovering between events. For systems with recovery of 72 hours or less, apply the removal efficiency tables provided in the new rule (Applicant's Handbook Volume 1, Appendix O — 80 tables total).

For fast-recovery systems (less than 3 hours), the annual effectiveness from the standard Appendix O tables can be increased by up to 10%, using the adjustment graph relating yearly rainfall volume capture percentage to design treatment depth.

Section 4

Efficiency Related to Site, Rainfall, and Treatment Depth

Retention efficiency is determined through long-term simulation of a catchment area at a given rainfall site for a specific design treatment depth (TD). The treatment depth is defined as:

Treatment Depth Definition TD (inches) = Treated Volume (ac-ft) × 12 in/ft ÷ Catchment Area (ac)

BMPFast develops efficiency values from 280 simulations per treatment depth per rainfall site. Harper's published tables provide retention efficiency for TDs of 0.25, 0.50, 0.75 inches, etc. — values not contained in the Applicant's Handbook directly. BMPFast allows the user to enter any TD value and interpolates accordingly.

BMPFast Retention Efficiency Worksheet

The Retention Efficiency worksheet in BMPFast allows direct lookup and interpolation of efficiency for any combination of rainfall zone, treatment depth, NDCIA Curve Number, and DCIA percentage. The efficiency output represents the fraction of annual runoff volume captured.

Section 5

Plot of Effectiveness for Discovery

The BMPFast Plot function generates a Retention Storage Curve — a graphical display of Treatment Efficiency (%) on the y-axis versus Retention Depth in inches on the x-axis, for the specific catchment conditions entered. This curve is a powerful "discovery" tool that allows the engineer to visualize how efficiency changes as available storage depth increases or decreases.

📈
Figure 5-A — Retention Treatment Efficiency Plot
Plot of Retention Storage Curve: Treatment Efficiency (%) on y-axis (0–100%), Retention Depth (inches) on x-axis (0–4 inches). Curve rises steeply from 0 to approximately 0.8 inches then flattens. A red dot marker appears at 65% efficiency corresponding to the current design TD. Menu options shown: Print, Copy, Back, Nitrogen, Phosphorus.

FIG 5-A · BMPFast Plot of Retention Storage Curve — Discovery Tool

The discovery application of this plot is straightforward: if a target removal efficiency of 90% is required, the engineer can read directly from the curve what treatment depth would be required to achieve that efficiency. This enables the critical design question:

Design Discovery Question

If we need 90% removal — do we have sufficient area and storage volume to accommodate a higher treatment depth? If so, what is the treatment depth required?

The "Get Depth" function within BMPFast allows the engineer to input a target efficiency and solve for the required retention depth directly, complementing the graphical approach. The Provided Retention Depth in inches over catchment area is reported as output (e.g., 0.9436 inches for a 90% target in the example scenario).

Section 6

Sizing the Retention BMP — Treatment Depth and Treatment Volume

The fundamental sizing relationship for a retention BMP connects the Treatment Depth (TD), Treatment Volume (TV), and the catchment area served. These three quantities are related by:

Core Sizing Relationship TD = TV / Area TV = Area × TD TD (in) = TV (ac-ft) × 12 in/ft ÷ Area (ac)

Example Calculation

For a catchment with an area of 12 acres, DCIA of 27%, and a non-DCIA CN of 64, a target annual removal efficiency of 64.5% requires a treatment depth of 0.33 inches. The corresponding calculations are:

Given: Area = 12 ac, DCIA = 27%, CN (NDCIA) = 64, Target = 64.5% efficiency From retention efficiency table: TD = 0.33 inch TV = 12 acres × 0.33 in ÷ 12 in/ft = 0.33 ac-ft = 14,375 cubic feet Verification (given volume → depth): TD = TV / Area = 0.33 ac-ft × 12 in/ft ÷ 12 acres = 0.33 inch ✓

This bidirectional calculation is used throughout BMPFast analysis: either working from a target efficiency to size the required volume, or from an available volume to determine achievable efficiency.

Example Problem — Project Description

The following example demonstrates a complete retention basin analysis using BMPFast for a residential development project in Central Florida.

Project Characteristics

Site Description

A retention basin is planned as a BMP for a 12.0-acre residential subdivision. The development site is located in Orange County near Orlando, FL on Hydrologic Group Soil A. In the pre-application meeting, the watershed characteristics, performance standards, and average annual rainfall were determined.

Example Project Input Parameters
Parameter Pre-Development Condition Post-Development Condition
Land Use Citrus Single-family residential
Non-DCIA Curve Number (CN) 50 64
DCIA (%) 0.0% 27%
Total Area (acres) 12.0
Average Annual Rainfall 50 inches (Florida Zone 2)
Hydrologic Soil Group Group A
Performance Standard OFW — Outstanding Florida Waters (annual nutrient loads)
Available Storage Volume Range 0.33 to 2.5 acre-feet

Treatment Depth Range

The retention basin area is fixed at 12 acres served area, and the available storage range translates to the following treatment depths:

Treatment Depth Calculation — Minimum and Maximum Minimum TD = 0.33 ac-ft × 12 in/ft ÷ 12 acres = 0.33 inches Maximum TD = 2.50 ac-ft × 12 in/ft ÷ 12 acres = 2.50 inches Result: OFW nutrient removal requirements can be met with a TD of 2.167 ac-ft (within the available range of 0.33 to 2.5 ac-ft)

Figure 10-A — Retention Storage Curve — Example Project
Plot of Retention Storage Curve for example project. Treatment Efficiency (%) on y-axis (0–100%), Retention Depth (inches) on x-axis (0–4 inches). Curve rises steeply, with a red dot marker at approximately 78% efficiency at the current design treatment depth (~0.6 inches). Curve approaches 100% asymptotically at depths above 2 inches.

FIG 10-A · BMPFast Discovery Plot — Example Project Retention Curve (78% efficiency at current TD)

The plot provides immediate engineering insight: given the curve shape, the relationship between incremental increases in treatment depth and corresponding gains in efficiency is clearly visible. In the steep portion of the curve (below approximately 1 inch TD), each additional increment of storage depth yields significant efficiency improvement. In the flat portion of the curve (above approximately 2 inches TD), additional storage provides diminishing returns.

The plot enables discovery: by reading the curve, the designer can identify how much additional storage would be needed to meet a higher removal target, or can confirm that a reduced storage volume will still achieve an acceptable efficiency level.

Section 11

Adding Groundwater Protection — Springs Impact Analysis

For sites where the retention basin infiltrates into groundwater that ultimately discharges to springs or other sensitive receiving waters, BMPFast includes a Groundwater Discharge Analysis capability. This analysis estimates both the recharge volume entering the groundwater and the nutrient concentration of that recharge.

To activate groundwater analysis, the user selects "Yes" for the Conduct Groundwater Discharge Analysis option on the project navigation screen. This adds a fourth step — Summary Treatment Report — to the navigation workflow and activates the groundwater discharge output fields.

This analysis is particularly relevant for projects within springshed protection zones, where the nitrogen and phosphorus concentrations in groundwater recharge are subject to regulatory scrutiny. The retention basin, by concentrating infiltration to a defined location, provides a mechanism for adding filter media to reduce nutrient loads to groundwater — the topic addressed in the following section.

By placing Biologically Active Media (BAM) in the bottom of the retention basin, the nitrogen concentration of groundwater recharge is substantially reduced. BMPFast computes the following groundwater discharge results with BAM treatment:

Section 14

Module Summary — What We Learned

This module demonstrated the complete workflow for analyzing a retention basin BMP using BMPFast, from conceptual definition through sizing, performance discovery, and groundwater protection analysis.

01 · Definition
A retention BMP prevents discharge of a given stormwater volume into surface waters through on-site storage, infiltration, and volume reduction — distinct from detention, which only attenuates discharge rate.
02 · BMPFast Navigation
The three-step workflow (Enter Catchment → Enter Treatment → Configure Catchments) supports annual mass removal analysis using retention. Groundwater discharge analysis adds a fourth step.
03 · Discovery via Plot
The Retention Storage Curve plot reveals how treatment efficiency varies with storage depth for site-specific conditions, enabling rapid identification of the storage depth needed to achieve any target removal efficiency.
04 · Groundwater Protection
BAM filter media placed in the retention basin bottom reduces groundwater recharge nitrogen concentration from 2.07 mg/L to 0.517 mg/L — a 75% reduction — while maintaining 10+ year service life.
05 · Service Life Worksheet
The Tools → Filter sizing worksheet computes required media volume and service life based on catchment TP load, sorption rate, media density, and filter volume provided. 5,400 CF yields 10.1 years for this example.
06 · Related BMPs
Several other BMPs in the BMPFast toolbox — including Exfiltration Trench/Vault, Permeable Pavement, Vegetated Filter Strip, Rain Garden, Tree Well, and Swale — use the same underlying retention efficiency curves.
Related BMPs Using Retention Curves

The following BMPs in BMPFast also rely on the retention storage curves developed from long-term simulation: Exfiltration Trench/Vault, Permeable Pavement, Vegetated Filter Strip, Stormwater Harvesting, Rain Garden, Tree Well, and Swale. Understanding retention efficiency is therefore foundational to analyzing all volume-reduction-based BMPs in the toolbox.
