Section 01

Runoff Characteristics

Stormwater runoff concentrations are characterized by a high degree of variability, occurring both from event to event and within individual storm events. Understanding and accounting for this variability is essential to accurate water quality assessment and engineering design.

Sources of Variability

Variability in runoff concentrations is caused by differences in the following factors:

  • Rainfall Intensity
  • Rainfall Frequency
  • Soil Types
  • Land Use
  • Intensity of Land Use
  • Weather Patterns
Monitoring Protocol Requirement

Variability must be included in the monitoring protocol for runoff collection in order to determine representative annual EMC values. Data collected under a narrow range of conditions will not capture the full range of concentrations that occur over the annual cycle.

Use of NPDES Data

NPDES (National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System) data should not be used as the basis for annual EMC values, because NPDES data reflect runoff characteristics for specific, defined rain event conditions rather than the full annual spectrum. However, NPDES data are useful for comparing different sites because the data are collected in a standardized, consistent manner across locations.

Section 02

Runoff Variability

Runoff pollutant concentrations do not follow a normal (arithmetic) distribution. Instead, they exhibit a log-normal distribution, which means the geometric mean — rather than the arithmetic mean — is the appropriate measure of central tendency for runoff characterization data.

Normal arithmetic means are not the appropriate measure of central tendency for runoff concentration data. Using arithmetic means will overestimate typical concentrations due to the influence of high-value outliers.

Data from highway runoff monitoring on I-4 and Maitland Avenue (1980–1982) illustrate this clearly. Zinc concentration data for both total and dissolved fractions, when plotted on a log-probability scale against the percentage of values less than a stated concentration, show a linear relationship — consistent with a log-normal distribution. The data demonstrate more than a 10-fold difference between minimum and maximum measured values.

Section 03

Runoff Characteristics and Loadings

Runoff characteristics are applied in numerous engineering analyses, including:

  • Pollutant loading analyses
  • TMDL (Total Maximum Daily Load) calculations
  • Pre/post development loading evaluations

Event Mean Concentration (EMC)

Runoff concentrations are expressed as an Event Mean Concentration (EMC). The EMC represents the flow-weighted average concentration for an entire storm event and is calculated as:

EMC Definition
EMC = Pollutant Loading ÷ Runoff Volume
Where pollutant loading = total mass of pollutant discharged during storm event, and runoff volume = total stormwater volume discharged during the same event.

Annual EMC Values

An annual EMC value is determined by evaluating event EMC values over a range of rainfall depths and seasons. Annual EMC values are generally determined through field monitoring and typically require a minimum of 7–10 monitored events collected over a range of conditions to be statistically representative.

Annual Mass Loading Calculation

Annual Mass Loading
Annual Mass Loading = Annual Runoff Volume × Annual EMC
This relationship links the volumetric runoff calculation with water quality characterization to estimate total annual pollutant loads from a land use area.
Section 04

History of the Florida EMC Database

The original database of Florida runoff characteristics was developed by Environmental Research & Design, Inc. (ERD) in 1990 in support of the Tampa Bay SWIM (Surface Water Improvement and Management) Plan.

Original Database Development (1990)

A literature review was conducted to identify runoff EMC values for single land use categories in Florida. Approximately 100 studies were identified. Each study was evaluated for:

  • Adequacy of the data
  • Length of study period
  • Number of monitored events
  • Completeness of the dataset
  • Monitoring protocol followed

Original Selection Criteria

  • Monitoring site included a single land use category — the most difficult criterion to satisfy
  • At least 1 year of data collection; minimum of 5 events monitored in a flow-weighted fashion
  • Wide range of rainfall depths and antecedent dry periods included in monitored events
  • Seasonal variability included in monitored samples

Approximately 40 studies were selected for inclusion in the database. Values were summarized by general land use category. This was the first known compilation of EMC data for Florida. EMC values in this original compilation were calculated as simple arithmetic means.

Historical Note

The 1990 Tampa Bay SWIM database represented the first systematic statewide compilation of runoff EMC values organized by land use category in Florida — a foundational reference for stormwater engineering practice in the state.

Section 06

Original Stormwater Loading Rate Publication (1994)

In October 1994, ERD published a non-funded summary of the runoff data collected as part of the Tampa Bay SWIM study, titled Stormwater Loading Rate Parameters for Central and South Florida.

New Content in 1994 Publication

The 1994 publication included all data from the 1990 collection effort and added the following:

  • Addition of loading rate information for highway/transportation land use
  • Agriculture category divided into pasture, citrus, row crops, and general ag
  • Added new references for previous land uses
  • Included EMC values for wetlands based on available existing wetland data for central and south Florida:
    • TP = 0.10 mg/l (100 µg/l)
    • TN = 1.00 mg/l (1,000 µg/l)

Areal Loading Rates

The 1994 publication introduced and included areal loading rates for stormwater parameters expressed in units of kg/acre-year. However, ERD no longer supports the areal loading methodology. This approach has been superseded by volume-based loading calculations using annual runoff volumes and annual EMC values.

Section 12

Natural Area Monitoring Project

Objectives

The Natural Area Monitoring Project was an FDEP-funded project conducted by ERD to characterize runoff quality from common natural undeveloped upland vegetative communities in Florida. The data were collected specifically to support the pre-development runoff quality component of the Statewide Stormwater Rule. Florida State Parks were used as monitoring sites because they provide protected, representative natural communities with minimal anthropogenic disturbance.

Work Efforts

  • A total of 33 automated monitoring sites were established in 10 State Parks throughout Florida
  • Monitoring was conducted over a 14-month period from July 2007 through August 2008 to capture a variety of seasonal conditions, including both wet and dry seasons
  • A total of 318 samples were collected and analyzed for general parameters, nutrients, demand parameters, fecal coliform, and heavy metals

Section 15

Natural Community Classification Indices

Several GIS-based classification systems are available for mapping natural community types in Florida. Understanding the differences between these systems is important for correctly assigning pre-development EMC values in loading analyses.

1. Florida Vegetation and Land Cover (FFWCC)

This system is part of Florida's Natural Heritage Program at Florida State University and reflects existing land cover based on aerial photography for both developed and natural areas. The original survey conducted in the 1990s included 17 natural and semi-natural cover types, 4 land cover types reflecting disturbed land, and 1 water class. A 2003 update expanded coverage to 26 natural and semi-natural cover types, 16 land cover types reflecting disturbed land, and 1 water class. Coverage maps are available for all of Florida, though interpretation is subject to the limitations of aerial photography. A digital GIS version was released in 2004.

2. Florida Natural Areas Inventory (FNAI)

Developed by the Florida Department of Natural Resources (DNR), FNAI reflects original natural vegetation associations in Florida. Natural communities are characterized and defined by a combination of physiognomy, vegetation structure and composition, topography, landform, substrate, soil moisture condition, climate, and fire regime. Communities are named for their most characteristic biological or physical feature and are grouped into 6 Natural Community Categories with 13 Natural Community Groups and 66 sub-groups based on hydrology and vegetation. FNAI is the classification system used by the Florida State Park system. Coverage maps are not available for all of Florida, but this system was selected for the natural area characterization study due to its use in the State Park system.

Reference: http://fnai.org/PDF/AA_Short_Descriptions_Final_2010.pdf

3. Florida Cooperative Land Cover Map

Released in July 2010 and updated to Version 3.5 in November 2021, the Florida Cooperative Land Cover Map combines aspects of both the FFWCC and FNAI systems. The map is available in GIS format with primary responsibility assigned to FWC with assistance from FNAI. Reference: Home — Florida Natural Areas Inventory (fnai.org)

Section 16

Use of FLUCCS Codes in Loading Calculations

The Florida Land Use, Cover and Forms Classification System (FLUCCS) is widely used in permitting and planning documents. However, there are important limitations to be aware of when using FLUCCS codes as the basis for EMC-based loading calculations.

Known Problems with Direct FLUCCS Application

  • FLUCCS codes contain too much detail and often misclassify land use activities relative to their runoff characteristics
  • Runoff EMC data are not available for all listed FLUCCS land use categories

To address these limitations, FLUCCS codes have been converted to the closest general EMC categories based on anticipated runoff characteristics. This conversion is available in the BMPFast model. Practical Guidance

When performing pre/post development loading analyses, practitioners should map site land uses to the EMC categories described in Section 5 rather than using FLUCCS codes directly. The BMPFast model includes a built-in FLUCCS-to-EMC category conversion table to facilitate this process.
