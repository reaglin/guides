Section 01

Definitions

Stormwater management practices are broadly classified as either retention or detention systems. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to selecting appropriate best management practices (BMPs) and complying with regulatory requirements.

Retention

A group of stormwater practices where the treatment volume is evacuated by either percolation into groundwater or evaporation. Key characteristics:

  • No surface discharge for treatment volume
  • Substantial reduction in runoff volume
  • Retention practices include:
    • Infiltration/Retention pond
    • Harvesting (Reuse irrigation)
    • Underground storage systems
Detention

A group of stormwater practices where the treatment volume is detained for a period of time before release. Key characteristics:

  • Discharge of treatment volume over a period of days
  • No significant reduction in runoff volume
  • Detention practices include:
    • Wet detention
    • Dry detention

Section 03

Retention Pond

A retention (infiltration) pond is a dry pond designed to capture and infiltrate runoff into the groundwater. The treatment volume is recovered entirely by percolation; there is no control structure outlet for the pollution abatement volume. An overflow weir may be provided for on-line systems to pass peak attenuation storage.

Section 05

Roadside Swales

Roadside swales serve a dual function in stormwater management — they simultaneously convey runoff and provide treatment through infiltration. As linear retention systems, they are particularly well-suited for roadway corridors where traditional retention ponds are impractical.

Functional Characteristics

  • Combine conveyance and treatment within a single linear feature
  • Used as linear retention systems along roadway corridors
  • Swale blocks, check dams, or raised inlets may be used to impound water for infiltration
  • A large portion of the runoff infiltrates during conveyance even without impoundment structures

Section 07

Pervious Concrete

Pervious (porous) concrete is a permeable pavement technology that allows stormwater to pass through the pavement surface and into a subsurface reservoir, where it infiltrates into the native soil. It is commonly used in parking areas and low-traffic roadways as an alternative to conventional impervious surfaces.

📷 Figure 7.1 — Pervious Concrete vs. Conventional Asphalt Photograph of a parking area showing a clear visual comparison between standard asphalt paving (left, with visible surface runoff pooling) and pervious concrete (right, with water visibly infiltrating through the surface). Both sections labeled in original image.
Figure 7.1. Side-by-side comparison of conventional asphalt paving and pervious concrete. (Source: Harper, 2026)

Layer Composition

Pervious concrete pavement systems consist of multiple engineered layers designed to manage infiltration, provide structural support, and store treatment volume:

📐 Figure 7.2 — Pervious Concrete Layer Diagram Detailed cross-section schematic showing four distinct horizontal layers from top to bottom: (1) Surface Course — diagonal hash pattern; (2) Filter Course — fine aggregate shown as small dots; (3) Reservoir Base Course — large aggregate shown as rounded stones; (4) Existing Soil — brick/hatched pattern. Each layer is labeled with bracketed annotations on the right side showing name and specifications.
Figure 7.2. Pervious concrete pavement layer composition schematic. (Source: Harper, 2026)
Surface Course
1/2" to 3/4" aggregate mix · 2.5 to 4" thickness typical. Open-graded mix providing high permeability while maintaining structural load capacity.
Filter Course
1/2" aggregate, 2" thick. Prevents migration of surface course aggregate into the reservoir base while allowing free drainage.
Reservoir Base Course
1" to 2" aggregate. Voids volume is designed for the required retention volume. Thickness is determined based on storage volume required to achieve the desired treatment efficiency.
Existing Soil
Minimal compaction to retain porosity and permeability. Native soil infiltration rate governs the long-term drawdown performance of the system.
Section 08

Additional Retention Options

Beyond conventional retention ponds and swales, a range of distributed retention practices can be applied at the parcel or site level to reduce delivered runoff volumes. While these options are generally applicable to smaller areas, their widespread adoption can meaningfully reduce total runoff loads delivered to receiving waters.

📷 Figure 8.1 — Retention Options Photo Array Five photographs arranged in a grid showing: (1) Permeable Pavers — interlocking brick pavers in a parking area with natural mulch border; (2) Permeable Concrete/Asphalt — close-up of water being discharged onto pervious asphalt, showing rapid infiltration; (3) Permeable Planters — raised planter with trees and shrubs adjacent to an urban walkway; (4) Grassed Parking Areas — turf grid system used in overflow parking area; (5) Rain Gardens — small residential bioretention area edged with river rock and planted with native wildflowers.
Figure 8.1. Distributed retention options applicable at parcel and site scales. (Source: Harper, 2026)

Distributed Retention Practice Types

  • Permeable Pavers: Interlocking pavers with aggregate-filled joints allowing infiltration. Suitable for driveways, walkways, and parking.
  • Permeable Concrete/Asphalt: Open-graded mix allowing water passage through pavement matrix. See Section 7 for detailed layer composition.
  • Permeable Planters: Raised or in-ground planting areas designed to capture and infiltrate runoff from adjacent impervious surfaces.
  • Grassed Parking Areas: Turf reinforcement grids or grass block pavers for low-frequency or overflow parking areas.
  • Rain Gardens: Shallow vegetated depressions designed to capture and infiltrate runoff from roofs, driveways, and streets.
Application Note Most of these options are designed for small areas and applied on a parcel basis. While they cannot substitute for required treatment facilities, they can further reduce the delivered runoff volume and assist in meeting water quality objectives for the overall project.
Section 09

Retention Modeling Methods

An evaluation of the efficiency of dry retention practices was conducted by Harper and Baker (2007) for the Florida Department of Environmental Protection (FDEP). This foundational analysis underpins the performance-based design approach incorporated in both the BMPFast model and Florida's 2024 Stormwater Rule.

Modeling Framework

The study was based on a continuous simulation of runoff from a hypothetical 1-acre site using the SCS curve number methodology. The comprehensive parametric analysis covered a wide range of site conditions representative of Florida development scenarios.

Analysis Parameters

DCIA Range: Directly Connected Impervious Area percentages from 0% to 100% in 10-unit intervals
Non-DCIA Curve Numbers: Values from 30 to 90 in 10-unit intervals, representing the range of pervious area conditions from open sandy soils to heavily urbanized pervious surfaces
Rainfall Data: Runoff calculated for continuous historical rainfall data sets for each of the 45 hourly Florida meteorological sites. Generally, 30–50 years of data per site were available, providing statistically robust results across Florida's diverse rainfall climatology.
Section 10

Efficiency Modeling Assumptions

The retention efficiency model relies on several key assumptions that define how pollutant removal is related to runoff volume retention. These assumptions are well-supported by available research and provide a defensible basis for regulatory application.

Fundamental Efficiency Relationship

The analysis assumes that the efficiency of an infiltration/retention practice is directly related to the portion of the annual runoff volume retained. For example, if 50% of the annual runoff volume is retained, the removal efficiency for Total Nitrogen (TN) and Total Phosphorus (TP) is also 50%. This relationship rests on two supporting assumptions:

  • Assumes no change in runoff concentrations during the individual storm event (concentrations are uniform, regardless of timing within event)
  • Ignores groundwater loading contributions

Continuous Simulation Approach

Performance efficiency was calculated using a continuous simulation of runoff inputs into a theoretical dry retention pond, based on individual storm events over the entire available rainfall record for all 45 hourly meteorological stations. Treatment logic applied after runoff enters the pond:

Events where runoff volume ≤ treatment volume: A removal efficiency of 100% is assumed — the entire event volume is captured and retained.
Events where runoff volume > treatment volume:
  • 100% removal for inputs up to the treatment volume
  • 0% removal for inputs in excess of the treatment volume — excess water bypasses the pond without treatment

Drawdown Assumptions

A hypothetical drawdown curve is used to evacuate water from the pond, calibrated to common Water Management District drawdown requirements:

  • Recovery of 50% of treatment volume in 24 hours
  • Recovery of 100% of treatment volume in 72 hours

First Flush Assumption

The model assumes no significant "first flush" effect from the watershed. While small watersheds (less than 5–10 acres) may exhibit first-flush behavior for certain rain events, there is no evidence that larger watersheds exhibit first-flush effects on a continuous basis. No consistent research supports this concept as a basis for design at typical project scales.

Efficiency Calculation and Data Aggregation

  • Pond efficiency is equal to the fraction of annual runoff volume infiltrated
  • Separate model runs were conducted for the entire period of rainfall record at each of the 45 hourly meteorological sites
  • Data were summarized by meteorological zone for use in design applications

Effectiveness of On-Line Retention

The modeled removal efficiency for a 0.50-inch on-line retention system varies substantially based on the site's DCIA percentage and the non-DCIA curve number. Understanding these relationships allows engineers to anticipate performance under different site conditions.

📈 Figure 12.1 — Effectiveness of On-Line Retention (0.50 Inch over watershed) Line graph with x-axis: Non-DCIA Curve Number (20–100); y-axis: Percent Removal (50–100%). Nine curves plotted for DCIA values of 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80, and 90. All curves show declining efficiency as the non-DCIA CN increases. Higher DCIA values produce lower removal efficiencies at all CN values. At DCIA=10, removal ranges from ~99% (CN=25) to ~61% (CN=98). At DCIA=90, removal ranges from ~83% (CN=25) to ~57% (CN=98). Curves converge at high CN values.
Figure 12.1. Removal efficiency of 0.50-inch on-line retention system as a function of non-DCIA Curve Number and DCIA percentage. (Source: Harper & Baker, 2007)
Efficiency Decreases As:
  • DCIA increases (more impervious area generates greater runoff volume)
  • Non-DCIA CN value increases (greater runoff from pervious areas)
Efficiency Increases As:
  • DCIA decreases (less impervious area relative to treatment volume)
  • Non-DCIA CN value decreases (more permeable pervious areas)
Section 13

Impacts of Accelerated Drawdown on Retention Efficiencies

A key question in retention system design is whether the rate of drawdown (treatment volume recovery) significantly affects overall annual pollutant removal efficiency. The modeling results demonstrate that this effect is relatively small.

Comparison: 72-Hour vs. 36-Hour Drawdown

📈 Figure 13.1 — Change in Retention Efficiency: 72-Hour vs. 36-Hour Drawdown Line graph with x-axis: Treatment Depth (inches), 0–4"; y-axis: Percent Change in Treatment (%), 0–4%. Six curves for Zone 1, Zone 2, Zone 3, Zone 4, Zone 5, and Statewide. All curves show a peak change at approximately 0.25" treatment depth (2.8–3.7%) that rapidly decreases to near zero by 2.0" treatment depth. Indicates that halving the drawdown time (72 to 36 hours) produces only minor efficiency improvements, particularly for larger retention volumes.
Figure 13.1. Change in mean annual retention efficiency from doubling the drawdown rate (72-hour to 36-hour recovery), by meteorological zone and statewide average. (Source: Harper & Baker, 2007)
Finding Increasing the rate of drawdown has minimal impact on treatment efficiency. Doubling the rate of recovery from 72 to 36 hours increases the retention efficiency by only 3–4% — and only for shallow retention depths. For retention depths greater than 2 inches, the improvement is less than 1%.

Comparison: Multiple Drawdown Rates vs. Standard 3-Day Drawdown

📈 Figure 13.2 — Percent Change in Retention Efficiency vs. Standard 3-Day Drawdown (Cross City Station) Line graph with x-axis: Treatment Depth (inches), 0–4"; y-axis: Percent Change in Treatment (%), –10 to +4%. Three curves plotted: Difference Between 1.5 Day and 3 Day (red, positive values 0–3%); Difference Between 7 Day and 3 Day (blue, negative values –4.5 to –1%); Difference Between 14 Day and 3 Day (green, negative values –8.5 to –2%). Demonstrates asymmetry: faster drawdown yields small positive gains while slower drawdown yields larger negative impacts on efficiency.
Figure 13.2. Effect of varying drawdown times relative to standard 3-day drawdown on mean dry retention treatment efficiency, Cross City meteorological station. (Source: Harper & Baker, 2007)
Key Asymmetry Finding Increasing the rate of drawdown has minimal impact on treatment efficiency. However, decreasing the rate of drawdown has a substantially larger negative impact on efficiency than the positive impact from increasing the rate. Extending recovery from 3 days to 14 days can reduce efficiency by up to 8–9% for shallow retention depths. This asymmetry has important implications for projects where site soils have lower infiltration rates.
Section 14

Regional Variability in Treatment Efficiency of Dry Retention

The performance efficiency of retention systems is not uniform across Florida. Differences in rainfall patterns — particularly event frequency, intensity, and seasonal distribution — result in significant variability in how well a given retention depth will perform in different parts of the state.

  • Performance efficiency of retention systems varies throughout the state due to differences in meteorological characteristics between zones
  • Design criteria based on treatment of 0.5 inch of runoff provide better annual mass removal than treatment of 1 inch of rainfall across all five meteorological zones
Conclusion Design criteria based on retention of 0.5 inch of runoff or runoff from 1-inch of rainfall both fail to meet the 80% treatment objective for typical Florida development conditions. Performance-based design using the meteorological zone-specific efficiency tables is required to consistently achieve treatment objectives.

Section 16

BMPFast Retention Efficiency Calculations

Calculation of runoff in the BMPFast model uses the tabular retention efficiency relationships developed by Harper and Baker (2007), Appendix D. The model interpolates within and between tables to calculate the precise efficiency for any combination of input parameters.

Required Input Data

  • Rainfall meteorological zone: Determined from the Florida rainfall zone map based on project location (Zones 1–5)
  • Annual rainfall depth: Obtained from annual rainfall isopleth maps for the project area
  • Project DCIA: Directly Connected Impervious Area percentage for the project catchment
  • Non-DCIA curve number: SCS curve number for the pervious portions of the catchment
  • Retention provided or desired performance efficiency: Either the depth of retention system provided (to calculate resulting efficiency) or the target efficiency (to calculate required retention depth)
Computational Method BMPFast conducts iterations within and between the 80 efficiency tables to calculate the appropriate efficiency or required retention depth for the specific combination of zone, rainfall, DCIA, and curve number. This automated interpolation procedure eliminates the need for manual table lookup and significantly reduces calculation time for complex projects.
Section 17

Summary

Key Takeaways — Retention Systems

  • Efficiencies of retention systems vary significantly throughout the State due to variability in meteorological characteristics between the five Florida rainfall zones.
  • Changes in recovery time (drawdown rate) have little impact on retention system performance — a finding that simplifies design by reducing the sensitivity of retention performance to soil infiltration rate variability.
  • Design criteria based on fixed retention depths (0.5" runoff or 1" rainfall) fail to consistently achieve the 80% annual mass removal treatment objective across Florida's diverse rainfall regions.
  • Performance-based retention design using zone-specific efficiency tables provides a technically defensible approach that accounts for regional rainfall variability.
  • The BMPFast model automates the iterative table interpolation process, enabling efficient performance-based retention design calculations for any Florida project location.
