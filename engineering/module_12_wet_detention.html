Section 1

Definitions

Stormwater best management practices (BMPs) are broadly classified into two groups based on how the treatment volume is managed: retention systems and detention systems. Understanding this fundamental distinction is essential to selecting an appropriate BMP for a given site and regulatory context.

Retention

A group of stormwater practices where the treatment volume is evacuated by either percolation into groundwater or evaporation. There is no surface discharge for the treatment volume, resulting in a substantial reduction in overall runoff volume.

Retention practices include:

  • Dry retention
  • Harvesting (reuse irrigation)
  • Underground storage systems
Detention

A group of stormwater practices where the treatment volume is detained for a period of time before release. The treatment volume is discharged over a period of days; there is no significant reduction in runoff volume.

Detention practices include:

  • Wet detention
  • Dry detention
Section 2

Wet Detention Ponds

Wet detention ponds are essentially man-made lakes. They are governed by the same physical, biological, and chemical processes that regulate natural lakes, making their performance highly predictable. Wet detention ponds can be designed as attractive amenities and are frequently integrated into the overall site development plan as landscaping features and recreational assets.

🏞
Figure 1 — Wet Detention Pond Examples
Left: Wet detention pond constructed as a development amenity with decorative fountain. Right: Aerial view of wet detention lakes integrated into a residential development plan.

Key Characteristics

  • Essentially man-made lakes in function and behavior
  • Subject to all physical, biological, and chemical processes operating in natural surface waters
  • Highly predictable pollutant removal performance
  • Pollutant removal processes occur within the permanent pool volume below the control elevation
  • Removal efficiency is a function of residence time

Section 3

Wet Detention Pollutant Removal Processes

Pollutant removal in wet detention ponds is accomplished through a combination of physical, biological, and chemical mechanisms. Most removal activity occurs during the quiescent period between storm events, making the permanent pool a critical design element.

Physical Processes

  • Gravity settling — primary physical process; efficiency depends on pond geometry, volume, residence time, and particle size
  • Adsorption onto solid surfaces

Biological Processes

  • Uptake by algae and aquatic plants
  • Metabolization by microorganisms

Chemical Processes

  • Co-precipitation by metal oxides

Role of the Permanent Pool

  • Reduces incoming flow energy and promotes settling
  • Provides habitat for aquatic plants and microorganisms
  • Biological processes occur primarily during quiescent periods between storms
Section 4

Detention Time

The performance efficiency of a wet detention pond is fundamentally a function of detention time — the average time water remains in the permanent pool before being discharged. Longer detention times allow more complete pollutant removal through all operative processes.

Detention Time Formula
td (days) = (PPV / RO) × (365 days / year)

PPV = permanent pool volume below control elevation (ac-ft)

RO = annual runoff inputs (ac-ft/yr)

td = mean annual detention time (days)

Section 5

TSS Removal as a Function of Detention Time

Total suspended solids (TSS) removal in wet ponds exhibits a characteristic two-phase pattern as detention time increases. Initial rapid settling of larger, heavier particles occurs at short detention times, followed by the slower removal of fine particles as detention time extends. Field data confirm that substantial TSS removal (approaching 80–85%) can be achieved at detention times greater than approximately 10–15 days, with diminishing marginal returns at longer detention times.

📈
Figure 3 — TSS Removal vs. Detention Time
Scatter plot with fitted curve showing TSS removal efficiency (%) on the y-axis (0–100%) versus detention time in days (0–50 days) on the x-axis. The curve rises steeply for the first 10 days (initial rapid settling of larger particles), then flattens asymptotically toward approximately 85% (slower settling of fine particles).
Section 6

Phosphorus Removal for Untreated Runoff in Wet Ponds

Phosphorus removal in wet detention ponds is highly predictable and has been documented in research studies with detention times ranging up to 450 days. All study ponds included littoral zones; therefore, littoral zone contributions are embedded in the calculated removal efficiencies. The following regression equation describes TP removal as a function of mean annual detention time:

Total Phosphorus Removal — Regression Equation
% Removal = 40.15 + 6.366 · ln(td) + 0.214 · [ln(td)]²

R² = 0.894

td = mean annual detention time (days)

Regulatory Note: The June 2024 Florida Stormwater Rule limits the maximum allowable detention time used in the removal efficiency calculation to 200 days. Pond designs proposing longer detention times may not claim removal credit beyond the 200-day performance level.
📈
Figure 4 — Total Phosphorus Removal vs. Detention Time
Scatter plot with fitted logarithmic curve showing TP removal efficiency (%) versus detention time td (days, 0–500). Data points from multiple Florida studies are fitted with the quadratic-log regression equation (R²=0.894). A vertical dashed line at 200 days marks the regulatory maximum detention time for credit under the new rule.
Section 7

Nitrogen Removal for Untreated Runoff in Wet Ponds

Nitrogen removal in wet ponds is more variable than phosphorus removal and depends significantly on the forms of nitrogen present in the runoff. Inorganic nitrogen is removed primarily through biological uptake by algae and aquatic plants. Particulate nitrogen is readily removed by sedimentation. Organic nitrogen, however, is not easily removed, which limits the maximum achievable total nitrogen removal efficiency.

Total Nitrogen Removal — Regression Equation
Efficiency (%) = (44.72 · td) / (5.46 + td)

R² = 0.808

td = mean annual detention time (days)

Regulatory Note: The new rule also limits the maximum allowable detention time for TN removal credit to 200 days.
📈
Figure 5 — Total Nitrogen Removal vs. Detention Time
Scatter plot with fitted hyperbolic curve showing TN removal efficiency (%) versus detention time td (days, 0–450). The data exhibit greater scatter than TP (R²=0.808), reflecting variability in nitrogen forms. The curve plateaus near 40–45% efficiency, substantially lower than the TP asymptote. A vertical dashed line marks the 200-day regulatory limit.
Section 8

Nutrient Removal Relationships for Wet Ponds

Nutrient removal in wet ponds is primarily a function of detention time. The removal curves for both TN and TP can be decomposed into two components: rapid initial removal of particulate species (occurring within the first days of detention) and slower, continuing removal of dissolved species.

📊
Figure 6 — TN and TP Nutrient Removal Relationships
Side-by-side scatter plots for Total Nitrogen (left) and Total Phosphorus (right), both showing removal efficiency (%) versus detention time td (days). Annotations identify the early steeper region as "removal of particulates" and the later gradual region as "removal of dissolved species."
Important limitations of these relationships:
  • These relationships were developed for untreated runoff only and do not apply when runoff receives pre-treatment prior to entering the wet pond.
  • Removal of dissolved pollutants is a function of water column concentration — removal rates decrease as concentration decreases.
  • Removal effectively stops when the irreducible concentration is reached (see Section 19).
Section 9

Factors Negatively Impacting Efficiencies of Wet Ponds

Several operational and ecological conditions can substantially degrade the pollutant removal performance of wet detention ponds:

  • Waterfowl loadings — Large populations of waterfowl (particularly wading birds and ducks) introduce substantial nutrient and pathogen loadings from fecal matter, offsetting pollutant removal.
  • Managing ponds as amenities — Fertilizing adjacent turf areas, applying herbicides, and aggressively maintaining ornamental aesthetics can introduce nutrients and disrupt the biological communities responsible for pollutant removal.
  • Cattail invasion — Dense stands of Typha spp. (cattails) can impede water circulation, create short-circuiting flow paths, and reduce effective pond volume.
  • Use of copper sulfate and herbicides for algae control — These treatments kill the algal and aquatic plant communities that are responsible for biological nutrient uptake, potentially eliminating much of the pond's biological removal capacity.

Section 11

Zonation in a Typical Lake

Wet detention ponds share the same physical and biological zonation structure as natural lakes. Understanding this zonation is important for assessing the potential for anoxic conditions and associated internal nutrient loading.

🏊
Figure 8 — Lake Zonation Diagram
Cross-sectional diagram of a lake illustrating: littoral zones at both margins; pelagic zone (open water) in the center; mixed layer; photic zone (epilimnion) where photosynthesis exceeds respiration; aphotic zone (hypolimnion) where respiration exceeds photosynthesis; and the light compensation depth at approximately 1% of surface irradiance.

Deep areas of natural lakes that become anoxic can release ammonia, phosphorus, and gases from organic-rich sediments that have accumulated over centuries (internal nutrient loading). However, wet detention ponds do not develop the muck accumulations or other conditions responsible for significant internal nutrient release, because they lack the centuries-long history of organic sediment accumulation found in natural lakes.

Despite the lack of documented evidence for internal loading in wet ponds, the Water Management Districts, in an abundance of caution, have elected to limit the portion of a wet detention pond's volume that can be credited toward detention time calculations to the volume above the hypothetical anoxic zone. ERD developed a methodology to estimate this anoxic zone depth from water quality parameters.

Stoneybrook Development Monitoring — Field Evidence for Wet Pond Performance

During the period August 2001 through December 2007, ERD conducted water quality monitoring at a series of deep wet detention ponds associated with the Stoneybrook residential development (27 storm events, n=27). Pond depths ranged from 20 to 25 feet. Water quality was measured at pond outfalls continuously for 72 hours following rain events of 0.5 inch or greater.

Key findings from the Stoneybrook monitoring program:

  • No significant change in outfall pollutant concentrations over the 72-hour post-storm monitoring period for any parameter.
  • Monitoring conducted by ERD has never detected evidence of significant nutrient release from bottom sediments in a wet detention pond.
  • Despite the lack of empirical evidence, WMDs require that the effective pond volume be limited to the zone above the hypothetical anoxic depth as a conservative design measure.
  • ERD developed a methodology for estimating anoxic zone depth based on algal productivity and water quality parameters.

Relationships Between Lake Trophic State Parameters

Phosphorus regulates the growth of algae in most freshwater lakes in Florida. Using Lake Watch data compiled from more than 1,000 lakes across Florida, ERD developed regression relationships between key trophic state parameters expressed as mean annual values:

Relationship 1: Total Phosphorus and Chlorophyll-a

TP — Chlorophyll-a Relationship (Florida Lakes)
ln(chyl-a) = 1.058 · ln(TP) − 0.934

chyl-a = chlorophyll-a concentration (mg/m³)

TP = total phosphorus concentration (μg/L)

Relationship 2: Chlorophyll-a and Secchi Disk Depth

Chlorophyll-a — Secchi Disk Depth Relationship (Florida Lakes)
SD = [24.2386 + (0.3041 × chyl-a)] / (6.0632 + chyl-a)

SD = Secchi disk depth (m)

chyl-a = chlorophyll-a concentration (mg/m³)

Section 14

Secchi Disk Depth

The Secchi disk depth is a standard measure of water transparency (clarity) in lakes, ponds, and other waterbodies. The technique was developed by Father Pietro Secchi (1818–1878), scientific advisor to the Pope, who first employed the method to measure water clarity in the Mediterranean Sea.

Measurement Protocol

  1. Lower a standard 20 cm diameter disk (divided into alternating black and white quadrants) into the water on a calibrated line.
  2. Continue lowering until the disk is no longer visible.
  3. Slowly raise the disk until it becomes visible again.
  4. The Secchi disk depth is the average of the disappearance depth and the reappearance depth.

Section 15

Estimation of Anoxic Depth

The depth at which anoxic conditions (dissolved oxygen <1 mg/L) develop in a lake or wet detention pond is a function of water quality characteristics. Because anoxia is driven by the depletion of oxygen in the water column below the photic zone, factors controlling light penetration should exhibit a predictable relationship with anoxic depth.

ERD developed a dataset from geomean water quality data obtained from more than 100 Florida lakes to evaluate these relationships. Collected parameters included:

  • Chlorophyll-a — measure of algal biomass; excess algae shades deeper water, reducing the photic zone
  • Secchi disk depth — measure of light penetration depth
  • Total phosphorus — in most stormwater ponds and lakes, algal productivity is phosphorus-limited; TP controls the algal standing crop
  • Anoxic depth — the depth at which dissolved oxygen concentration decreases to <1 mg/L

A multiple regression analysis was conducted to derive a predictive relationship for anoxic depth from the measured water quality variables.

Calculation of Anoxic Depth

🏊
Figure 12 — Lake Zonation with Anoxic Zone
Cross-sectional lake diagram (same as Figure 8) with the aphotic hypolimnion highlighted in red, representing the anoxic zone. The anoxic depth is measured from the surface to the oxic/anoxic transition.
Anoxic Depth Regression Equation
AD (m) = 3.035 × Secchi (m) − 0.004979 × Total P (mg/L) + 0.02164 × chyl-a (mg/m³)

R² = 0.951

AD = anoxic depth (m)

Valid ranges for the equation:

  • 0.25 m < anoxic depth < 9.0 m
  • 0.09 m < Secchi disk depth < 3.49 m
  • 0.001 mg/L < Total P < 0.498 mg/L
  • 1 mg/m³ < chlorophyll-a < 332 mg/m³

This example evaluates wet detention system design for a hypothetical 100-acre mixed residential/commercial development with 35% impervious cover, assessed at three Florida locations: Pensacola, Orlando, and Key West. The target performance standard is 80% TP removal.

Calculate Required Pond Detention Time (Td)

Using the TP removal equation and solving iteratively for 80% removal:

Eff = 40.13 + 6.372 · ln(td) + 0.213 · [ln(td)]²

By iteration: Td ≈ 200 days → 79.9% TP removal

Anticipated TN removal for a 200-day detention time:

Eff = (44.72 × 200) / (5.46 + 200) = 42.6% TN removal

Calculate Required Permanent Pool Volume (PPV)

Using the detention time formula: PPV = RO × Td × (1 year / 365 days)

Step 4

Calculate Mean Annual Pond TP Concentration (Pensacola Example)

Annual mass of TP discharged from pond at 79.9% removal:

TPdischarge = 55.4 kg/yr × (1 − 0.799) = 11.1 kg/yr

Assuming inflow volume equals outflow volume (149.3 ac-ft/yr), the mean discharge concentration is:

C = (11.1 kg/yr) / (149.3 ac-ft/yr) × unit conversions = 60 μg TP/L

Unit conversion chain: kg/yr ÷ ac-ft/yr × (1 ac/43,560 ft²) × (1 ft³/7.48 gal) × (1 gal/3.785 L) × (10⁶ mg/kg)

Step 5

Calculate Mean Annual Pond Chlorophyll-a Concentration

ln(chyl-a) = 1.058 · ln(60) − 0.934
chyl-a = e2.94 = 40.4 mg/m³
Step 6

Calculate Mean Annual Pond Secchi Disk Depth

SD = [24.2386 + (0.3041 × 40.4)] / (6.0632 + 40.4)
SD = 0.79 m = 2.6 ft
Step 7

Calculate Mean Annual Depth of Anoxic Conditions

Anoxic Depth Equation
AD = 3.035 × Secchi + 0.02164 × chyl-a − 0.004979 × Total P

AD = anoxic depth (m) | Secchi = depth (m) | chyl-a = mg/m³ | Total P = μg/L

Step 8

Calculate Mean Annual and Minimum Monthly Anoxic Depth

AD = 3.035(0.79) + 0.02164(40.4) − 0.004979(60)
Mean Annual AD = 2.97 m = 9.8 ft

Converting to minimum monthly design value:

Min. Monthly AD = 0.892 × 2.97 m = 2.65 m = 8.7 ft

The design pond depth below the control elevation should not exceed the minimum monthly anoxic depth. In this example, only the top 8.7 ft of the water column may be credited as aerobic, biologically active volume for detention time calculations.

Section 19

Concept of Irreducible Concentration

Irreducible concentrations reflect fundamental limitations on pollutant removal pathways within a given treatment system. In wet detention ponds, the most significant removal processes are sedimentation and biological uptake. As concentrations in the water column decrease, removal rates slow because the concentration gradient driving uptake diminishes.

When the irreducible concentration is reached, no significant additional removal is possible regardless of how much additional treatment volume or time is provided. This concept is widely used in modeling the performance of wastewater treatment wetlands and is equally applicable to stormwater wet ponds.

Parameter Units Assumed Minimum Irreducible Concentration
Total Nitrogenμg/L400
Total Phosphorusμg/L10
Implication for design: If the incoming runoff concentration is already at or near the irreducible concentration, additional detention volume will not provide meaningful pollutant reduction. This underscores why the nutrient removal equations are valid only for untreated runoff entering directly into a wet pond.
Section 20

Comparison of 14-Day Wet Season with Mean Annual Detention Time

Several Water Management Districts base wet detention pond design on a 14-day wet season detention time criterion. The BMPFast methodology, by contrast, uses a mean annual detention time, which integrates performance across all seasons.

Because Florida receives significant rainfall throughout the year (not only during the summer wet season), a 14-day wet season detention time translates into a longer mean annual detention time. The table below shows the equivalent mean annual detention times corresponding to a 14-day wet season design across Florida's five meteorological zones:

Meteorological Zone Equivalent Mean Annual Detention Time (days)
Zone 1 — Panhandle17.1
Zone 2 — Central19.9
Zone 3 — Keys21.8
Zone 4 — West Coastal20.2
Zone 5 — Southeast21.0

Section 21

Impacts of Color on Wet Pond Effectiveness

Sources and Nature of Color

Water color in Florida stormwater is caused primarily by dissolved organic molecules — specifically tannins and lignins — produced by the decomposition of leaves, roots, and plant litter. Wetlands commonly discharge highly colored water into receiving ponds. Color is measured in Platinum Cobalt Units (PCU) or CPU.

🌊
Figure 15 — Water Color Caused by Dissolved Tannins
Photograph of a water body exhibiting intense brown-red coloration from dissolved tannin compounds derived from decomposing organic matter.

Impacts of Color on Pond Performance

The presence of elevated color in a wet detention pond's water column has multiple interacting effects that substantially reduce pollutant removal performance:

  • Reduced light penetration — color absorbs sunlight and reduces the depth of the photic zone, limiting algal and aquatic plant growth.
  • Reduced pH — colored water often has pH values below 5, which limits the species of algae and aquatic plants capable of growing in the pond.
  • Natural algaecide effects — some color compounds act as natural inhibitors of algal growth.
  • Nutrient sequestration in organic molecules — nutrients in colored water are more likely to be bound into dissolved organic molecules, making them unavailable for algal uptake and biological removal.
  • Substantially reduced effectiveness — in highly colored ponds, performance is limited primarily to removal of particulate matter through sedimentation; biological nutrient removal is negligible.
Design implication: Sites draining through or adjacent to wetlands may experience chronically elevated water color in the receiving wet pond. In these cases, the standard nutrient removal relationships (based on detention time) may substantially overestimate actual performance. Site-specific color data should be evaluated during system design.
