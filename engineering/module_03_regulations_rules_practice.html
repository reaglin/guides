Overview

Navigating the evolving landscape of stormwater management regulations does not have to slow a project down. This module walks through the latest rule changes, proven analytical tools, and practical strategies that help permit applicants and project managers achieve fast, compliant regulatory approval in the state of Florida.

Florida's New Stormwater Rule, effective June 28, 2024, with full implementation required by December 28, 2025, introduced sweeping updates to how Best Management Practices (BMPs) are analyzed. Practitioners who understand these changes — and who have the right tools — are positioned for streamlined permit approvals. Those relying on outdated methods risk delays, reviewer pushback, and resubmission cycles.

Learning Objectives
After completing this module, you will be able to:
  1. Explain how new rules, regulations, and practice affect stormwater management water quality permit applications — and why these requirements change over the years.
  2. Identify the tools and resources available to assist in obtaining a permit in a timely manner while remaining consistent with new regulations and practice — understanding what is in the stormwater professional's toolbox.

Context

Where Are These Improvements Coming From?

The improvements reshaping stormwater permitting in Florida emerge from multiple converging sources. Years of dedicated research and field testing have produced breakthroughs in how BMPs are understood and analyzed. These advances are grounded in real-world Florida data and validated through extensive monitoring programs across the state's unique climate and soil conditions.

Research & Testing New Stormwater Rule (June 28, 2024) Environmental Stewardship Groups Municipal Representatives Reviewers & Consultants Applicant's Handbooks (A.H.) Software Security (FS 288.860)

Research and Testing

Breakthrough discoveries from university and agency research programs have fundamentally reshaped how BMP effectiveness is calculated. New data on EMCs, vault performance, detention options, permeable pavements, and harvesting retention provide the scientific foundation for the updated regulations. Examples of newly incorporated technologies include underground vaults, expanded detention options, new permeable pavement types, stormwater harvesting systems, and enhanced retention analysis methods.

Critically, software used to apply these improvements must be approved by at least one regulatory agency before it can be used in permit applications.

The New Stormwater Rule (June 28, 2024)

The new stormwater rule specifies how projects must be analyzed. It sets the analytical framework and introduces updated performance standards, revised EMC values, new annual rainfall data, and expanded BMP options. Full implementation was required by December 28, 2025.

Stakeholder Collaboration

Environmental stewardship groups, municipal representatives, permit reviewers, consultants, and the Applicant's Handbooks all contribute to the evolving regulatory landscape. This collaborative approach ensures that rules reflect both environmental protection goals and practical implementation realities.

Regulatory Requirement

Security of Software

FS 288.860 — Foreign Control of Intellectual Property

Florida Statute 288.860 addresses the issue of foreign control of intellectual property. The 2021 Florida Legislature enacted this statute, with implementing guidance issued in December 2022. New requirements related to artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) programming add another layer of compliance that stormwater analysis software must meet in order to be approved for use in regulatory permitting.

Practical Implication
Any software used to support a Florida stormwater permit application must comply with FS 288.860 restrictions on foreign control of the underlying intellectual property. Practitioners should verify that their analysis tools meet current security requirements before submitting applications.
Rationale

Need for More Tools in the Toolbox — Why?

The stormwater management profession is at a critical inflection point. With the 2024 rule introducing sweeping changes to BMP effectiveness analysis, practitioners need modern tools that can keep pace with regulatory evolution. Relying on outdated methods or software that has not been updated to reflect current standards is a recipe for permit delays, inconsistent reviews, and missed opportunities for better environmental outcomes.

  • 01
    Incorporate Recent Improvements. New rules, regulations, and practices demand tools that take less time to complete an analysis of BMP effectiveness. Traditional manual calculations and outdated spreadsheets cannot accommodate the volume and complexity of changes introduced in the 2024 rule. Modern tools must integrate these updates seamlessly so that applicants can produce compliant analyses quickly and accurately.
  • 02
    Consistency in ERP Assessment. There is a pressing need for consistency in the assessment of Environmental Resource Permits (ERPs). When different applicants use different methods or outdated data, reviewers spend excessive time reconciling discrepancies. Results should also support other regulatory programs including TMDL, BMAP, flood control, and consumptive or water use permits — creating a unified analytical framework.
  • 03
    Florida-Specific Data Required. Analysis must use data from Florida, not data developed for a different climate or enforcement program. In Florida, BMP effectiveness is evaluated on an annual basis — a critical distinction from other states. Using out-of-state models or generic national datasets can lead to inaccurate effectiveness calculations that reviewers will flag, causing significant delays.
  • 04
    Professional Discovery and Better Design. Professional discovery and a deeper understanding of hydrologic events leads to better design outcomes. When practitioners have tools that allow them to explore different scenarios — varying curve numbers, recovery rates, rainfall zones, and BMP configurations — they can optimize designs that not only meet regulatory requirements but also deliver superior environmental performance.

New Rule Detail

Specified Performance Standards from the New Rule

Source: A.H. Vol 1, Sec. 8.3, June 28, 2024

One of the most significant changes in the June 28, 2024 stormwater rule is the introduction of specified performance standards. These standards establish clear benchmarks for pollutant removal that every BMP system must meet, representing years of research into actual water quality impacts. They provide a consistent, science-based framework for evaluating stormwater treatment effectiveness across all Florida water management districts.

Critical Note
Most existing software does not include the new performance standards, which sometimes results in delays in obtaining a permit. The solution is to "hard-wire" the standards into compliant software. Hard-wired numbers are generally not questioned during review; User Defined values require justification and typically add weeks to the process.

When performance standards are "hard-wired" into analysis software, both applicants and reviewers can have confidence that the calculations meet current regulatory requirements without the need for lengthy justification. Additional standards can be added to address local needs and sensitivity analysis. The framework attempts to maintain past Worksheet formats for continuity.

New Rule Detail

Annual Rainfall Data — Updated by the New Rule

Source: A.H. Vol 1, June 28, 2024, Appendix M Figure 2

The new stormwater rule brings a critical update to the annual rainfall data used in BMP effectiveness calculations. For decades, practitioners relied on rainfall isopluvial maps from the year 2000. The 2024 rule introduces updated annual rainfall depths derived from current NOAA data, reflecting more than two decades of additional precipitation records. These changes directly affect runoff volume calculations, BMP sizing, and ultimately the pollutant removal effectiveness that determines whether a project meets performance standards.

Key Takeaway
The updated annual rainfall data shows the need for site-specific evaluations. Modern analysis software should automatically select the appropriate rainfall depth based on the project's geographic location within Florida. A project in Orlando may face very different rainfall conditions than one in the Florida Keys or the Panhandle.

Catchment Data

Event Mean Concentrations (EMCs)

Source: A.H. Vol 1, Table 9.2, June 28, 2024

EMCs are the foundational water quality data that drive pollutant loading calculations in every stormwater permit application. The 2024 rule updated the EMC values in Applicant's Handbook Volume 1, Table 9.2, reflecting more current monitoring data from Florida catchments. These values represent the average concentration of specific pollutants in stormwater runoff from different land use types and are used to calculate both pre-development and post-development pollutant loads.

If an EMC is not available in the standard "Pull-Down" list from the Applicant's Handbook, User Defined values can be entered on a site-specific basis. However, there is a significant practical problem: User Defined EMCs usually require additional time to justify to reviewers, often leading to back-and-forth correspondence and delays. In contrast, "hard-wired" numbers built into approved software and referenced directly to the A.H. are generally not questioned during review.

Pro Tip
Whenever possible, use the standard EMC values from A.H. Vol 1, Table 9.2 that are hard-wired into compliant software. User Defined EMCs, while available, typically add weeks to the review process as reviewers request justification documentation.

BMP Analysis

Wet Detention Pond Options

Source: A.H. Vol 1, Appendix O and A.H. Vol 2

Wet detention ponds remain one of the most widely used BMPs in Florida stormwater management. The 2024 rule and its associated Applicant's Handbook updates introduce important refinements to how wet detention performance is calculated, including new options for ponds without littoral zones, updated Average Residence Time (ART) limits, and revised MAP (Maximum Allowable Pollutant) loading calculations. These changes give applicants more flexibility in pond design while ensuring consistent water quality outcomes.

Maximum ART
As specified in A.H. Vol 1, Appendix O, the maximum Average Residence Time for credit purposes is 200 days. Annual residence times cannot exceed this upper limit in some applications.
Littoral Zone Requirement
A pond must include a littoral zone for full credit. If no littoral zone is present, the removal efficiency credit is reduced by 10%. When Floating Wetland Mats (MAPs) are used, the applicable improvement credit percentage applies. Note the 5% coverage requirement (12 tables in AH 1, Appendix O).

Key Parameters

  • →
    Permanent Pool Volume (acre-feet): Input required to calculate Average Residence Time.
  • →
    Floating Wetland or Mats Improvement Credit (%): Additional credit for floating treatment wetlands or vegetative mats placed within the pond.
  • →
    Input Pond TP (µg/l): If site-specific data is available, pond total phosphorus concentration can be entered for more refined calculations.

BMP Analysis

Fast Recovery Retention Systems

Exfiltration Pipes and Vaults in Limestone Areas

Source: A.H. Vol 1, Appendix O (retention) and SFWMD A.H. Vol 2

Fast recovery retention systems — including exfiltration pipes and underground vaults installed in limestone geology — represent a significant advancement in stormwater treatment technology. These systems achieve dramatically higher annual pollutant removal effectiveness compared to conventional retention systems with slower 72-hour recovery times. The faster a retention system can drain and restore its treatment capacity, the more rainfall events it can capture throughout the year, resulting in higher annual effectiveness.

In fast recovery retention systems operating in suitable limestone areas, the treatment volume can be recovered in less than 3 hours, compared to the standard 72-hour recovery assumption. This rapid turnaround means the system is ready to capture and treat the next rainfall event much sooner.

Standard Recovery (<72 Hours)
Apply the removal efficiency tables from the new rule (2024), A.H. Vol 1, Appendix O. There are 80 tables available for various combinations of CN, DCIA, zone, and design depth. Note: Challenging interpolation exists for CN and DCIA values other than increments of 5.
Fast Recovery (<3 Hours)
For fast recovery systems, adjust the standard table values using the fast recovery graph. The increase in annual effectiveness is up to 10% above the standard recovery rate values. Assumes initial abstraction of 0.1 inch.

BMP Analysis

Stormwater Harvesting Performance

REV Curves for Stormwater Harvesting

Source: A.H. Vol 1, Appendix O, Table 1

Stormwater harvesting is an increasingly important BMP strategy that simultaneously addresses water quality treatment, water supply, and flood control objectives. The performance of harvesting systems is characterized using REV curves — a powerful analytical tool that relates three key variables.

R
Rate — average daily use rate (Y-axis, in/day over EIA)
E
Efficiency — annual removal (retention) effectiveness (%)
V
Volume — storage volume available for harvesting

One REV curve exists for each meteorological zone in Florida, calculated as an average of the rainfall patterns within that zone. A critical improvement in modern analysis is the treatment of runoff volume. Older software fixed the runoff coefficient at 0.8, which resulted in conservative (understated) estimates of pollutant removal effectiveness. Updated tools allow the runoff coefficient to vary based on actual site conditions, producing more accurate and often more favorable harvesting performance calculations.

Important Improvement
When ROC = 0.80 (old fixed value), harvested efficiency may be approximately 61%. When ROC = 0.325 (site-specific, lower impervious fraction), harvested efficiency may reach 90% — a significant difference that can determine whether a project meets its performance standard.

→
Updated Software

Permit Integration

Other Permits: Consumptive Use Permits (CUP) and Harvesting

Source: Applicant's Handbook for Water Use Permit Applications — SFWMD June 13, 2022; SWFWMD (WUP) May 2014; SJRWMD Aug 2014

Stormwater harvesting systems that supply irrigation or other beneficial uses may also require Consumptive Use Permits (CUPs) — also referred to as Water Use Permits (WUPs) in some districts. Modern BMP analysis software can calculate the key parameters needed for both the stormwater quality permit and the CUP application from a single analysis, reducing duplication of effort and ensuring consistency between applications.

The analysis relates the total area contributing to the harvesting system, the total green area available for irrigation, and the weighted rational runoff coefficient, to produce estimates of average yearly demand for harvested water, average supply of harvested water, and the average supplemental (non-harvested) water needed per year.
