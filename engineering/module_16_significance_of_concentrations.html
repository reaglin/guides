Significance of Concentrations on BMP Performance

Harvey H. Harper  ·  Environmental Research & Design, Inc.  ·  University of Central Florida

Learning Objectives

  1. Understand how pollutant concentrations vary across different conveyance and treatment systems in residential runoff.
  2. Explain why first-order rate kinetics govern all biological, physical, and chemical removal processes in BMPs.
  3. Describe the species distribution of phosphorus and nitrogen and its significance for BMP selection.
  4. Identify irreducible concentrations in wet detention ponds for nitrogen and phosphorus.
  5. Evaluate manufacturer removal efficiency claims in the context of actual stormwater concentrations.
  6. Distinguish how hardwood versus herbaceous wetlands respond to stormwater nutrient inputs.
  7. Apply treatment train design principles to optimize overall system performance.

Section 1Phosphorus Concentrations in Residential Runoff

One of the foundational concepts in BMP design is understanding baseline pollutant concentrations entering the treatment system. A study of ten residential sites in Florida compared phosphorus concentrations in curb-and-gutter drainage systems versus roadside swales, examining all major phosphorus fractions.

Curb & Gutter vs. Swale: Phosphorus Species

Box plot analyses comparing Soluble Reactive Phosphorus (SRP), Dissolved Organic Phosphorus, Particulate Phosphorus, and Total Phosphorus consistently showed lower concentrations in swale runoff than in curb-and-gutter systems. This finding has direct implications for BMP sizing and selection:

📊
Figure 1
Box plots comparing four phosphorus species (SRP, Dissolved Organic P, Particulate P, Total P) between curb & gutter and swale conveyance systems. Data from n=10 residential sites in Florida. Y-axes scaled to µg/L; statistical descriptors include 10th, 25th, median, mean, 75th, and 90th percentiles plus outliers.
Key Finding

Concentrations of all phosphorus species are consistently lower in swale runoff than in curb-and-gutter systems. Swales provide effective pretreatment of stormwater runoff before it reaches a detention pond or other structural BMP.

This difference arises because swales allow infiltration, grass filtering, sedimentation, and biological uptake along the flow path — processes that are absent when runoff is collected and conveyed directly by curb and gutter systems. The implication for BMP design is significant: the choice of conveyance upstream of a treatment facility materially affects the influent concentration and therefore the required treatment volume.

Section 2Nitrogen Concentrations in Residential Runoff

The same ten-site study examined nitrogen concentrations across five species: Ammonia (NH₃), Nitrate/Nitrite (NOx), Dissolved Organic Nitrogen, Particulate Nitrogen, and Total Nitrogen.

📊
Figure 2
Box plots comparing five nitrogen species (Ammonia, NOx, Dissolved Organic N, Particulate N, Total N) between curb & gutter and swale conveyance systems. Data from n=10 Florida residential sites. Y-axes range up to approximately 250, 500, 800, 600, and 1,600 µg/L respectively.
Key Finding

Concentrations of all nitrogen species are lower in swale runoff than in curb-and-gutter systems. This result confirms that swales function as effective pretreatment for both nitrogen and phosphorus.

The pattern is consistent across all nitrogen fractions. For the inorganic species (ammonia and NOx), biological uptake by swale vegetation and microbial activity in the soil-water interface accounts for much of the reduction. For particulate and dissolved organic forms, physical filtration and settling along the swale length play a larger role.

Section 3Wet Detention Pond Discharges in Watersheds with Reuse Irrigation

A particularly important special case is the behavior of wet detention ponds in residential communities where reclaimed water (reuse) is used for landscape irrigation. Reclaimed water typically contains elevated nutrient concentrations relative to natural stormwater, and when applied to landscaping it contributes to elevated nutrient loads in the resulting runoff.

📊
Figure 3
Box plots comparing all four phosphorus species (SRP, Dissolved Organic P, Particulate P, Total P) between raw single-family residential runoff and wet detention pond discharges in watersheds with reuse irrigation systems. Y-axes in µg/L; SRP scale extends to 300 µg/L, Total P to 500 µg/L.

The data reveal a counterintuitive result: phosphorus concentrations for most parameters in pond discharges from reuse-irrigated watersheds are higher than typical raw residential runoff without reuse. This occurs because the pond is receiving nutrient-enriched runoff from reuse-irrigated landscapes, and the standard detention pond is not sized to treat these elevated loads down to pre-development levels.

Regulatory Implication

Watersheds with reuse irrigation require a substantially larger treatment volume to achieve post-development concentrations below pre-development levels. Reuse irrigation may not be compatible with the new Florida Stormwater Rule, which requires post-development pollutant loads to not exceed pre-development conditions.

Figure 4
Pie chart showing the typical distribution of phosphorus species in Florida residential stormwater runoff: SRP 43%, Dissolved Organic P 30%, Particulate P 27%.

Fate of Phosphorus in Wet Detention Ponds

The species distribution determines how much of the total phosphorus load can physically be removed by a wet detention pond:

Removal Pathway Analysis

Removable species include SRP and Particulate P — approximately 70% of total P.

SRP is removed through co-precipitation with metal oxides and biological uptake. Particulate P is removed primarily by gravity settling. Together these two fractions constitute roughly 70% of total phosphorus.

Dissolved Organic P (30%) is removed only through slow biological degradation processes, requiring very long detention times.

If 85% of the removable species are captured (a typical performance target for well-designed wet ponds), the resulting total phosphorus removal is approximately: 70% × 0.85 = 59.5% ≈ 60%. This figure aligns closely with the maximum TP removal achieved in wet ponds operating at typical hydraulic detention times.

At very long detention times (approximately 180 days), a portion of the dissolved organic P may also be biologically converted and removed, allowing somewhat higher overall removal.

Fate of Nitrogen in Wet Detention Ponds

Removal Pathway Analysis

Removable species include Ammonia, NOx, and Particulate N — approximately 48% of total N.

Dissolved Organic N (52%) is the dominant species and is only slowly removed through biological mineralization processes.

If 85% of the removable fraction is captured: 48% × 0.85 = 41%. This is consistent with maximum TN removal achieved in wet ponds under typical conditions.

At long detention times (approximately 1 year or more), a portion of dissolved organic N may be converted and removed, but even at extremely long detention times, TN removal in wet ponds cannot exceed approximately 40–50%. This is a fundamental constraint imposed by the species distribution, not by pond design.

This analysis demonstrates a critical design principle: even a theoretically perfect detention pond — one that removes all setteable and biologically reactive fractions — cannot achieve more than 50% TN removal from typical residential runoff because more than half the nitrogen is present in a dissolved organic form that is resistant to conventional removal mechanisms.

Section 6Wet Detention Ponds

Wet detention ponds are the most widely used stormwater treatment BMP in Florida. They function essentially as engineered lakes, subject to the same physical, biological, and chemical processes that govern natural surface water quality. This makes their performance highly predictable compared to more complex or novel technologies.

Physical Structure

The treatment function of a wet pond occurs within its permanent pool volume — the water that remains in the pond between storm events. The pond design includes a littoral zone (shallow vegetated margins), a deeper permanent pool, and a control structure with weir and orifice that manages the drawdown of runoff captured during storm events. Typical side slopes are 6:1 or 4:1 in the littoral zone and 2:1 in the deeper pool.

🏞️
Figure 6
Cross-sectional diagram of a typical wet detention pond showing: Top of Bank (T.O.B.), Groundwater Table (GWT), Normal Water Level (NWL), Littoral Zone with aquatic vegetation, Permanent Pool, Peak Attenuation zone, control structure with Weir and Orifice, Control Elevation, 6:1 or 4:1 side slopes in shallow area, and 2:1 side slopes in deeper zone. Note indicates that pollutant removal processes occur within the permanent pool volume and that removal efficiency is a function of residence time.

Pollutant Removal Processes

Physical Processes

Gravity settling is the primary physical mechanism. Efficiency depends on pond geometry, permanent pool volume, hydraulic residence time, and particle size distribution of suspended solids. Adsorption onto solid surfaces also occurs, particularly for phosphorus sorption onto mineral particles.

Biological Processes

Nutrient uptake by algae and submerged or emergent aquatic plants. Metabolization by bacteria and other microorganisms, particularly important for inorganic nitrogen removal through nitrification/denitrification and biological phosphorus uptake.

Chemical Processes

Co-precipitation of phosphorus with metal oxides (iron, aluminum, calcium) — particularly important for SRP removal. This process is concentration-dependent and becomes less effective as SRP concentrations decrease toward irreducible levels.

Key Design Principle

All three categories of removal processes are functions of pollutant concentration and residence time. Removal efficiency is directly related to how long the water column remains in contact with the biological, physical, and chemical removal mechanisms in the pond.

Irreducible Concentrations

The data in the table above illustrate a fundamental principle: nutrient concentrations in well-performing pond discharges are so low that further removal is biologically and chemically impossible — the system has reached its irreducible concentration.

Irreducible Concentration Concept

When nutrient concentrations in the water column drop below the minimum threshold required for biological uptake or chemical precipitation, no additional removal can occur regardless of detention time. For wet detention ponds, the irreducible concentrations are approximately:

Total Nitrogen: ~ 350–400 µg/L

Total Phosphorus: ~ 10–15 µg/L

The specific observations that define these irreducible conditions are:

Inorganic nitrogen — Ammonia (20 µg/L) and NOx (16 µg/L) are near the biological uptake limit. Algae and microorganisms cannot access nitrogen at concentrations below approximately 130 µg/L total inorganic nitrogen (TIN), so no further uptake is possible.

Dissolved Organic N — At 266 µg/L, this fraction dominates the residual TN (~66% of total). It remains resistant to removal because it requires slow biological mineralization.

SRP — At 6 µg/L, the pond is operating at or below the threshold for biological phosphorus uptake and chemical co-precipitation. Minimum uptake concentrations for SRP are approximately 10 µg/L (0.01 mg/L); below this threshold, uptake ceases.

TSS and Turbidity — Extremely low values (2.3 mg/L TSS; 2.0 NTU turbidity) confirm that essentially all setteable solids have been removed.

The practical conclusion is that a pond performing at these concentrations has maximized its treatment capability. Adding more surface area, deepening the pond, or extending detention time will yield no meaningful additional pollutant reduction. This defines the design ceiling for wet detention technology on residential runoff.

Section 10Nutrient Removal Relationships for Wet Ponds

Empirical data from monitored wet detention ponds have been used to develop statistical removal relationships as functions of hydraulic detention time. These relationships confirm that nutrient removal is primarily a function of detention time.

Regression Equations

For Total Nitrogen, the best-fit relationship is linear in detention time (R² = 0.808):

Efficiency (%) = (5.46 × t_d) / (44.72 + t_d) where t_d = hydraulic detention time (days)

For Total Phosphorus, the best-fit relationship is logarithmic in detention time (R² = 0.894):

Percent Removal = 40.15 + 6.366 × ln(t_d) + 0.214 × [ln(t_d)]² where t_d = hydraulic detention time (days)

(Right) Total Phosphorus removal efficiency (%) vs. detention time t_d (0–500 days); data points as filled circles, showing rapid initial removal of particulates ~40%, then continued increase for dissolved species removal, reaching 80–85% at long detention times; R² = 0.894. Important Limitations of These Relationships

Critical Limitation

These empirical removal relationships were developed using data from ponds receiving untreated runoff only. They do not apply when the incoming runoff has already received pretreatment (e.g., from swales, other BMPs, or treatment trains). Applying these curves to pretreated runoff will significantly overestimate achievable removal efficiency.

The underlying reason for this limitation is the first-order kinetic nature of pollutant removal. Removal of dissolved pollutants is a function of the water column concentration at any given moment:

As concentrations decrease toward the irreducible threshold, removal rates slow and eventually stop entirely. When pretreated water enters a pond at already-low concentrations, the standard removal curves — which assume untreated runoff concentrations as the starting point — are no longer valid. The irreducible concentrations for wet ponds are approximately TN = 350 µg/L and TP = 10 µg/L.

Section 11Wet Detention Enhancement — Floating Treatment Islands

Floating treatment islands (FTIs) consist of buoyant mats planted with emergent aquatic vegetation. The plant root systems hang below the mat into the water column, providing surface area for microbial biofilm growth and direct nutrient uptake by plant roots. A field study was conducted to evaluate FTI performance in an existing wet detention pond.

Section 12Manufacturer's Removal Claims

A recurring problem in stormwater BMP evaluation is the misapplication of performance data developed at nutrient concentrations far higher than those typically encountered in stormwater. Understanding the mechanism behind this discrepancy is essential for engineering practice.

The Concentration Problem

Many product testing studies for stormwater BMPs are conducted using concentrations far higher than those actually observed in stormwater runoff — drawn from wastewater treatment technology or agricultural irrigation contexts. Product efficiency claims are commonly based on the most favorable testing results obtained at these elevated concentrations.

Practitioner Guidance

When evaluating any BMP product for nutrient removal, always ask for performance data at the concentration range expected in your specific application. Products that work effectively on wastewater or agricultural runoff — where nutrient concentrations are one to two orders of magnitude greater than stormwater — frequently provide zero measurable benefit when applied to stormwater at typical concentrations.

Section 13Adsorption Media

Description and Mechanism

Adsorption media BMPs remove stormwater pollutants through two primary mechanisms: sorption of dissolved constituents onto solid surfaces, and physical entrapment of particulate matter within the media matrix. Different media types are designed to maximize removal of specific target pollutants.

Evaluated media types include sand, clay, carbon sources (compost, sawdust, cardboard, paper, and agricultural residues), lime rock, and various manufactured media blends.

Concentration Dependence of Sorption

Like all other BMP removal mechanisms, sorption processes obey first-order rate kinetics and are fundamentally concentration-dependent:

📈
Figure 13
Michaelis-Menten kinetics curve (enzyme kinetics / sorption rate curve) plotting Rate of Reaction (y-axis) vs. Substrate Concentration (x-axis). Curve shows: initial rapid increase from origin, inflection point at Km (half-saturation constant) corresponding to 1/2 Vmax, then asymptote approaching maximum velocity Vmax. Illustrates that reaction rate increases proportionally with concentration at low concentrations and that all media have a minimum required concentration below which no removal occurs.

Key principles governing adsorption media performance:

Sorption rates increase as concentrations increase. At low concentrations, the reaction rate decreases until it reaches zero at the minimum uptake concentration. Minimum uptake concentrations vary by media type and target constituent. All media have a minimum required concentration below which no removal occurs. Manufacturer's efficiency claims frequently lack information on the concentration range used in testing.

UCF Bold & Gold Media

A commonly evaluated adsorption medium for stormwater nutrient removal is the University of Central Florida Bold & Gold (B&G) media. Two formulations have been developed:

Original composition: 50% sand in natural soil, 30% tire crumb, 20% sawdust.

Current version — CTS24 (developed specifically for highway runoff): expanded clay, tire crumb, and sand.

Section 14Shallow Hardwood Wetlands

Characteristics

Hardwood forested wetlands (cypress domes, mixed hardwood swamps) are shallow waterbodies with nutrient-rich, acidic, and typically anaerobic soils. The water quality of discharges from these systems is governed primarily by an equilibrium between the accumulated organic soils and the water column — a fundamentally different mechanism from what occurs in open water wet ponds.

🌲
Figure 14
Two photographs: (Top) A shallow hardwood wetland (cypress or mixed hardwood swamp) with trees standing in water, thick leaf litter on ground, low light penetration through canopy. (Bottom) Detailed diagram of phosphorus cycling in a wetland system showing: inflows of Runoff and Atmospheric Deposition, plant biomass P, litterfall, aerobic and anaerobic zones, and biogeochemical pathways including DIP (dissolved inorganic phosphorus), DOP (dissolved organic phosphorus), POP (particulate organic phosphorus), PIP (particulate inorganic phosphorus), adsorbed P, Fe/Al/Ca-bound P, and peat accretion. Illustrates the complex soil-water phosphorus equilibrium.

Equilibrium Behavior

Unlike wet detention ponds, the water quality of hardwood wetland discharges is not primarily a function of residence time relative to an untreated runoff starting concentration. Instead, water quality is governed by an equilibrium between the water column and the organic soils:

First-order reaction kinetics based on the concentration gradient between water column and soil pore water govern the rate of exchange. Equilibrium is reached rapidly — typically within 3–4 days of residence in the wetland. High-concentration inputs will be reduced as the water column equilibrates toward the soil porewater concentration. Low-concentration inputs will be increased — the wetland releases nutrients into the water column if the incoming concentration is below the equilibrium level.

These equilibrium concentrations represent the asymptotic water quality that results from extended contact with hardwood wetland soils, regardless of the concentration of the incoming water.

A critical implication is that pre-treating runoff with alum or other coagulants before routing through a hardwood wetland provides no lasting benefit — the sediment contact re-equilibrates the treated water back toward the soil-controlled concentration within days.

Section 15Nutrient Equilibrium in Herbaceous Wetlands

Shallow herbaceous wetlands — dominated by emergent vegetation such as cattails, bulrush, and pickerelweed — function fundamentally differently from hardwood forested wetlands. The ecological structure of herbaceous systems creates conditions highly favorable for nutrient removal.

🌾
Figure 16
Photograph of a shallow herbaceous wetland showing dense stands of emergent vegetation (cattails, bulrush, and mixed grasses) with standing water visible between plant stalks. Low topography, open sky, and flooded ground visible throughout frame.

Mechanisms of Nutrient Removal

The dense emergent vegetation provides a large amount of structural surface area — plant stalks, submerged stems, root masses — that supports a large and diverse community of algae, bacteria, and micro-organisms. These biological communities form a dense, gelatinous biofilm layer on plant stalks referred to as zoogleal material.

Water flowing through the wetland meanders around and between plant stalks. This tortuous flow path provides extended contact time with the zoogleal biofilm communities, creating large opportunity for biological uptake of nutrients at every point in the flow path.

While herbaceous wetland soils are also anaerobic, a critical distinction from hardwood wetlands is that the dense vegetation effectively limits contact between the water column and the nutrient-rich soils. Sediment nutrient release is therefore minimal in well-established herbaceous systems.

Contrast with Hardwood Wetlands

In a hardwood wetland, the absence of dense emergent vegetation means water has direct and prolonged contact with nutrient-releasing soils — leading to equilibrium conditions that control (and often increase) outlet nutrient concentrations. In a herbaceous wetland, the vegetation structure intercepts the water column and drives biological treatment before significant soil contact occurs. This is the key difference that makes herbaceous wetlands effective for stormwater polishing applications.

Section 17Treatment Train — Wet Pond + Shallow Herbaceous Wetland

When a wet detention pond is followed by a properly designed shallow herbaceous wetland, a very different result is obtained compared to the hardwood wetland scenario. The herbaceous wetland acts as a true polishing step, achieving further reductions in both nitrogen and phosphorus from already-treated pond effluent.

Design Principles for the Treatment Train

The wet detention pond performs the primary function — removal of solids and dissolved nutrients — and its efficiency is calculated using the standard empirical removal curves (Section 10). The herbaceous wetland then receives pond effluent at 35 µg/L TP and 750 µg/L TN and achieves additional removal through biological uptake by the zoogleal biofilm communities and emergent vegetation, driving concentrations down to approximately 15 µg/L TP and 400 µg/L TN.

Critical Design Note

The standard wet detention pond removal curves cannot be applied directly when sizing the pond in a treatment train that includes downstream herbaceous wetland polishing. The curves were developed for direct discharge from the pond, not for systems where additional treatment occurs downstream. The pond efficiency must be modeled as the first stage, with herbaceous wetland removal applied incrementally to pond effluent concentrations — not to raw influent concentrations.

Section 18Summary

This module has established the central principle that governs all BMP selection and sizing decisions: the concentration of target pollutants in the source water must be matched to the concentration reduction capabilities of the selected BMP. Failing to account for actual stormwater concentrations — whether by over-relying on manufacturer claims, misapplying wetland types, or using removal curves outside their applicable range — leads to systems that perform far below design expectations or fail entirely.

First-Order Rate Kinetics

All biological, physical, and chemical removal processes in stormwater BMPs obey first-order rate kinetics. This means removal rate increases and decreases in direct proportion to the water column concentration of the target pollutant. Every process — biological uptake, adsorption, co-precipitation, gravity settling — requires a minimum concentration to initiate. Below the minimum concentration, no removal occurs regardless of residence time, media surface area, or biological activity.

Evaluating Manufacturer Claims

Literature removal efficiencies are frequently based on elevated nutrient concentrations where removals are greatest and most impressive. A product that achieves 90% removal starting from 1,000 µg/L will achieve 0% removal starting from 100 µg/L — both results may be technically accurate, but only one is relevant to a typical stormwater application.

Product removal efficiencies stated as percentage values have little practical meaning without disclosure of the concentration range tested. The minimum achievable effluent concentration for a BMP is far more important than its rated percentage removal.

When evaluating any product, always ask: What are the typical stormwater concentrations for my application? What effluent concentrations was this product tested at? What is the minimum achievable effluent concentration? What are the field performance data under actual stormwater conditions?

Technology Transfer Limitations

Techniques that are successful in wastewater treatment frequently cannot be transferred to stormwater applications. Wastewater nutrient concentrations are typically one order of magnitude greater than stormwater concentrations — providing the minimum concentration conditions required for many removal technologies to function effectively. The same technology applied to stormwater will often find that influent concentrations are already below the minimum threshold for removal.

Field Testing

Field testing under actual site conditions is essential before implementing any retrofit BMP, particularly novel or proprietary technologies. Laboratory or controlled testing results at elevated concentrations do not substitute for field performance data. Testing should be conducted using actual site runoff, at actual site concentrations, over a sufficient monitoring period to capture the range of storm events and seasonal variation.
