Learning Objectives

Upon completing this module, participants will be able to:

  1. Understand the definition and regulatory basis of DCIA.
  2. Know what is important for discharge volume and mass estimates when disconnecting the DCIA.
  3. Enter data into BMPFast for analysis to meet performance standards for a discharge analysis.
  4. Apply calculation methods used to estimate discharge volume and mass.
  5. Work through a complete example problem for disconnecting DCIA in a residential development scenario.
  6. Recognize that discharge mass and volume may also be independently important beyond percentage removal targets (e.g., in Basin Management Action Plan areas).
Key Principle
Composite CN calculation must be based on volume discharge, not the arithmetic average or the area-weighted average. The simple arithmetic average CN can significantly overestimate runoff and does not reflect the nonlinear nature of the curve number method.

DCIA Definition

The following definition is drawn from the Florida Applicant's Handbook (AH) Volume 1, 2024:

Regulatory Definition — AH Vol. 1, 2024

"Directly connected impervious area" or "DCIA" means the area covered by a building, impermeable pavement, and/or other impervious surfaces, which drains directly into a conveyance system without first flowing across sufficient permeable vegetated land area — as referenced in Section 9.2 of this Volume — to allow for infiltration of runoff.

In practical terms, DCIA encompasses any impervious surface where stormwater runoff is channeled directly into pipes, inlets, or other conveyance infrastructure rather than first passing over and through vegetated pervious areas. Rooftops draining to gutters and downspouts connected to storm drains, parking lots with curb-and-gutter drainage, and roads with curb inlets are common examples of DCIA.

Non-DCIA Criteria (Section 9.2)

Section 9.2 of AH Volume 1 defines the conditions under which impervious areas are considered not directly connected — and therefore classified as Non-DCIA:

AH Vol. 1, Section 9.2 — Non-DCIA Definition

Non-Directly Connected Impervious Areas (NonDCIA) include all pervious areas and portions of impervious areas that flow over at least:

  • 10 feet of undisturbed pervious area with Hydrologic Soil Group (HSG) A or B soils; or
  • 20 feet of undisturbed pervious area for other soil types (HSG C or D);

unless the applicant demonstrates that a narrower width would provide sufficient infiltration to disconnect the impervious area by percolating the desired runoff volume from a 3-year, 1-hour storm event.

The 3-year, 1-hour storm event is the governing design storm for evaluating disconnection. Runoff from the disconnected impervious area must be fully infiltrated within the pervious strip. The soil storage capacity S′ derived from the curve number of the receiving pervious soils must equal or exceed the rainfall depth from the 3-year, 1-hour IDF curve for the project's meteorological zone.

Curve Number Method for Disconnection Analysis

The SCS (NRCS) Curve Number method is used to determine whether the pervious receiving area has sufficient soil storage capacity to absorb the runoff from the disconnected impervious area. The key parameter is the potential maximum retention S′:

SCS Potential Maximum Retention
S′ = (1000 / CN) − 10

Example: CN = 78 → S′ = (1000/78) − 10 = 2.82 inches

For disconnection to be valid, S′ must exceed the 3-year, 1-hour rainfall depth for the site's meteorological zone. In the example problem (Florida Zone 4, mean annual rainfall = 52 inches), the IDF curve yields a 3-year, 1-hour volume of 2.4 inches. Since S′ = 2.82 inches > 2.4 inches, the soils can store more than the design storm produces — disconnection is feasible for HSG A/B soils with CN ≈ 78.

Critical Error to Avoid
Do not use a simple arithmetic average or area-weighted average CN to compute composite runoff. The CN-to-runoff relationship is highly nonlinear. Averaging CNs before computing runoff produces systematic errors. The composite CN must be derived from the volume-discharge method, computing individual area runoff volumes and summing before back-calculating a composite CN. In the example, a simple average yields CN = 86.5, while the correct composite CN from BMPFast is 91.27 (with DCIA) or 83.81 (with 1.1 ac disconnected).

Composite CN Calculator in BMPFast

BMPFast includes a Composite CN Calculator accessible from the Watershed Characteristics screen. Users enter each land area with its associated CN value. The calculator computes the runoff coefficient C and weighted C for each area, sums them, and derives the volume-correct composite CN. The interface also displays the DCIA Percentage and the Avg Weighted C.

Curve Number Value Rationale

The specific CN values used in this analysis reflect important technical considerations within the BMPFast framework:

Why CN = 95 for Impervious Areas?

ROC (Runoff Coefficient) is calculated using an initial abstraction of 0.10 inches — rainfall excess begins once this threshold is exceeded. The CN associated with a rainfall excess starting at 0.10 inches is 95.

This reflects the near-total impermeability of paved or roofed surfaces, where almost all rainfall above a minimal surface storage threshold becomes runoff.

Why CN = 55 for Pervious Areas?

Sandy soils are used for the pervious receiving area, but they are assumed to be compacted from construction activity. CN = 55 reflects moderately compacted sandy conditions.

Natural, undisturbed sandy soils (HSG A) would typically have CN values well below 55. Where native sandy soils are preserved, a lower CN and therefore greater soil storage capacity may be justified.

Composite CN Sensitivity to DCIA Fraction

The composite CN is highly sensitive to the fraction of the catchment that remains as DCIA versus pervious or disconnected impervious. The following comparison illustrates the impact of disconnecting 1.1 acres of the 1.3-acre DCIA:

Basin Management Action Plans (BMAPs)
In designated BMAP areas, absolute discharge mass (pounds per year) may be regulated in addition to percentage removal efficiency. In these cases, disconnecting DCIA provides a meaningful additional benefit by reducing the total mass load reaching receiving waters — even when both scenarios would satisfy percentage-based targets. Designers working in BMAP areas should consider DCIA disconnection as a cost-effective mass reduction strategy.

Key Takeaways

Summary of Core Concepts

  1. DCIA Definition: Impervious area that drains directly to a conveyance system without passing over sufficient pervious area for infiltration. Disconnection requires at least 10 feet (HSG A/B) or 20 feet (HSG C/D) of undisturbed pervious area, unless soil percolation is demonstrated for the 3-year, 1-hour storm.
  2. Soil Storage Check: Use S′ = (1000/CN) − 10 to compare pervious area storage capacity against the 3-year, 1-hour IDF rainfall. Disconnection is feasible only when S′ exceeds the design storm depth.
  3. Volume-Correct Composite CN: Always compute composite CN from the sum of individual area runoff volumes — never from arithmetic or area-weighted CN averages. BMPFast automates this calculation.
  4. CN Values: Use CN = 95 for impervious areas (initial abstraction = 0.10 in) and appropriate CN for pervious areas based on soil type and condition (CN = 55 for compacted sandy soil; lower for undisturbed native sandy soils).
  5. Mass vs. Percentage: Even when percentage removal targets are met with or without disconnection, disconnecting DCIA meaningfully reduces total discharged pollutant mass — critical in BMAP-regulated areas where absolute mass load limits apply.
  6. BMPFast Workflow: General Site Info → Watershed Characteristics (CN, DCIA%) → BMP Entry (Rain Garden) → Configure Catchments (Routing) → Summary Treatment Report.
