What Is a Wet Detention Pond?

A wet detention pond is, simply put, a pond — one that holds water year around. The regulatory definition provides important technical precision:

Regulatory Definition "Wet detention" means the collection and temporary storage of stormwater in a permanently wet impoundment in such a manner as to provide for treatment through physical, chemical, and biological processes with subsequent gradual release of the stormwater.

Unlike dry retention or infiltration systems, wet detention maintains a permanent pool of water. Incoming stormwater mixes with this pool and is held for a period sufficient for physical settling of particulates, biological uptake of nutrients, and chemical transformation processes. Treated water is then released gradually through a control structure.

Key structural elements of a wet detention pond include a permanent pool at a defined Normal Water Level (N.W.L.), side slopes (typically 6:1 on the landward side and 2:1 on the pond side), a pollution abatement volume above the permanent pool, peak attenuation volume, a littoral zone (if required), and an outflow control structure consisting of a weir and orifice with an outflow pipe set at a control elevation.

Effectiveness as a Function of Annual Residence Time (ART)

The primary driver of wet detention treatment performance is the Average Annual Residence Time (ART) — a measure of how long, on average, stormwater remains in the permanent pool before being discharged. Longer residence times allow more complete physical, chemical, and biological treatment.

Formula — Annual Residence Time ART (days) = [Volume of Permanent Pool (ac-ft) × 365 days/year] ÷ Annual Runoff (ac-ft/year)

The ART forms the X-axis of the detention efficiency curves. Treatment performance for Total Phosphorus (TP) and Total Nitrogen (TN) are calculated separately using empirically derived regression equations developed from field monitoring of Florida wet detention facilities.

Performance Equations

TP Removal Efficiency (R² = 0.897) Percent Removal = 40.13 + 6.372 · ln(td) + 0.213 · [ln(td)]²
TN Removal Efficiency (R² = 0.800) Percent Removal = (43.75 · td) ÷ (4.38 + td)

Where td = Average Annual Residence Time (days), equivalent to ART.

Managed Aquatic Plants (MAP) Credit

Managed Aquatic Plants — including floating wetland mats — can receive up to 20% additional removal credit, provided there is sufficient area coverage and a documented, more frequent replacement plan. The standard credit for floating wetlands at 5% surface area coverage is 12%, as discussed in the following section.

Floating Wetlands and MAP Credits

Floating wetland systems — buoyant platforms supporting emergent aquatic vegetation — can be installed within the permanent pool of a wet detention pond to enhance nutrient removal through additional biological uptake pathways. Research has demonstrated that floating wetlands with 5% surface area coverage provide a 12% additional removal credit.

BMPs in Series: Fundamental Principles

When two or more wet detention ponds are configured in series — each with, for example, a 50-day residence time — a critical misconception must be avoided: the removal efficiencies of the individual ponds cannot be simply added together.

Common Error Doubling the permanent pool does not double the removal. You cannot remove the same pollutants more than once. A general rule applies: upstream BMPs do impact downstream effectiveness when the removal mechanisms are the same.

Why Simple Addition Fails

Consider two detention BMPs in series, each with a 50-day ART, producing individual efficiencies of approximately 40% for TN and 70% for TP:

Approach TN Efficiency TP Efficiency Valid?
Incorrect: Add individual efficiencies 40% + 40% = 80% 70% + 70% = 140% ✗ Invalid
Correct: Sum ART, read combined curve 50d + 50d = 100d → ~43% 50d + 50d = 100d → ~74% ✓ Valid
Regulatory Constraint Equation 9-5 from AH Vol. 1, Appendix O cannot be used for two detention BMPs in series because the upstream detention BMP does affect the pollutant concentrations entering the downstream pond, violating the equation's independence assumption.

Series Efficiency Using Equation 9-5 (Mixed-Mechanism BMPs)

Equation 9-5 from AH Vol. 1 can be applied when the removal mechanisms of the upstream and downstream BMPs are fundamentally different — for example, wet detention followed by a sorption media filter. The equation assumes each downstream BMP acts independently of upstream BMPs in terms of pollutant removal mechanisms:

Equation 9-5 — Overall Treatment Train Efficiency Efftotal = 1 − [(1 − E₁) × (1 − E₂) × (1 − E₃) × … × (1 − En)]

Simplified for two BMPs:
Efftotal = Eff₁ + [(1 − Eff₁) × Eff₂]

Wet Detention Effectiveness When Preceded by Upstream Retention

When a retention BMP (which infiltrates water and removes particulates) is located upstream of a wet detention pond, a more complex interaction occurs. Upstream retention removes a fraction of particulate-bound nutrients from the runoff before it reaches the wet pond. This has two important consequences:

  1. The overall system efficiency is enhanced relative to a standalone wet pond.
  2. The effective removal efficiency of the wet pond itself is reduced because the particulate nutrients that the pond would have settled out have already been removed upstream.
Key Principle When there are BMPs in series and the upstream BMP is retention, particulates are removed first. When followed by a wet pond, the wet pond's percent removal is reduced by the fraction of particulate nutrients removed upstream — at a maximum of 20% for phosphorus and 10% for nitrogen. The fraction removed upstream is proportional to the treatment depth, and the maximum adjustment is scaled proportionally to the upstream annual removal percentage.

This adjustment mechanism prevents "double counting" of particulate removal credit across both the upstream retention and downstream wet detention systems.

Example Calculation — Series with 50% Upstream TN Retention

For a standalone system with 50% TN retention upstream and 38% wet detention downstream at ART = 31 days:

100 mg
→
Upstream Retention
50% removal
→
50 mg remaining
→
Wet Detention

→
33.5 mg discharged
The wet pond removal is adjusted from 38% (standalone) to 33% (accounting for upstream particulate capture).
Overall: Eff = 1 − [(1 − 0.50) × (1 − 0.33)] = 1 − 0.335 = 66.5% combined TN removal

Particulate Removal Upstream — Calculating Adjustments

To properly account for the interaction between upstream retention and downstream wet detention, the fraction of nutrients in particulate form must be known. The following procedure applies:

Inputs Required

Parameter Description Example Value
% particulates in runoff Fraction of total nutrient load in particulate form 50%
% annual TN load of particles removed upstream Read from TN removal charts for the upstream retention system 20%
% annual TP load of particles removed upstream Read from TP removal charts for the upstream retention system 40%

Calculating Maximum Adjustments

Maximum TN Reduction (MTN) MTN = (% particles in runoff) × (fraction annual TN load removed upstream)
MTN = 50% × 20% = 10% maximum reduction in TN wet pond credit
Maximum TP Reduction (MTP) MTP = (% particles in runoff) × (fraction annual TP load removed upstream)
MTP = 50% × 40% = 20% maximum reduction in TP wet pond credit

Key Methodological Difference In the 2020 software, the wet pond removal is reduced by a constant amount regardless of the upstream retention efficiency. In the 2025 software, the decrease in wet pond removal is a function of upstream retention efficiency — making it more physically accurate. The 2025 methodology also supports use of upstream LID retention systems with low effectiveness (such as tree wells and rain gardens) that were not properly handled in the 2020 version.

Key Takeaways

Definition

A wet detention pond is a permanently wet impoundment that treats stormwater through physical, chemical, and biological processes with gradual controlled discharge.

Annual Residence Time

ART drives performance: ART = (Pool Volume × 365) ÷ Annual Runoff. Longer ART yields better pollutant removal. Regulatory cap is 200 days for credit purposes.

Littoral Zone Effect

A littoral zone is required for full efficiency credit. Without one, removal is reduced by 10%: Rem(no lit) = Rem(lit) ÷ 1.1.

BMPs in Series

Do not add efficiencies from ponds in series — sum the ART values instead. Equation 9-5 applies only when removal mechanisms differ between upstream and downstream BMPs.

Upstream Retention Interaction

Upstream retention reduces downstream wet pond efficiency by the fraction of particulates already removed. Maximum adjustments: 10% for TN and 20% for TP.

Treatment Train Requirement

Wet detention alone frequently does not meet performance standards. In this example, adding a surface discharge filter raised combined TN to 78% and TP to 93%, satisfying all requirements.
