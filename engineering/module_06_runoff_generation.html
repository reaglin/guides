BMPFast Stormwater Training Series · Module 6

Runoff Generation

Harvey H. Harper  ·  January 2026  ·  Environmental Research & Design, Inc.

This module presents the theoretical basis and computational methodology for estimating stormwater runoff volumes from developed catchments in Florida, with particular emphasis on the SCS Curve Number methodology as implemented in the BMPFast model and Florida's 2024 Stormwater Rule.

1. Runoff Generation

Runoff is one component of the hydrologic cycle — the continuous movement of water through the environment via precipitation, infiltration, evapotranspiration, and streamflow. Understanding how precipitation translates into surface runoff is fundamental to stormwater management design.

Runoff generation is governed by three primary factors:

  • Precipitation — depth, intensity, and temporal distribution of rainfall events
  • Soil types — the infiltration capacity and permeability of soils in a watershed
  • Land cover — the degree to which the land surface is vegetated, paved, or otherwise modified

2. Development Impacts on Hydrology

3. Runoff Volume Estimation

Accurate estimation of runoff volume requires a model that can incorporate all of the factors that influence the rainfall–runoff relationship within a watershed. These factors include:

  • Land use — the type and density of development
  • Impervious surfaces — the fraction of the basin covered by impervious materials
  • Soil types — classified into Hydrologic Soil Groups (A, B, C, D)
  • Topography
    • Basin slope — affects the rate of overland flow
    • Depressional areas — areas that store water and reduce runoff
  • Precipitation depth and event characteristics — the size and distribution of individual storm events

A runoff model must be capable of incorporating each of these factors into event-based runoff depths in order to yield reliable volume estimates for stormwater treatment design.

4. Runoff Coefficients (C Values)

Runoff coefficients reflect the proportion of rainfall that becomes runoff under specified conditions. Tabular C values are widely used to size drainage pipes through application of the Rational Formula:

Rational Formula — Peak Flow
Q = C × i × A
Q = peak flow rate (cfs)
C = runoff coefficient (dimensionless) — estimated for a design storm event, typically 10-year
i = rainfall intensity (in/hr) for the design storm
A = drainage area (acres)

Important limitation: Rational Formula runoff coefficients are calibrated for peak-flow design storm conditions and cannot be used for estimation of runoff volumes for non-design storm conditions or for evaluating average annual pollutant loadings.

5. SCS Curve Number Methodology

Multiple runoff models were evaluated for use in the BMPFast methodology, including the SCS Curve Number method, ICPR, and SWIM. The SCS Curve Number (CN) methodology was selected based on the following considerations:

  • It is the most common methodology used by civil engineers and is embedded in many proprietary hydrologic models throughout Florida.
  • The methodology is documented in NRCS Technical Release TR-55, Urban Hydrology for Small Watersheds (June 1986).
  • Curve numbers (CN values) are empirically derived values used to predict runoff volume as a function of soil type and land cover.
  • The method predicts event-specific runoff depths and volumes from individual storm events.
  • The model was used to calculate annual runoff coefficients (C values) for meteorological sites throughout Florida.
📄 Reference — NRCS TR-55 Cover USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service, Technical Release TR-55: "Urban Hydrology for Small Watersheds," June 1986. This document provides the foundational CN methodology used in the BMPFast model.

6. Hydrologic Soil Groups

The SCS method is based on Hydrologic Soil Groups (HSG), which classify soils by their minimum infiltration rate for bare soils after thorough wetting. Four groups are defined:

Table 1 — Hydrologic Soil Group Definitions

HSG Description Soil Infiltration Rate Runoff Potential
A Deep, well to excessively drained sands with a high rate of water transmission. Very High Very Low
B Moderately deep to deep, moderately well to well drained soils with moderately fine to moderately coarse textures. High Low
C Soils with a layer that impedes downward movement of water and soils with moderately fine to fine structure. Low High
D Clay soils, soils with a permanent high water table, soils with a hardpan or clay layer at or near the surface. Very Low Very High

8. Hydrologic Conditions

Each Hydrologic Soil Group has sub-sets for varying hydrologic conditions (poor, fair, and good), defined based on a combination of factors that affect infiltration and runoff:

  • Density and canopy of vegetative areas
  • Amount of year-round cover
  • Amount of grass or close-seeded legumes
  • Percent of residue cover on the land surface (good condition ≥ 20%)
  • Degree of surface roughness

Poor Condition

Factors impair infiltration and tend to increase runoff. Typical examples include heavily grazed or compacted soils with sparse vegetative cover.

Fair Condition

Typical or average runoff conditions. Represents moderate vegetative cover with some limitations to infiltration.

Good Condition

Factors encourage average and better-than-average infiltration and tend to decrease runoff. Dense, healthy vegetative cover with minimal soil disturbance.

9. SCS Method of Calculating Runoff

The SCS method estimates runoff depth for individual storm events through a two-step process. First, soil storage capacity is computed from the weighted-average CN value based on land use and soil type. Then, runoff depth is calculated using the standard SCS equation.

Step 1 — Soil Storage Capacity

Soil Storage Parameter
S = (1000 / CN) − 10
S = potential maximum retention after runoff begins (inches)
CN = weighted-average curve number for the drainage area

Step 2 — Runoff Depth

SCS Runoff Equation
Q = (Pᵢ − 0.2S)² / (Pᵢ + 0.8S)
Q = runoff depth for event i (inches)
Pᵢ = rainfall depth for event i (inches)
S = soil storage parameter (inches)
Note: Q = 0 when Pᵢ ≤ 0.2S (no runoff generated below this threshold)

Limitation of standard SCS: The standard SCS method can be subject to large errors when CN values are averaged across areas with widely different characteristics. To reduce this error, the BMPFast methodology modifies the SCS method to calculate separate runoff volumes for the Directly Connected Impervious Area (DCIA) and the non-DCIA area.

10. Relationship Between Curve Number, DCIA, and C Value

The relationship between CN values, percent DCIA, and the resulting annual runoff coefficient (C value) is not linear — this has important implications for the way runoff calculations should be structured.

  • Linear relationship exists between C Value and percent DCIA — doubling DCIA approximately doubles the runoff contribution from impervious surfaces.
  • Exponential relationship exists between C Value and CN value — small increases in CN at the high end of the scale produce disproportionately large increases in runoff.
  • This exponential behavior implies that averaging CN values across areas with different characteristics is statistically invalid and leads to significant overestimation of runoff volume.

The surface demonstrates the linear relationship with DCIA and the exponential relationship with CN value.

11. Directly Connected Impervious Areas (DCIA)

The definition of Directly Connected Impervious Area (DCIA) varies depending on the purpose of the hydrologic analysis:

Flood Routing — Major Events

For flood design purposes, DCIA includes all impervious areas from which runoff discharges directly into the drainage system. Areas where runoff discharges as concentrated shallow flow over pervious areas and then into the drainage system (e.g., shallow roadside swales) are also considered DCIA. This definition is often generously estimated to provide a safety factor in design.

Annual Runoff Estimation — Common Daily Events

For annual runoff volume calculations used in stormwater treatment sizing, DCIA is defined more narrowly: it includes only impervious areas from which runoff discharges directly into the drainage system during small, common rainfall events. Shallow vegetated swales are not included under this definition. As a result, this approach generally yields a lower DCIA value than the flood routing definition.

The BMPFast methodology uses the annual runoff estimation definition of DCIA, which yields more conservative (lower) impervious area values and thus avoids overestimating required treatment system sizes.

12. Non-DCIA Area Calculations

Non-Directly Connected Impervious Area (non-DCIA) includes all pervious areas plus any impervious areas that are not classified as DCIA. Runoff from these areas must pass over or through vegetated or pervious surfaces before reaching the drainage system.

Non-DCIA Curve Number

A composite curve number is calculated for the non-DCIA area using an area-weighted average of the pervious CN and the impervious non-DCIA CN (using 98 for all impervious surfaces):

Non-DCIA Composite Curve Number
nDCIA CN = [(Areaperv × CNperv) + (Areanon-DCIA × 98)] / (Areaperv + Areanon-DCIA)

Non-DCIA Soil Storage

The composite non-DCIA CN value is then used to calculate soil storage for the non-DCIA area:

Non-DCIA Soil Storage
S = (1000 / nDCIA CN) − 10

13. Calculation of Runoff Volumes

The BMPFast methodology calculates runoff volumes separately for the DCIA and non-DCIA areas. Using a single overall CN value for the entire drainage basin would lead to significant errors in annual runoff estimation due to the nonlinear relationship between CN and C values.

1. Runoff from Non-DCIA Areas

Runoff from the non-DCIA area is calculated using the modified SCS equation with the composite non-DCIA curve number:

Non-DCIA Runoff per Event
QnDCIA,i = (Pᵢ − 0.2S)² / (Pᵢ + 0.8S)
CN = curve number for pervious area
Imp. = percent impervious area
DCIA = percent directly connected impervious area
non-DCIA CN = composite curve number for non-DCIA area
Pᵢ = rainfall depth for event i (inches)
QnDCIA,i = runoff depth from non-DCIA area for event i (inches)

2. Runoff from DCIA

DCIA runoff is calculated using a simplified linear relationship that accounts for the initial abstraction on impervious surfaces:

DCIA Runoff per Event
QDCIA,i = Pᵢ − 0.1
When Pᵢ is less than 0.1 inches, QDCIA,i = 0 (no runoff generated from very small events).
The 0.1-inch initial abstraction accounts for depression storage on paved surfaces.

15. Hydrologic Modeling

To develop annual runoff coefficient tables for use in the BMPFast methodology, a continuous simulation of runoff from a hypothetical 1-acre site was conducted using the SCS curve number methodology applied to historical hourly rainfall records from 45 meteorological sites across Florida.

The scope of the modeling effort was extensive:

  • Historical data ranged from 13 to 64 years per site, with most sites containing 30+ years of record (mean of 4,685 rain events per site).
  • Runoff was modeled for all individual rain events at each site.
  • DCIA percentages were varied from 0% to 100% in 5-unit intervals.
  • Non-DCIA curve numbers were varied from 25 to 95 in 5-unit intervals.
  • This produced 350 combinations per rainfall site × 45 sites = 15,750 total model runs.

The total generated runoff depth was compared with the total rainfall depth over the simulation period to calculate the mean annual runoff coefficient (ROC) for each combination of site, DCIA, and non-DCIA CN:

Mean Annual Runoff Coefficient
ROC = Total Runoff Depth Over Simulation Period / Total Rainfall Depth Over Simulation Period

16. Meteorological Sites

The 45 hourly rainfall sites used for runoff modeling were distributed throughout the state of Florida to capture the full range of climatic variability across the state. Sites span from the Panhandle region in the northwest to Key West in the south.

18. Impacts of Rainfall Characteristics on Runoff Generation

The proportion of annual rainfall volume delivered in events greater than 1.0 inch varies significantly across Florida's meteorological stations. This distribution of event sizes — rather than total annual rainfall depth — is the primary driver of differences in annual ROC values between regions.

  • Key West and Melbourne have a large percentage of small events and a relatively low percentage of large events (greater than 1 inch). This results in less annual runoff volume despite substantial total annual rainfall, because small events generate proportionally less runoff.
  • Pensacola and Tallahassee have a low percentage of small events and a higher percentage of large events. This results in more annual runoff volume because larger events exceed the initial abstraction threshold more frequently and generate disproportionately greater runoff depths.

19. Similar Meteorological Zones

A cluster analysis was conducted to group the 45 meteorological sites into regions with similar annual rainfall/runoff relationships (ROC values). The analysis identified five significantly different meteorological zones across Florida. Importantly, differences between zones are due to rainfall distribution characteristics (i.e., the proportion of large versus small events) rather than total annual rainfall depth.

  • Zone 1 — Panhandle: Pensacola, Panama City, Tallahassee area — high ROC values due to large event proportion
  • Zone 2 — North/Central Florida: Jacksonville, Gainesville, Ocala area
  • Zone 3 — Central Florida: Orlando, Tampa area
  • Zone 4 — Southwest/South Florida: Fort Myers, Miami area
  • Zone 5 — Keys/Southeast: Key West, South Florida — lowest ROC values

20. Runoff Calculation Data

The complete ROC value tables for all five meteorological zones are provided in Appendix C of Harper and Baker (2007). BMPFast uses these tables as lookup references and performs interpolation for combinations of DCIA and CN that fall between the tabulated values.

Required input data for runoff calculations include:

  • Rainfall meteorological zone (determined from the rainfall zone map, Figure 9)
  • Annual rainfall depth from isopleth maps
  • Basin DCIA percentage
  • Non-DCIA curve number (composite, as calculated per Section 12)

BMPFast conducts iterations for uneven values of DCIA and CN, interpolating between tabulated values to calculate the annual runoff coefficient (ROC value) and annual runoff volume.

21. Impacts of Disconnecting DCIA on Annual C Values

A key design strategy for reducing stormwater runoff volumes is to minimize DCIA — directing roof and driveway drainage to pervious areas (lawns, swales) rather than directly to the storm drain system. This approach can significantly reduce the annual runoff coefficient and, consequently, the required treatment system size.

🏗️ Figure 11 — DCIA Maximizing vs. Minimizing Site Designs Side-by-side cross-section diagrams illustrating two residential drainage approaches. Left (Design Which Maximizes DCIA): Roof drains discharge to street via downspouts; driveway drains to street via curb and gutter — all impervious runoff enters the storm drain directly. Right (Design Which Minimizes DCIA): Roof drains discharge to lawn via splash blocks; driveway drains to lawn via grassed swale — impervious runoff passes over pervious areas before reaching the drainage system.

22. Example Calculations

The following example demonstrates the step-by-step calculation of annual runoff volume for a 100-acre mixed-use development site using the BMPFast methodology.

Site Description

Step 1 — Land Use

Total site area: 100 acres

  • 90 acres of single-family residential development
  • 5 acres of stormwater management systems
  • 5 acres of preserved wetlands

Note: The 5-acre stormwater management area is excluded from runoff volume calculations because runoff generated in these areas is incorporated into the performance efficiency estimates for the stormwater system.

Step 2 — Ground Cover and Soil Types

A. Residential areas will be covered with lawns in good condition.
B. Soil types are in Hydrologic Soil Group D.

Step 3 — Impervious and DCIA Areas

Residential areas will be 25% impervious, 75% of which will be DCIA.

Impervious Area = 25% of developed site = 90 ac × 0.25 = 22.50 acres
DCIA Area = 22.50 ac × 0.75 = 16.88 acres
DCIA Percentage = (16.88 ac / 90.0 ac) × 100 = 18.75% of developed area
Step 4 — Composite Non-DCIA Curve Number

Curve number for lawns in good condition in HSG D = 80

Pervious area = 90 ac total − 22.50 ac impervious = 67.50 acres
Non-DCIA impervious area = 22.50 ac − 16.88 ac DCIA = 5.62 acres
CN for impervious areas = 98
Non-DCIA CN = [67.50 ac (80) + 5.62 ac (98)] / (67.50 ac + 5.62 ac)
Non-DCIA CN = 81.4
Step 5a — Annual Runoff Volume (Pensacola, Zone 1)

The model calculates the annual runoff coefficient based on the meteorological zone and the hydrologic characteristics:

Location: Pensacola = Zone 1
DCIA = 18.75%, non-DCIA CN = 81.4
Annual C value (from Zone 1 ROC table) = 0.304
Annual rainfall for Pensacola = 65.5 inches (from Isopleth Map)
Annual runoff volume = 90 ac × 65.5 in/yr × (1 ft / 12 in) × 0.304
Annual generated runoff volume = 149.3 ac-ft/yr
Step 5b — Annual Runoff Volume (Key West, Zone 3)

Same site parameters applied in a different meteorological zone:

Location: Key West = Zone 3
DCIA = 18.75%, non-DCIA CN = 81.4
Annual C value (from Zone 3 ROC table) = 0.266
Annual rainfall for Key West = 40.0 inches (from Isopleth Map)
Annual runoff volume = 90 ac × 40.0 in/yr × (1 ft / 12 in) × 0.266
Annual generated runoff volume = 79.8 ac-ft/yr

This example illustrates that despite identical site characteristics, a project in Pensacola generates nearly twice the annual runoff volume of an equivalent project in Key West — a direct consequence of the differences in rainfall event distribution between the two meteorological zones.

23. Summary

Like rainfall, runoff generation in Florida is highly variable across the state and is driven by three interacting factors:

Impervious Area

A direct (linear) relationship exists between impervious percentage and annual runoff volume. Minimizing DCIA is the most effective design strategy for reducing runoff generation.

Non-DCIA CN Value

An exponential relationship exists between CN value and runoff. This makes CN averaging statistically invalid and necessitates the separate DCIA/non-DCIA calculation approach used in BMPFast.

Rainfall Characteristics

The distribution of event sizes — not total annual rainfall — drives regional differences in annual ROC values. The Panhandle generates substantially more runoff per inch of rain than South Florida.

Florida's 2024 Stormwater Rule and the BMPFast model use the Harper and Baker (2007) report methodology for calculating runoff volumes, applying the modified SCS CN approach with separate DCIA and non-DCIA calculations and zone-specific ROC lookup tables.
