Vegetated Stormwater Treatment Areas (STAs)

Harvey H. Harper • Environmental Research & Design, Inc. • January 2026

1. Overview

Vegetated Stormwater Treatment Areas (STAs) are constructed stormwater management systems that use aquatic and emergent vegetation to remove pollutants — particularly nutrients — from stormwater runoff before it is discharged to receiving water bodies. STAs are designed to exploit the natural biological and physical processes associated with wetland plant communities to attenuate nitrogen and phosphorus loads.

While the concept is theoretically sound, field monitoring of constructed STAs in Florida has revealed a number of significant performance limitations that designers and permit applicants must understand before incorporating STAs into a stormwater management plan.

2. Nutrient Uptake Mechanisms

Nutrient removal in vegetated STAs occurs through two primary biological processes:

  • Uptake through plant roots — Rooted aquatic and emergent macrophytes assimilate dissolved nitrogen and phosphorus directly from the water column and underlying sediment porewater through their root systems. This process sequesters nutrients into plant biomass.
  • Biological communities attached to plant stalks — Periphyton, biofilms, and other microbial communities colonize the submerged surfaces of plant stems and stalks. These communities provide additional surface area for biological nutrient transformation processes, including nitrification, denitrification, and phosphorus assimilation.

Substrate Considerations

To accelerate plant establishment, many STA installations have historically added organic muck soils to provide a nutrient-rich substrate that promotes rapid plant growth and more quickly achieves full vegetation coverage. While this practice does improve plant establishment rates, it has produced significant unintended consequences for water quality performance, as detailed in the monitoring findings below.

Hydrologic Effects

Vegetated STAs typically generate large evapotranspiration (ET) losses. The combination of open water evaporation and transpiration by dense vegetation can substantially reduce the volume of water that exits the STA relative to the volume that enters. While this volume reduction can appear to improve pollutant removal on a concentration basis, it does not necessarily reflect true pollutant mass removal — and can actually mask concentration increases occurring within the system.

3. Monitoring Findings — STAs With Imported Organic Soils

Environmental Research & Design, Inc. (ERD) conducted long-term performance monitoring of five STA systems, each of which had imported organic muck soils at construction to enhance vegetation establishment. The findings were consistent and concerning:

  • All five systems exhibited a net loss of runoff volume between inflow and outflow — consistent with the expected evapotranspiration effect.
  • Despite volumetric reduction, nutrient concentrations increased between the inflow and outflow at all sites, indicating that the systems were releasing nutrients into the water rather than removing them.

Mass Removal Performance Summary

When evaluated on the basis of pollutant mass (rather than concentration), the five monitored STAs produced the following range of results:

  • 1 site — Net removal of Total Nitrogen (TN), but net export of Total Phosphorus (TP)
  • 2 sites — Net export of both TN and TP
  • 2 sites — Net retention of both TN and TP, with average mass removal rates of approximately:
    • TN: ~25%
    • TP: ~45%
Critical Finding Of five monitored STAs constructed with imported organic muck soils, only two demonstrated any net mass removal of nutrients, and only at modest removal rates (25% TN, 45% TP). The remaining three systems functioned as net nutrient sources.

Expanded ERD Monitoring Program

ERD subsequently conducted long-term monitoring across four additional STA systems. The results from this expanded dataset reinforced the earlier findings:

  • None of the four sites exhibited statistically significant nutrient removal.
  • Three of the four sites had a net export of TP.
  • Two of the four sites had a net export of TN.

Runoff concentrations measured at the outfall of these systems were found to have increased during their transit through the STA for the following parameters:

  • Total Nitrogen (TN)
  • Soluble Reactive Phosphorus (SRP) and Total Phosphorus (TP)
  • Color

4. Observed Mass Export Data

Detailed monitoring at one representative STA — a shallow pond constructed with approximately one foot of imported organic soils — documented substantial mass export of multiple pollutant parameters over an approximately one-year monitoring period. The system, initially planted with emergent vegetation, rapidly transitioned to a dense mixed-vegetation community that attracted waterfowl, adding an additional nutrient loading pathway.

The concentration data for these same parameters showed increases from inflow to outflow, confirming that the apparent low mass export is an artifact of water volume loss rather than genuine biological uptake and retention.

5. The Organic Soil Problem

The core mechanism underlying poor STA performance in systems constructed with imported muck soils is straightforward: organic soils act as a continuous internal nutrient source that releases both TN and TP at rates that substantially exceed the capacity of the vegetation to take up those nutrients.

The decomposition of organic matter in the muck soils drives:

  • Mineralization of organically bound nitrogen, generating ammonium and subsequently nitrate that leaches into the water column.
  • Release of phosphorus previously sorbed to or incorporated in organic matter, increasing soluble reactive phosphorus concentrations in the water column.
  • Generation of organic color compounds and particulate matter as organic soil particles are resuspended or transported through the system.
Key Principle The nutrient release rate from decomposing organic soils consistently exceeds the uptake rate achievable by the planted vegetation, resulting in a net export condition for both nitrogen and phosphorus from the STA system as a whole.

The addition of organic soils was originally justified on the grounds that it would accelerate vegetation establishment and thus shorten the time before the STA reached design treatment performance. In practice, the nutrient release from the soils not only negated any treatment benefit but actively degraded the quality of the stormwater being treated during transit through the system.

This finding represents a fundamental design flaw in a significant number of constructed STAs and underscores the need for careful consideration of substrate selection in any new STA design.

6. STA Design Recommendations

Based on the ERD monitoring data and analysis of multiple STA systems, the following design recommendations are provided for the construction of vegetated stormwater treatment areas:

  • Eliminate the import of organic soils. Under no circumstances should muck, peat, or other organic soils be imported and placed in the STA basin as a planting substrate. The documented nutrient release from these materials consistently overwhelms plant uptake capacity and produces a net pollutant export condition.
  • Allow vegetation to establish on existing native soils. Aquatic and emergent macrophytes are capable of colonizing and establishing in native mineral soils. The process is slower and requires a longer establishment period to achieve complete vegetation coverage, but it avoids the internal nutrient loading problem associated with organic substrate additions.
Design Tradeoffs Using native soils as the STA substrate will result in a slower vegetation establishment rate and a longer time to reach full canopy coverage. However, this tradeoff is essential to achieving any meaningful nutrient removal performance. A fully vegetated STA constructed without supplemental organic soils will outperform a rapidly established STA that exports nutrients from its organic substrate.

Additional design considerations that follow from the monitoring record include:

  • Manage inflow hydraulics to minimize resuspension of bottom materials.
  • Design outlet structures to allow drawdown and vegetation management without introducing excessive turbidity.
  • Evaluate the potential for waterfowl colonization, which can introduce external nutrient loads through fecal deposition and can disturb vegetation structure; design accordingly to discourage large waterfowl concentrations within active treatment cells.

7. Summary

Vegetated Stormwater Treatment Areas are a conceptually appealing stormwater BMP that leverages the nutrient uptake capacity of wetland vegetation and associated biofilm communities. However, long-term field monitoring of multiple Florida STA systems has demonstrated that systems constructed with imported organic muck soils consistently fail to achieve meaningful pollutant mass removal, and frequently function as net nutrient sources.

The primary cause of failure is the internal release of nitrogen and phosphorus from decomposing organic soils, which exceeds the uptake capacity of the plant community. Evapotranspiration-driven volume reductions can mask this problem on a concentration basis, but mass balance analysis reveals the true performance deficit.

The key design change required to improve STA performance is straightforward: eliminate the import of organic soils and allow vegetation to establish on native mineral substrates. This will extend the establishment period but is necessary to avoid creating a system that actively degrades stormwater quality rather than improving it.
