Denitrification

Harvey H. Harper, Environmental Research & Design, Inc. January 2026

Overview

Denitrification is a microbially-mediated process that converts oxidized forms of nitrogen — primarily nitrate (NO₃⁻) and nitrite (NO₂⁻) — into nitrogen gas (N₂), effectively removing reactive nitrogen from the water column. In wastewater treatment plants, denitrification is a well-understood and routinely engineered process. Its application to urban stormwater management, however, is considerably more constrained, and understanding these limitations is essential for sound stormwater BMP design.

This module examines the biochemical requirements for denitrification, the kinetics governing its rate, the specific challenges posed by urban stormwater chemistry, and the engineered systems that have been developed to exploit or promote denitrification as part of a BMP treatment train.

Learning Objectives

Upon completing this module, the learner will be able to:

  • Describe the biochemical process of denitrification and the four essential requirements for it to occur.
  • Explain why urban stormwater runoff presents challenges for effective denitrification.
  • Identify the nitrate concentration thresholds that govern whether denitrification is feasible for a given source water.
  • Understand the first-order reaction kinetics of denitrification and their design implications.
  • Compare the major engineered denitrification system types and their applicability in a stormwater context.
  • Recognize the pre-design verification steps required before committing to a denitrification-based BMP.

What Is Denitrification?

Denitrification is the biological reduction of nitrogen oxides (collectively referred to as NOₓ) to molecular nitrogen gas under anaerobic or anoxic conditions. Facultative heterotrophic bacteria perform this reduction as an alternative to aerobic respiration when dissolved oxygen is depleted, using nitrate as a terminal electron acceptor instead of oxygen. The overall reaction sequence is:

NO₃⁻ → NO₂⁻ → NO → N₂O → N₂ (gas)

Each step is catalyzed by a specific reductase enzyme. The end product — dinitrogen gas — escapes to the atmosphere, permanently removing the nitrogen from the aquatic system. This makes denitrification fundamentally different from biological nitrogen uptake by plants or algae, which merely sequesters nitrogen into biomass that may later decompose and release nitrogen back into the water.

In wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs), denitrification is engineered in dedicated anoxic zones with controlled addition of simple carbon sources such as methanol or acetic acid to drive the reaction predictably. In natural and engineered stormwater systems, conditions are far less controlled, making the process intermittent and often rate-limited.

Denitrification Requirements

Four conditions must be simultaneously satisfied for denitrification to proceed at a meaningful rate:

1. Degradable Carbon Source

Denitrifying bacteria require an electron donor — a source of biodegradable organic carbon — to reduce nitrate. The carbon source must be readily degradable, quantified as biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) rather than total organic carbon (TOC). Simple organic compounds such as methanol, acetate, and glucose are readily utilized; complex refractory organics such as humic acids (which give water its color) are not.

  • WWTPs supply controlled quantities of simple organics (methanol, acetic acid) to ensure adequate carbon availability.
  • Urban stormwater runoff generally contains low BOD, limiting the available electron donor and therefore the maximum rate of denitrification.
  • High-color water sources (dominated by humic acids) contribute TOC that is poorly available for denitrification, compounding the carbon limitation.
Design Implication

Where denitrification is intended in a stormwater BMP, an auxiliary carbon source — such as woodchips, corncob, or another lignocellulosic material — must be incorporated to compensate for the low BOD of urban runoff.

2. Reduced Anoxic Environment

Denitrification is an anaerobic respiratory process. Dissolved oxygen (DO) suppresses denitrification because aerobic respiration is thermodynamically favored over nitrate reduction. For significant denitrification to occur, the system must achieve and maintain reducing conditions characterized by a minimum redox potential (Eh) of −100 to −200 mV.

In practice, this requires either permanent saturation (as in a submerged filter or the internal water storage zone of a modified bioretention cell) or sufficiently long hydraulic residence times under sealed or saturated conditions that dissolved oxygen is consumed prior to the onset of denitrification.

3. Significant Nitrate Source

Perhaps the most critical and frequently overlooked requirement in the stormwater context: the source water must contain sufficient nitrate to sustain meaningful denitrification.

Optimum NO₃⁻ for Denitrification
280 – 840µg/L
Concentration Below Which Denitrification Essentially Stops
< 100µg/L NOₓ

Urban stormwater runoff often does not contain sufficient nitrate to sustain meaningful denitrification. Data from ERD monitoring of four denitrification bed installations found that three of the four sites had insufficient NOₓ concentrations for significant denitrification to occur. This is a critical finding: the presence of significant NOₓ concentrations in the source water must be verified through site-specific water quality sampling prior to committing to a denitrification-based BMP design.

Critical Pre-Design Requirement

The presence of significant NOₓ concentrations in the intended source water must be verified through site-specific sampling and analysis before designing any denitrification system. Assuming adequate nitrate without data has led to non-functional installations.

4. Proper Environmental Conditions

Denitrification is sensitive to pH and temperature:

pH

Denitrifying bacteria have an optimum pH range of 7.0 – 8.5. Activity declines sharply below pH 6.0 and above pH 9.0. This is an important consideration for Florida stormwater, where drainage from natural upland areas with organic soils can produce acidic, high-color water with pH values in the range of 4.5 – 6.0 — conditions that are directly contraindicated for denitrification.

Temperature

The optimum temperature range for denitrification is 5 – 30°C, with rates declining substantially at temperatures below 5°C. In Florida's subtropical climate, low temperatures are generally not a limiting factor; however, elevated summer temperatures can stress microbial communities if they approach or exceed the upper bound.

Water-Based Environment

Denitrification requires a saturated, water-based environment. Intermittent wetting and drying of soils between storm events — characteristic of conventional stormwater BMPs — creates aerobic conditions during dry periods that must be overcome before denitrification can resume in the subsequent storm. This transition period reduces effective denitrification efficiency in intermittently loaded systems.

Contraindicated Conditions

Conditions that render denitrification ineffective or impractical include:

  • High-color water with low pH — Humic acid-dominated water (common in Florida wetland and upland drainages) combines poor carbon quality with acidic pH, precluding effective denitrification on both counts.
  • Sources with low nitrate concentrations — As discussed above, the absence of adequate NOₓ is the single most common reason that designed denitrification systems fail to perform.

Reaction Kinetics

Understanding the kinetics of denitrification is essential for proper system sizing and realistic performance expectations.

First-Order Reaction Behavior

Denitrification follows first-order reaction kinetics with respect to nitrate concentration. This means the rate of nitrate removal is proportional to the instantaneous nitrate concentration: as concentration decreases, so does the reaction rate — logarithmically. This has two important design consequences:

  • Achieving very high percentage removals (e.g., >90%) requires disproportionately long hydraulic residence times compared to moderate removals.
  • At low incoming nitrate concentrations, the system operates far from its theoretical maximum rate throughout the entire treatment cycle, further reducing efficiency.

Reaction as an Electron Exchange

Denitrification is fundamentally a redox (oxidation-reduction) reaction. Nitrate is the oxidizing agent (electron acceptor), and the organic carbon source is the reducing agent (electron donor). The availability of either can limit the overall reaction rate. Carbon limitation is common in urban stormwater systems, while nitrate limitation is the dominant constraint at most Florida stormwater sites based on ERD monitoring data.

Process Rate and Duration

Denitrification is a relatively slow biological process. Under typical environmental conditions, approximately 90% completion is reached in 3–4 days at optimum substrate concentrations and temperatures. This is a critical sizing parameter for passive denitrification systems that rely on hydraulic residence time rather than mechanical aeration or mixing.

Key Parameter Summary

Nitrate concentration is the single most important factor regulating denitrification rate. Optimum performance occurs at NO₃⁻ concentrations of 280–840 µg/L. Below 100 µg/L NOₓ, the process essentially stops. Approximately 90% completion in 3–4 days under optimal conditions.

Urban Stormwater Context

The characteristics of urban stormwater runoff create several challenges for effective denitrification that distinguish it from municipal wastewater treatment applications.

Nitrogen Species in Urban Runoff

Nitrogen in urban runoff exists in multiple forms: organic nitrogen, ammonium (NH₄⁺), nitrite (NO₂⁻), and nitrate (NO₃⁻). Total nitrogen in urban runoff is dominated by organic nitrogen and ammonium (Total Kjeldahl Nitrogen, or TKN), with nitrate typically comprising a smaller fraction. National median event mean concentrations for nitrate-nitrite (NO₂⁺NO₃) in urban runoff range from approximately 543 µg/L in open/non-urban areas to 736 µg/L in residential land uses, and these values are often well below the optimum denitrification range when seasonal variability is accounted for. Low BOD in Stormwater

While the BOD values in Table 18-1 might appear adequate, they represent median event mean concentrations that include a wide range of variability. More critically, the nitrogen species available for denitrification (NOₓ) represents only a fraction of total nitrogen in stormwater, and low BOD limits the available electron donor for the denitrifying bacteria. Urban runoff BOD is generally insufficient to sustain denitrification without supplemental carbon addition.

Nitrogen Removal Pathways in BMPs

Nitrogen can be controlled in stormwater BMPs through alternating aerobic and anaerobic conditions, maintenance of neutral pH, low toxicant concentrations, and biological uptake. In conventional wet detention ponds, the dominant nitrogen removal pathways are sedimentation of particulate nitrogen, biological uptake into littoral vegetation, and natural denitrification occurring in the sediment-water interface under anaerobic conditions in bottom sediments. These natural denitrification processes are generally limited by the same constraints described above. SFWMD BMP Manual, 2002

Engineered Denitrification Systems

Where source water characterization confirms adequate NOₓ concentrations, several engineered system types are available. Each addresses the fundamental requirements of denitrification differently.

Deep Bed Denitrification Filters

Deep bed denitrification filters are engineered structures most commonly used in advanced wastewater treatment applications, particularly at municipal WWTPs where effluent polishing to low total nitrogen limits is required. The system consists of a deep bed of granular filter media through which clarified, nitrified effluent is passed upflow or downflow under anoxic conditions, with continuous or intermittent dosing of a supplemental carbon source to drive the denitrification reaction.

Key components of a deep bed denitrification filter include:

  • Filter media — granular material (typically sand or anthracite) that supports a biofilm of denitrifying bacteria.
  • Support gravel — coarser material beneath the media providing structural support and hydraulic distribution.
  • Underdrain system — collects treated effluent and distributes backwash water.
  • Backwash (BW) air header and laterals — supply air for periodic air-scour backwashing to remove accumulated solids and restore hydraulic capacity.
  • Sump and cover plate — collects backwash waste and provides structural base.

While deep bed filters are highly effective for tertiary nitrogen removal in WWTPs, their application to urban stormwater is limited by the typically insufficient NOₓ concentrations in stormwater, high capital cost, requirement for continuous operation, and need for chemical carbon addition and backwashing infrastructure.

Woodchip Denitrifying Bioreactors

Denitrifying woodchip bioreactors represent a passive, low-input alternative to conventional engineered denitrification systems. These systems use lignocellulosic wood chips as both a physical support matrix for denitrifying bacteria and as a slow-release organic carbon (electron donor) source. The wood chips provide a long-term, consistent supply of dissolved organic carbon through slow hydrolysis and degradation, eliminating the need for liquid chemical feed systems.

Common configurations include:

  • Denitrification walls — subsurface reactive barriers that intercept shallow groundwater.
  • In-line reactor beds — basins through which drainage water is piped, typically serving agricultural tile drainage or municipal outfalls.
  • Modified bioretention cells — conventional bioretention systems modified to include an internal water storage zone (IWSZ) saturated with woodchips.
  • Streambed bioreactors — installed in or adjacent to drainage channels.

Research has demonstrated successful nitrogen removal in field-scale woodchip bioreactor systems for periods of up to 15 years, even under fluctuating influent nitrate concentrations and variable flow rates. The woodchip substrate tolerates the highly variable hydraulic loading characteristic of stormwater systems better than liquid carbon dosing systems. Cost estimates for agricultural applications have been reported at approximately $10–15 per acre per year when capital costs are amortized over a 10–15 year system life.

Carbon Source Advantage

The use of a solid organic substrate such as woodchips eliminates the need for a liquid chemical feed system for organic carbon addition, reducing operational complexity and the risk of over- or under-dosing that is inherent in metered methanol or acetate addition.

Modified Bioretention Systems

Conventional bioretention systems (rain gardens and biofiltration cells) are not inherently designed for denitrification. In fact, conventional systems with free-draining media may exhibit net nitrate leaching as ammonium is nitrified in the aerobic media and the resulting nitrate passes through without reduction. Modification to include an internal water storage zone (IWSZ) creates the sustained anoxic conditions necessary for denitrification.

A modified bioretention cell designed for denitrification typically consists of the following zones from top to bottom:

  • Surface ponding zone (aerobic, stormwater storage)
  • Mulch layer (aerobic, particulate capture)
  • Topsoil/engineered media layer (aerobic, nitrification)
  • Nitrification zone (transition)
  • Denitrification IWSZ (permanently saturated, woodchip-amended, anoxic)
  • Drainage layer

Studies have shown that the addition of woodchips and biochar to the IWSZ can achieve nitrate removal efficiencies of 47–74% in modified systems, compared to net nitrate leaching in conventional sand bioretention systems. Research on microbial communities in BMP soils has further demonstrated that metabolically active denitrifiers are present across multiple BMP types — including surface sand filters — suggesting that denitrification can occur opportunistically wherever anoxic microsites develop, even in systems not explicitly designed for it.

Florida Regulatory Context

Florida's Environmental Resource Permit (ERP) program, administered under Chapter 62-330, F.A.C., establishes stormwater treatment criteria applicable to all regulated SWM system designs. The Applicant's Handbook Volume II (SFWMD), effective June 28, 2024, provides specific design and performance criteria for SWM systems within the South Florida Water Management District. SFWMD AH Vol. II, 2024

Denitrification is not explicitly required by Florida's stormwater treatment standards, which are generally expressed as volumetric retention/detention criteria or pollutant loading limits rather than as specific process requirements. However, denitrification systems may be proposed as alternative or supplemental treatment approaches under the alternative design provisions of the ERP program, provided the applicant demonstrates through plans, test results, or monitoring data that the alternative meets the conditions for issuance set forth in Rules 62-330.301 and 62-330.302, F.A.C. SFWMD AH Vol. II, 2024

For projects discharging to impaired water bodies or Outstanding Florida Waters (OFWs), enhanced treatment requirements may create the need for advanced nitrogen removal strategies, potentially including engineered denitrification as part of a comprehensive BMP treatment train.

Regulatory Pathway

Applicants proposing denitrification-based systems should document source water NOₓ concentrations, BOD availability, and system hydraulic residence time calculations as part of the reasonable assurance demonstration required for alternative design approval under the ERP program.

Design Considerations Summary

The following considerations should be addressed in any project evaluating denitrification as a stormwater treatment strategy:

BOD / degradable carbon Adequate labile carbon for electron donor supply Supplement with woodchips, corncob, or other solid carbon media in the anoxic zone

ERD Field Experience

Of four denitrification bed installations monitored by Environmental Research & Design, Inc., three had insufficient NOₓ concentrations for denitrification to occur to any significant extent. This 75% failure rate underscores the critical importance of pre-design water quality verification before committing to denitrification-based treatment systems for urban stormwater applications.
