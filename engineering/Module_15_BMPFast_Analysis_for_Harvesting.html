What Is Harvesting and Why Do It?

Definition

Harvesting is the collection and reuse of retained stormwater for non-potable purposes. Typical applications include: irrigation, car washing, cooling tower make-up water, toilet flushing, wetland enhancement, and similar non-drinking uses.

Reasons to Harvest Stormwater

  1. Obtain an Environmental Resource Permit (ERP) or estimate Basin Management Action Plan (BMAP) credit.
  2. Reduce stormwater pollutant loads discharged to surface waters.
  3. Protect and maintain groundwater resources.
  4. Lower the cost of potable water supply.
  5. Reduce saltwater intrusion in coastal areas.
  6. Lower the overall cost of stormwater management (harvested water can be a revenue source).
  7. Achieve some or all of the above simultaneously.
Key Concept

The term harvesting is preferred over "reuse" to avoid confusion with treated wastewater reuse programs, which are subject to different regulatory requirements and treatment standards.

Rainwater Harvesting vs. Stormwater Harvesting

Rainwater Harvesting

Rainwater harvesting effectiveness is estimated based on the potential for daily use of the collected water. It is applied to catchment areas where runoff can be fully contained with no significant groundwater interaction. A typical example is a building rooftop draining to a cistern. No credit is given for pollutant concentration reduction under this approach.

Stormwater Harvesting

Stormwater harvesting effectiveness is estimated based on the potential for use of the collected water approximately two times per week. It applies to catchment areas that are influenced by both groundwater and rainfall, such as surface drainage areas discharging to a wet detention pond. When stormwater harvesting is combined with a wet detention pond, credit for concentration reduction from the wet detention is included in the analysis.

Characteristic Rainwater Harvesting Stormwater Harvesting
Typical collection system Rooftop cistern Wet detention pond / surface drainage
Groundwater interaction None assumed Groundwater and rainfall influence
Basis for use rate estimation Daily use potential ~2 times per week use potential
Concentration reduction credit No Yes (when combined with wet detention)

Florida Case Examples

Over 700 stormwater harvesting systems have been documented in Florida. The following examples illustrate the range of applications, scales, and economic benefits achievable.

1. South Bay Utilities — Coastal Residential Irrigation

This upscale residential community implemented stormwater harvesting for irrigation demand. Key project characteristics include:

  • Not subject to FPSC (Florida Public Service Commission) regulation.
  • Delivered water cost of $0.50 per 1,000 gallons (2015 estimate).
  • Customer agreements established with a 900-home Homeowners Association (HOA).
  • Located in a coastal, environmentally fragile resource area — harvesting reduces demand on the local aquifer and reduces saltwater intrusion risk.

2. Winter Park, Florida — Regional Pond Retrofit

A regional stormwater pond serving both roadway and commercial property was retrofitted for water harvesting. The system collects runoff water, controls pollution through wet detention, and delivers harvested water for lawn irrigation. The community saved both potable water and money, while replacing potable water that previously supplied irrigation.

3. Southeast Central Florida — Large-Scale Agricultural Irrigation

A large-scale harvesting operation covering approximately 550,000 acres supplies irrigation water through a network of canals, ponds, and wells. Key project data:

  • Subject to FPSC (Exclusive) regulation.
  • Historical cost: $0.192 per 1,000 gallons (2004); estimated $0.89 per 1,000 gallons in 2024.
  • Infrastructure: canals, ponds, and horizontal subsurface wells.
  • Capacity: 20 MGD (million gallons per day), expandable.

4. Golf Course and I-4 Stormwater Management Plan

This example demonstrates a multi-stakeholder win-win arrangement involving FDOT, a local high school, and a public golf course:

  • Plan objective: Provide irrigation water to a golf course to improve playability and course appearance.
  • FDOT role: Supplied the harvested stormwater from highway drainage.
  • Result: FDOT and the high school both meet stormwater regulations; citizens gained a new golf course; all parties benefit from stormwater treatment and conservation of potable water that would otherwise be used for irrigation.
  • Environmental benefit: The Wekiva River is protected from increased stormwater pollutant loads.

5. City of Miramar — Broward County Mixed-Use System

A municipal stormwater supply system combining reclaimed water and harvested stormwater for Irrigation Quality (IQ) supply:

  • Estimated capital cost: $1.3 million (2014).
  • System capacity: 1 MGD.
  • Water type: blended reclaimed and harvested water, delivered as IQ (Irrigation Quality).
  • Delivered cost: $0.80 per 1,000 gallons (2014 estimate).
  • Note: When harvested water is mixed with reclaimed water for contact applications, chlorination must be maintained to preserve residual disinfection.
IQ Water Note

IQ stands for Irrigation Quality — water suitable for non-potable irrigation and similar uses. When harvested water is blended with reclaimed water and may come into contact with people, the blended water must be chlorinated to maintain a residual disinfectant concentration.

Annual Removal Curves — REV Curves for Stormwater Harvesting

REV curves are the primary graphical tool used in BMPFast to quantify the harvesting efficiency of a stormwater system. The three axes represent:

  • R — the Y-axis: average daily Rate of use (inches/day over Effective Impervious Area, EIA).
  • E — the curve label: annual removal (retention) Efficiency (%).
  • V — the X-axis: Volume of storage for harvesting (inches over EIA).
Key Relationship

For a given storage volume (V) and use rate (R), the REV curves yield the expected annual pollutant removal efficiency (E). When stormwater harvesting is combined with wet detention, a separate concentration reduction credit is added to the harvesting efficiency.

Source: Applicant's Handbook Vol. 1, Appendix O, Table 1.

REV curves are provided for Florida's five meteorological zones. Zone selection is based on project location; each zone reflects regional differences in rainfall patterns that affect harvesting efficiency.

Each graph plots Use Rate (inches/day over EIA) on the Y-axis (0.00–0.50) versus Runoff Volume of Water (inches over EIA) on the X-axis (0–6), with efficiency contour lines at 20%, 30%, 40%, 50%, 60%, 70%, 80%, and 90%. Example Problem — Redevelopment with Wet Detention and Harvesting

Problem Statement

A redevelopment project (increase in directly connected impervious area) involves a 2-acre site classified as light industrial in meteorological zone 4. The municipality requires that runoff be used for irrigation on a 0.93-acre plot of land within the project. Conditions:

  • Curve Number (CN): 60
  • Pre-development DCIA: 20%
  • Post-development DCIA: 32%
  • Wet pond area: 0.28 ac (loading from this area is excluded from catchment loading)
  • No littoral zone in the wet pond
  • Approved harvest volume: 0.25 ac-ft
  • Irrigation rate: 1 inch per week (average yearly)
  • Mean annual rainfall: 52 inches (Zone 4)

Question: What BMP plan meets the redevelopment performance standard?

CUP and WUP Permit Analysis

BMPFast harvesting analysis can also support Consumptive Use Permit (CUP) or Water Use Permit (WUP) applications, which are issued by Florida's Water Management Districts. These permits govern the quantity of water that may be withdrawn from a water source for beneficial use.

Relevant regulatory sources include: SFWMD Applicant's Handbook for Water Use Permit Applications (June 13, 2022), SWFWMD (WUP) guidelines (May 2014), and SJRWMD guidelines (August 2014).

Practical Implication

Scenario A (ROC = 0.8) has a much higher runoff coefficient, producing nearly sufficient harvested supply (12.83 ac-ft/yr) to meet demand (13.13 ac-ft/yr) with only 0.29 ac-ft/yr supplemental water needed. Scenario B (ROC = 0.325) has a lower runoff coefficient and generates substantially less supply (7.673 ac-ft/yr), requiring 5.458 ac-ft/yr of supplemental water. This illustrates how impervious cover and runoff coefficient directly impact harvesting feasibility.

REV Curve Interpretation — Zone 2 Examples

REV curves provide a rapid graphical method for estimating harvesting efficiency. Two cases for Zone 2 illustrate the impact of the runoff coefficient (ROC) on annual harvesting efficiency:

Case 1: ROC = 0.80 → 61% Annual Harvesting Efficiency

With a high runoff coefficient of 0.80, the system operates on the REV curve at the 61% efficiency contour. This reflects a relatively high ratio of runoff supply to storage volume, meaning much of the runoff is captured and used before overflowing.

Case 2: ROC = 0.325 → 90% Annual Harvesting Efficiency

With a lower runoff coefficient of 0.325, the system captures nearly all available runoff — the curve plot intersects the 90% efficiency contour. Less total runoff is generated, but the harvest system retains almost all of it, resulting in higher efficiency.

Key Insight

A lower runoff coefficient does not necessarily mean less benefit — it can mean higher harvesting efficiency because all generated runoff is captured and used. The critical design question is whether the absolute volume of harvested water meets the demand, even if the efficiency percentage is high.

Module Summary — What Did We Learn?

Key Takeaways

  1. Harvesting defined: Rainwater harvesting applies to fully contained catchments with no groundwater interaction; stormwater harvesting applies to surface drainage catchments with groundwater influence. Only stormwater harvesting receives concentration reduction credit when combined with wet detention.
  2. Software navigation: BMPFast processes harvesting analysis through the standard six-step workflow — Catchment entry, Treatment entry, Catchment configuration, and reporting.
  3. Annual mass removal analysis: The harvesting worksheet calculates treatment efficiency from REV curve data based on irrigation area, available harvest volume, and weekly harvest rate.
  4. REV curve discovery: The Plot REV function in BMPFast allows engineers to interactively explore how changes in storage volume and use rate affect harvesting efficiency for the selected meteorological zone.
  5. Water Use Permit support: BMPFast output — including annual supply, demand, and supplemental water requirements — directly supports CUP/WUP permit applications to Florida Water Management Districts.
  6. Example problem result: A 2-acre light industrial redevelopment in Zone 4, combined wet detention (0.23 ac-ft permanent pool) and stormwater harvesting (0.25 ac-ft harvest volume, 1 in/week), achieved 68.13% nitrogen removal (required 45%) and 80.29% phosphorus removal (required 80%), meeting all redevelopment performance standards.
