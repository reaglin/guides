Section 01 — Foundation

Florida Stormwater Rule History Chapter 62-25 — Regulations of Stormwater Discharge

Florida's first stormwater regulation — Chapter 62-25, Regulations of Stormwater Discharge — was adopted in 1981, making Florida the first state in the United States to enact such a rule. It became effective in February 1982 and remained the primary regulatory framework until its repeal in 2017.

Chapter 62-25 was a technology-based rule built around four key components:

Performance Standard — Minimum Level of Treatment

The rule established a performance standard requiring a reduction in the average annual post-development stormwater pollutant loading of Total Suspended Solids (TSS). Discharges from standard stormwater systems were required to reduce TSS loading by 80%. For stormwater discharges directed to Outstanding Florida Waters (OFWs), the required reduction was raised to 95%.

Design Criteria

Specific design criteria were established to enable practitioners to achieve the required levels of treatment. These criteria defined the physical parameters that treatment systems must meet.

Rebuttable Presumption

The rule established a rebuttable presumption: stormwater systems designed in accordance with the specified criteria were presumed not to cause harm to water resources. This presumption could be rebutted if evidence demonstrated otherwise.

Periodic Review and Updating of BMPs

Recognizing the evolving nature of stormwater science, the rule included a provision for the periodic review and updating of Best Management Practices (BMPs) based on ongoing research.

Section 02 — Water Resource Implementation

Florida Water Resource Implementation Rule Chapter 62-40 Florida Administrative Code

Chapter 62-40 of the Florida Administrative Code (FAC) contains the Water Resource Implementation Rule, with key provisions governing stormwater management becoming effective on May 7, 2005.

Section 62-40.431 — Stormwater Management Program

Section 3(a) — FDEP Role

The Florida Department of Environmental Protection (FDEP) is responsible for coordinating the statewide stormwater management program by establishing goals, objectives, and guidance for the development and implementation of stormwater management programs by the Water Management Districts and local governments.

Section 3(b) — District Role

The Water Management Districts (WMDs) shall be the chief administrators of the state stormwater management program. FDEP shall implement the state's stormwater management program in Districts that do not have the economic and technical resources to implement a comprehensive surface water management program.

Section 62-40.432 — Surface Water Management Regulation (2005)

Section 62-40.432 directed FDEP and the Districts, pursuant to Section 373.418, F.S., to specify design and performance criteria for new stormwater management systems when adopting rules. The required performance criteria are:

Criterion (1)

Achieve at least 80 percent reduction of the average annual load of pollutants that would cause or contribute to violations of state water quality standards.

Criterion (2)

Achieve at least 95 percent reduction of the average annual load of pollutants that would cause or contribute to violations of state water quality standards in Outstanding Florida Waters.

Section 03 — District-Level Implementation

District Implementation and Observed Shortcomings

While FDEP provides overarching guidance to the Water Management Districts for treatment systems intended to meet the state performance objectives, each individual District is responsible for developing its own specific design criteria for stormwater BMPs. This decentralized framework produced significant variation across the state:

  • Every District developed a different set of design standards.
  • Design criteria vary widely throughout Florida.
  • Performance efficiencies achieved by implemented systems also vary widely.

In keeping with the rebuttable presumption established under Chapter 62-25, discharges from systems meeting the District-specific design criteria were presumed to comply with state water quality standards. However, research conducted by Environmental Research & Design, Inc. (ERD) and other investigators indicated that current designs were, in many cases, not achieving the required pollutant load reductions mandated by state rules.

Section 04 — Regulatory Research

Evaluation of Stormwater Effectiveness FDEP Agreement S0108 — 2006

In response to evidence that existing design criteria were failing to achieve required performance, FDEP issued a contract to ERD in 2006 to evaluate current stormwater design criteria across the state. The work was performed under FDEP Agreement S0108, formally titled "Evaluation of Current Stormwater Design Criteria within the State of Florida."

Scope of Work

The study scope addressed the following objectives:

  • Determine whether current stormwater design criteria meet the performance standards outlined in Chapter 62-40.432 FAC — Surface Water Management Regulation.
  • If design criteria are found to be deficient, recommend changes to meet the required performance criteria.
  • Evaluate design criteria sufficient to achieve no net increase in post-development pollutant loadings relative to pre-development conditions.
  • Conduct analysis for nitrogen and phosphorus as primary indicator pollutants.
    • If performance criteria are met for nitrogen and phosphorus, they will be met for other significant pollutants including BOD, TSS, and heavy metals.

Section 05 — Methodology and Outcomes

Study Objectives and Outcomes June 2007 Final Report

A key objective of the study was to develop scientifically defensible and reproducible design methodologies using proven approaches already familiar to practicing design engineers.

Scope Exclusions

The following alternative stormwater management techniques were explicitly outside the scope of this study:

  • Low Impact Design (LID)
  • Stormwater Reuse
  • Street Sweeping
  • Pervious Pavement
  • Gross Pollutant Separators
  • Evaluation of wetland loadings

Study Results — June 2007 Report

The final results were delivered in a June 2007 report. The report provided a comprehensive series of tables, figures, calculation methods, and design examples for achieving the following performance levels:

  • 80% pollutant removal
  • 95% pollutant removal
  • No net increase — pre-development vs. post-development loading parity

The methodology was vetted through a series of Public Workshops attended by the Water Management Districts and other interested parties. The Districts subsequently adopted the report as the standard method for calculating pollutant load reductions for use in pre- versus post-development analyses.

The 2007 report forms the technical foundation for the loading calculations and removal efficiency values that underpin both the new Statewide Stormwater Rule and the BMPTrains design tool.

Section 06 — Legislative History

Statewide Stormwater Rule Efforts

2010
Initial Rulemaking Effort A Technical Advisory Committee (TAC) was established and convened for 8 meetings. The TAC developed a Draft Applicant's Handbook based largely on the 2007 ERD report. However, the rule development effort and FDEP-funded supporting research were subsequently eliminated under Governor Scott's administration.
2020
Clean Waterways Act Approved during the 2020 Legislative session and signed by the Governor on June 30, 2020. The Act directed FDEP to initiate rulemaking by January 1, 2021. Key mandates included:
  • Update the Environmental Resource Permit Applicant's Handbook using the most recent scientific information available.
  • Consider and address low-impact BMPs.
  • Develop measures for consistent application of the net improvement performance standard.
2020–21
TAC Rule Development Workshops A new TAC was convened for 13 meetings between December 2020 and November 2021. Topics addressed included BMP efficiencies, maintenance requirements, and desired levels of treatment.
Mar 2022
FDEP Final Report FDEP issued a Final Report including specific recommendations for rule revisions. The Applicant's Handbook was revised from the 2010 draft version.
Jun 2024
Senate Bill 7040 — Signed into Law On June 28, 2024, Governor Ron DeSantis signed Senate Bill 7040 into law, formally updating Florida's stormwater rules and design criteria. The bill represents the most significant update to Florida's stormwater regulatory framework since Chapter 62-25 was adopted in 1981.

Section 07 — New Rule Requirements

Statewide Stormwater Rule — Key Provisions

Implementation Timeline

Applications for individual and general Environmental Resource Permits (ERPs) that are deemed complete after December 28, 2025 (18 months from the rule effective date) are required to meet the revised Stormwater Quality Nutrient Permitting Requirements.

Maintenance Requirements

  • Establishment of routine maintenance cost estimate requirements.
  • Certification of financial capability for ongoing system maintenance.
  • Establishment of consistent inspection requirements for the permittee based on the design of the stormwater treatment system.

Design Flexibility

  • Flexibility to use existing, or apply new, site- or regional-specific Event Mean Concentrations (EMC).
  • Permittees may choose from a range of innovative BMPs.
  • Off-site compensation may be used to meet treatment performance standards.
  • Performance criteria are applied at the project outfall.

Section 08 — Geographic Framework

Florida HUC-12 Boundary Map

The new performance standards under Senate Bill 7040 are applied based on project location within a Hydrologic Unit Code (HUC) 12 watershed boundary. The applicable standard is determined by the downstream correlation of that watershed to designated Impaired Waters or Outstanding Florida Waters (OFWs).

The HUC-12 system divides Florida's landscape into discrete drainage subbasins. Practitioners must:

  • Locate the project site on the HUC-12 boundary map with Impaired Waters and OFW overlays.
  • Determine whether the project's HUC-12 watershed has a downstream connection to Impaired Waters and/or OFWs.
  • Select the appropriate performance standard from Table 1 based on this determination.

Section 09 — Permitting Documents

Environmental Resource Permit Applicant's Handbook Volume I — General and Environmental

FDEP developed Volume I of the Environmental Resource Permit (ERP) Applicant's Handbook, effective June 28, 2024. This volume applies across all five Water Management Districts (WMDs) and is incorporated by reference into subsection 62-330.010(4), F.A.C.

Volume I — Key Attributes

  • Applies uniformly to each of the 5 Water Management Districts.
  • Based largely on the methodology established in the 2007 ERD report.
  • Part II, Sections 8 and 9 contain the basic evaluation and design criteria for stormwater treatment.
  • Allows "compensating treatment" approaches:
    • Overtreatment — designing a system to exceed the required performance standard, providing a buffer.
    • Off-site treatment — achieving a portion of the required load reduction at a location other than the project site.

Volume II — District-Specific Supplements

Each Water Management District developed its own Volume II supplement to address unique regional characteristics, including:

  • District-specific hydrology
  • Regional land use patterns

The five participating Water Management Districts are: Northwest Florida WMD, Suwannee River WMD, St. Johns River WMD, Southwest Florida WMD, and South Florida WMD.
