Creating Good Surveys for Software Evaluation

📋 Module Overview

Surveys are one of the most powerful tools for gathering user feedback on software. A well-designed survey yields actionable data that directly informs design improvements. A poorly designed survey yields noise—data that tells you little or nothing useful. This guide covers the instruments, structure, presentation, and analysis needed to run surveys that actually help you improve your software.

Core Principle: Every question in your survey should be answerable with "so what do we fix?" If you can't explain what you'd change based on the answer, reconsider the question.

Definition and Purpose

A survey is a structured set of questions administered to a group of users to collect data about their experiences, opinions, and behaviors. In software evaluation, surveys let you hear from many users at once—something that interviews and usability tests alone cannot scale to.

What Surveys Are Good For

  • Measuring satisfaction: How happy are users with specific features or the overall experience?
  • Identifying problem areas: Which parts of the interface frustrate or confuse users?
  • Prioritizing improvements: Which issues matter most to users?
  • Tracking changes over time: Did a redesign actually improve the experience?
  • Reaching many users: Collecting data at scale that would be impractical to interview individually

Surveys vs. Other Evaluation Methods

Method Best For Limitation
Survey Broad data from many users; measuring satisfaction; identifying patterns Can't observe behavior; users may not articulate issues accurately
Cognitive Walkthrough Identifying task-specific learnability problems Evaluator may miss issues real users face
Heuristic Evaluation Broad usability assessment against principles Expert perspective only, no actual user data
Usability Test Deep understanding of real user behavior Time-intensive; small sample size
Interview In-depth exploration of user needs and opinions Hard to scale; subject to interviewer bias

Surveys Work Best When Combined

A common and effective approach is to use a survey after a usability test or cognitive walkthrough. The structured evaluation finds specific issues; the survey measures how widely those issues are felt across your user base and which ones users rank as most important to fix.

The Actionability Standard

The most important criterion for any survey question is whether it produces actionable data—information that directly informs a design decision.

✗ Not Actionable

"Overall, how satisfied are you with this app?"

You get a number. A 3 out of 5. Now what? You don't know what to fix, where to focus effort, or why users are dissatisfied.

✓ Actionable

"How easy was it to find the Search feature?" followed by "If finding Search was difficult, what would have helped you find it faster?"

Now you know specifically where navigation is failing and have user-generated ideas for fixing it.

✓ The Actionability Test — Ask Yourself:

  • If 80% answer "strongly disagree," do I know what to change?
  • If 80% answer "strongly agree," do I know what to keep?
  • Does this question target a specific feature, screen, or interaction?
  • Will the answer help me decide between design alternatives?
  • Can the result be linked to a specific part of the interface?

Choosing the Right Question Type

The question type you choose determines what kind of data you collect and what you can do with it. Mixing types strategically gives you both measurable data and rich explanation.

Likert Scale

Rating Agreement or Satisfaction on a Fixed Scale

What it is: A statement that users respond to on an ordered scale, typically 5 or 7 points from Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree (or equivalent anchors).

Best for: Measuring attitudes, satisfaction, and perceived usability consistently across many respondents.

Use 5 points when: Your audience is general and you want simplicity.

Use 7 points when: You need more discrimination between responses and your audience can handle nuance.

Rating Scale

Numeric or Star Ratings

What it is: Users assign a numeric score—often 1–5 or 1–10—or select a number of stars to represent quality or satisfaction.

Best for: Quick satisfaction ratings, Net Promoter Score (NPS), and collecting data that is easy to average and compare.

Limitation: Rating scales without anchors are interpreted differently by different people. A 7/10 to one user may equal a 4/10 to another.

How would you rate the Search feature?
★★★☆☆
(3 out of 5 stars selected)

How likely are you to recommend this app? (0–10)
0 1 2 7 8 9 10
Multiple Choice (Single Answer)

Select One Option from a Defined List

What it is: User selects exactly one answer from a predefined list. The options are mutually exclusive.

Best for: Categorizing users, identifying which feature was most problematic, or choosing among clearly distinct alternatives.

Always include: An "Other (please specify)" option when your list may not cover every case. Always include "None of the above" or "Not applicable" where appropriate.

Which feature did you find most difficult to use?
Search
Checkout
Account Settings
Notifications
Other (please specify)
I did not find any feature difficult
Actionability Tip: Answers directly identify where to focus design effort. If 60% select "Checkout," you know exactly where to investigate next.
Checkboxes (Multiple Answer)

Select All That Apply

What it is: User may select one or more answers. Options are not mutually exclusive.

Best for: Identifying all features used, all problems encountered, or all preferences that apply simultaneously.

Caution: Multi-select questions are harder to analyze than single-select. Report the percentage of respondents who selected each option, not the percentage of total selections.

Which of the following problems did you encounter? (Select all that apply)
Couldn't find what I was looking for
Buttons were too small to tap
Error message was confusing
App was slow
I did not encounter any problems
Ranking

Order Items by Preference or Importance

What it is: Users drag or number items to place them in order from most to least important, preferred, or frequent.

Best for: Prioritization. When you need to know which issues users want fixed first or which features they value most.

Limitation: Keep lists short (5–7 items maximum). Ranking more than 7 items becomes cognitively taxing and produces unreliable results.

Rank these improvements from most important (1) to least important (5):
1Faster search results
2Simpler checkout flow
3Larger tap targets
4Clearer error messages
5Better onboarding tutorial
Actionability Tip: Ranking is one of the most powerful tools for software teams because it directly answers: "What should we work on first?"
Open-Ended (Text Response)

Free-Text Responses in Users' Own Words

What it is: A text field where users write their own answer with no predefined options.

Best for: Capturing the "why" behind numeric scores, discovering issues you didn't anticipate, and getting users' suggestions in their own words.

Limitation: Time-consuming to analyze at scale. Not all users will complete them. Use selectively—not for every question.

Best placement: Immediately after a scale or rating question as a follow-up: "What is the main reason for your score?"

You rated the checkout experience 2 out of 5. What made it difficult?
Type your answer here...

What is the ONE change that would most improve your experience?
Type your answer here...
Gold Question: "What is the one thing you would change about this app?" Open-ended, specific enough to produce focused answers, and directly actionable. This single question often surfaces the most important design insight of the entire survey.
Semantic Differential

Rating Between Two Opposing Adjectives

What it is: Users rate where they feel the interface falls between two opposite descriptors on a 5- or 7-point scale.

Best for: Assessing personality and tone of the interface, comparing before/after redesigns, or measuring perceived qualities like complexity.

How would you describe this interface? (Circle your position)
Confusing ○ ○ ● ○ ○ Clear
Cluttered ○ ● ○ ○ ○ Minimal
Frustrating ○ ○ ○ ● ○ Satisfying

Survey Structure

How you organize a survey is nearly as important as the questions themselves. A well-structured survey reduces abandonment, reduces cognitive fatigue, and produces cleaner data.

Recommended Survey Structure

1. INTRODUCTION (5–8 lines)
- What is the survey about?
- Who is conducting it?
- How long will it take? (Be honest—overestimates cause abandonment)
- How will responses be used?
- Is it anonymous? Voluntary?

2. SCREENING / CONTEXT QUESTIONS (1–3 questions)
- Brief questions to establish user context
- How often do they use the app?
- Which features have they used?
- Do NOT ask for personal details you don't need

3. CORE EVALUATION QUESTIONS (5–12 questions)
- Specific, feature-by-feature assessment
- Ordered from general to specific
- Mix of scale, multiple choice, and ranking
- Immediately after scale questions: open-ended follow-ups

4. OVERALL ASSESSMENT (2–3 questions)
- Overall satisfaction rating
- Net Promoter Score (if relevant)
- "What one thing would you change?"

5. CLOSING (3–5 lines)
- Thank participants for their time
- Reiterate how feedback will be used
- Optional: contact info for follow-up questions

Question Ordering Principles

Funnel Pattern

Start broad and narrow down. This warms up respondents and avoids priming them before broader questions.

  1. Overall experience (broad)
  2. Feature areas (medium)
  3. Specific interactions (narrow)
  4. Open-ended details

Task-Aligned Order

Follow the natural user journey through the app. Questions match the sequence users actually encounter features.

  1. Onboarding / first launch
  2. Navigation / finding features
  3. Core task completion
  4. Error handling / recovery
  5. Overall impression

⚠️ Question Order Bias

Early questions can color how respondents answer later ones. If you ask "How satisfied are you overall?" first, users anchor on that answer for everything that follows. Always ask specific questions before overall satisfaction to get unanchored data on specifics.

Length and Completion Rate

Survey Length Typical Completion Rate Best For
1–3 minutes (3–5 questions) High (70–85%) Post-task micro surveys, satisfaction checks
5–10 minutes (8–15 questions) Moderate (50–65%) Feature evaluation, most course assignments
10–20 minutes (15–25 questions) Lower (30–45%) Deep evaluation; incentivized participants
20+ minutes Very low (under 25%) Avoid unless highly motivated audience
Practical Guideline: For evaluating a student prototype, aim for 8–12 focused questions that can be completed in 5–7 minutes. State the time estimate in your introduction and be accurate—if you say 5 minutes, design for 5 minutes.

Pitfalls That Corrupt Your Data

These mistakes don't just produce weak data—they produce actively misleading data that can send your design work in the wrong direction.

🚫 Pitfall 1: Leading Questions

Questions that suggest a desired answer, or frame the situation in a way that pushes users toward a particular response.

✗ Leading: "Our new streamlined navigation makes it easier to find features, doesn't it?"
The phrasing practically demands agreement.
✓ Neutral: "How easy was it to find the features you were looking for?"
No implied answer—users report their actual experience.

🚫 Pitfall 2: Double-Barreled Questions

A single question that asks about two separate things. Users may feel differently about each, making their answer uninterpretable.

✗ Double-barreled: "The app was easy to use and loaded quickly."
What if it was easy to use but slow to load? A user can't give an accurate single answer.
✓ Separated: "The app was easy to use." [separate question] "The app loaded quickly."
Each question has a clear, singular answer.

🚫 Pitfall 3: Ambiguous Wording

Questions where different respondents interpret the meaning differently, making answers incomparable.

✗ Ambiguous: "Do you use this app regularly?"
"Regularly" could mean daily, weekly, or monthly depending on the user.
✓ Specific: "How often do you use this app?" with options: Daily / Several times a week / Once a week / Less than once a week / This was my first time
Concrete options eliminate interpretive differences.

🚫 Pitfall 4: Loaded / Assumptive Questions

Questions that assume something is true that may not be, or that contain emotionally charged words.

✗ Assumptive: "When did you stop finding the navigation confusing?"
Assumes the user found it confusing—which may not be true.
✗ Loaded: "How frustrated were you by the slow loading times?"
Assumes frustration and assumes loading was slow. Introduces emotional priming.
✓ Neutral: "How would you describe the app's loading speed?" with scale from Very Slow to Very Fast.

🚫 Pitfall 5: Too Many Open-Ended Questions

Open text fields require significant effort from respondents. Overusing them leads to survey fatigue, terse or shallow responses, and low completion rates.

Rule of Thumb: No more than 2–3 open-ended questions in a survey. Use them strategically as follow-ups to low ratings, not as a substitute for thoughtfully designed structured questions.

🚫 Pitfall 6: Unbalanced Scales

Scales that have more positive options than negative (or vice versa) skew results before a single response is collected.

✗ Unbalanced: Very Bad / Bad / OK / Good / Very Good / Excellent
6 options with 4 positive and 2 negative—skewed toward positive responses.
✓ Balanced: Strongly Disagree / Disagree / Neutral / Agree / Strongly Agree
Equal options on each side with a neutral midpoint.
Even vs. Odd Scales: An odd-numbered scale (5-point, 7-point) includes a neutral midpoint. An even-numbered scale (4-point, 6-point) forces users to lean one way. For software evaluation, odd scales are generally preferred unless you specifically want to force a direction.

🚫 Pitfall 7: Acquiescence Bias

Respondents tend to agree with statements regardless of their true opinion—especially if questions are all worded positively.

Solution: Mix positively and negatively worded questions. If every statement is "Feature X was easy/clear/fast," users may just agree with everything. Including a reversed question ("The navigation was difficult to learn") catches respondents who are agreeing without reading carefully.
Positive framing:
"Finding a recipe was easy."
(Agree = good)

Reversed framing:
"I had trouble finding recipes."
(Agree = bad)

If a user scores both high, they're
not reading carefully — flag their
response as potentially unreliable.
Important: When you include reversed questions, remember to reverse the scores when computing averages. A "Strongly Agree" (5) on a negative statement should be treated as a 1, not a 5.

🚫 Pitfall 8: Survey Fatigue

Too many questions—or too many hard questions in a row—cause respondents to disengage. Later answers become careless or are skipped entirely.

Combating Survey Fatigue

  • Keep total questions under 15 for a standard software evaluation
  • Break long surveys into clearly labeled sections
  • Put most important questions first (completion isn't guaranteed)
  • Use simple question types (Likert, MC) before open-ended ones
  • Show a progress indicator so respondents know how much is left

🚫 Pitfall 9: Collecting Data You Can't Act On

The most common—and most costly—pitfall in software evaluation surveys. Questions that produce interesting data but no design decisions waste respondent time and dilute your actionable findings.

✗ Cannot act on:
"How long have you been using smartphone apps?"
Not Actionable
Demographic trivia—doesn't connect to a design decision.
✓ Can act on:
"Which step in the booking process was most confusing?"
Actionable
Answer directly points to where design work is needed.

Apply this test to every question before finalizing: "If 80% of users answer [X], what would we change in the design?" If you can't answer that clearly, the question doesn't belong in the survey.

Turning Responses into Design Decisions

Collecting survey data is only half the work. Systematic analysis transforms raw responses into specific, prioritized design recommendations. The goal is not to summarize the data—it is to interpret what it means for your design.

Analyzing Quantitative Data (Scale and Choice Questions)

Step 1: Calculate Descriptive Statistics

For each Likert / Rating Scale question, calculate:

• Mean (average) ← Most useful single number
• Median ← Less affected by extreme answers
• Distribution ← How many chose each option?

Example — Q3: "I was able to find recipes that matched what I was looking for."
(n = 20 responses)

Responses:
Strongly Disagree (1): 2 responses (10%)
Disagree (2): 5 responses (25%)
Neutral (3): 4 responses (20%)
Agree (4): 6 responses (30%)
Strongly Agree (5): 3 responses (15%)

Mean: 3.15
Median: 3
% Agree or Strongly Agree: 45%
% Disagree or Strongly Disagree: 35%

Interpretation:
A mean of 3.15 is close to neutral. With 35% disagreeing, this is a
clear problem area. The recipe discovery experience is not working
reliably for a significant portion of users.

Step 2: Visualize the Distribution

Averages can hide important patterns. Always look at the full distribution.

Interpreting Results Responsibly

⚠️ Common Analysis Mistakes

  • Ignoring sample size: 3 responses is not enough to draw conclusions. Be explicit about confidence levels.
  • Reporting averages without distributions: A mean of 3 could mean everyone chose 3, or half chose 1 and half chose 5. These have very different design implications.
  • Treating open-ended responses as universal: One person's strong complaint is not necessarily representative. Look for themes, not outliers.
  • Cherry-picking quotes: Always report the proportion of users who raised a theme, not just the most colorful quote.
  • Forgetting to reverse negative questions: If Q5 was reversed, a score of 4 means poor usability, not good. Failing to reverse scores will corrupt your averages.
  • Over-interpreting small differences: A mean of 3.4 vs 3.6 across 10 responses is not a meaningful difference.

✓ The Goal of Analysis

  • Connect every finding to a specific part of the interface
  • Prioritize by frequency, severity, and user-stated importance
  • Translate "users found X confusing" into "change Y to Z"
  • Note what is working — not just what is broken
  • Be honest about confidence given your sample size
  • Produce a ranked list of design changes, not just a list of problems

Survey Design Checklist

Before You Write Any Questions

☐ I know exactly what design decisions this survey will inform
☐ I have identified the 3-5 most important things to learn
☐ I know who my respondents are and their context
☐ I have chosen a tool (Google Forms, SurveyMonkey, etc.)
☐ I have planned how I will analyze the results

For Each Question You Write

☐ It asks about ONE thing (not double-barreled)
☐ The wording is neutral (not leading or loaded)
☐ The terms are unambiguous (no "regularly," "often," "good")
☐ I can answer: "If most users score this low, I will change [X]"
☐ Scale questions have equal positive/negative options
☐ Multiple choice includes "Other" and "None/N/A" where appropriate
☐ The question matches the right instrument type for what I need

Before Sending the Survey

☐ Introduction explains purpose, time, and anonymity
☐ Questions are ordered general → specific
☐ Overall rating is placed at the END
☐ Open-ended questions are used sparingly (max 2-3)
☐ Total length is under 12 questions / 8 minutes
☐ At least one reversed question is included
☐ I have piloted the survey with 1-2 people and refined it
☐ The survey link/form has been tested end-to-end
☐ Data collection is set up correctly (responses going somewhere)

After Collecting Responses

☐ Reversed questions have been rescored before averaging
☐ Means AND distributions are reported for scale questions
☐ Open-ended responses have been coded into themes
☐ Each finding is linked to a specific design element
☐ Recommendations are specific and actionable (not "improve navigation")
☐ Positive findings are included alongside problems
☐ Limitations of sample size are acknowledged
☐ Recommendations are prioritized (not a flat list)

Question Type Quick Reference

Type Use When You Need Watch Out For
Likert Scale Measuring agreement/satisfaction on a specific feature Keep statements feature-specific; always add follow-up for low scores
Rating Scale Quick scoring; NPS; overall impressions Label anchors clearly; avoid scales without endpoints defined
Multiple Choice Identifying which feature/area has the problem Include "Other" and "None" options; keep list under 7 options
Checkboxes Capturing all issues or all features used Report % of respondents, not % of selections
Ranking Prioritizing what to fix first Keep to 5-7 items maximum; harder to analyze
Open-Ended Understanding "why"; getting ideas you didn't anticipate Use sparingly; place after low-scoring questions; requires coding
Semantic Differential Assessing perceived character; before/after comparisons Keep pairs genuinely opposite; don't mix positive and negative poles

Pitfalls Quick Reference

Pitfall Quick Fix
Leading question Remove adjectives that imply a preferred answer
Double-barreled Split into two separate questions
Ambiguous wording Replace vague terms with defined options or specific timeframes
Acquiescence bias Include at least one reversed (negatively-worded) statement
Unbalanced scale Count positive and negative options — they must be equal
Survey fatigue Cut to 12 questions or fewer; show progress bar
Non-actionable question Ask: "If most answer X, what would I change in my design?"
Order bias Ask specific questions before overall satisfaction
