Topic 01
Founding Principles & the Declaration of Independence
▼

Core Founding Principles

The American republic was built on Enlightenment-era ideas drawn from philosophers like John Locke, Montesquieu, and Jean-Jacques Rousseau. These principles form the ideological bedrock of U.S. democracy.

  • Popular Sovereignty: Government authority derives from the consent of the governed — the people are the ultimate source of political power.
  • Natural Rights / Unalienable Rights: People possess rights that cannot be taken away by any government — life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.
  • Limited Government: Government power is restricted by law and a written constitution; no one is above the law.
  • Separation of Powers: Government is divided into three independent branches to prevent tyranny.
  • Checks and Balances: Each branch can limit the powers of the other two branches.
  • Republicanism: Citizens elect representatives to make laws on their behalf rather than governing directly themselves.
  • Federalism: Power is divided between the national government and state governments.
  • Individual Rights: The rights of individuals are protected even against majority rule.

The Declaration of Independence (1776)

⭐ Key Fact
The Declaration of Independence was adopted by the Second Continental Congress on July 4, 1776. Its primary author was Thomas Jefferson, with revisions by Benjamin Franklin and John Adams.

The Declaration has four main parts:

  • Preamble: States the purpose and philosophical basis — all men are created equal and possess unalienable rights.
  • Statement of Rights: Lists natural rights: life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness; government derives power from the consent of the governed.
  • List of Grievances: 27 charges against King George III justifying independence.
  • Declaration: Formally declares the colonies to be free and independent states.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the primary purpose of government according to the Declaration of Independence?
Answer: To secure the unalienable rights of the people (life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness).
📝 Typical Exam Question
Which philosopher most directly influenced the phrase "all men are created equal" in the Declaration of Independence?
Answer: John Locke — his Second Treatise of Government (1689) described natural rights of life, liberty, and property.
📝 Typical Exam Question
According to the Declaration, what right do the people have when a government becomes destructive of these rights?
Answer: The right to alter or abolish that government and establish a new one.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What document established the first frame of government for the United States after independence?
Answer: The Articles of Confederation (ratified 1781). It was later replaced by the U.S. Constitution (1789).

Key Vocabulary

  • Unalienable rights: Rights that cannot be surrendered or taken away
  • Social contract: The agreement between citizens and their government
  • Tyranny: Cruel or oppressive government rule
  • Sovereignty: Supreme authority to govern
  • Consent of the governed: Government legitimacy requires approval of the people

Topic 02
The United States Constitution
▼

Overview & Context

The U.S. Constitution was written during the Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia during the summer of 1787 and ratified by the required nine states on June 21, 1788. It replaced the weak Articles of Confederation and established the framework of the federal government that continues today.

⭐ Key Fact
The Preamble begins: "We the People of the United States, in Order to form a more perfect Union…" — establishing that authority comes from the people (popular sovereignty).

The Seven Articles

ArticleSubjectKey Provisions
Art. I The Legislature Creates a bicameral Congress (Senate + House); grants specific enumerated powers including taxing, declaring war, regulating commerce
Art. II The Executive Creates the Presidency; qualifications (35 years old, natural-born citizen, 14 years a resident); commander-in-chief; treaty power
Art. III The Judiciary Creates the Supreme Court; defines treason; Congress may establish lower courts
Art. IV States' Relations Full Faith and Credit Clause; Privileges and Immunities; guarantees republican form of government to each state
Art. V Amendment Process Two methods to propose (2/3 of Congress or national convention); two methods to ratify (3/4 of states)
Art. VI Supremacy Clause Constitution is the "supreme Law of the Land"; federal law supersedes conflicting state law
Art. VII Ratification 9 of 13 states needed to ratify; became effective June 21, 1788

Constitutional Principles

🔒 Enumerated Powers

  • Powers explicitly listed in the Constitution
  • Article I, Section 8 lists Congress's powers
  • Examples: coin money, declare war, regulate interstate commerce

🌐 Implied Powers

  • Powers not stated but reasonably inferred
  • Based on the Necessary and Proper ("Elastic") Clause — Art. I, Sec. 8
  • Example: creating a national bank

⚖️ Judicial Review

  • Power of courts to invalidate laws that violate the Constitution
  • Established in Marbury v. Madison (1803)
  • Not explicitly in the Constitution — inferred by Chief Justice Marshall

🚫 Reserved Powers

  • Powers not given to federal government stay with states or the people
  • Guaranteed by the 10th Amendment
  • Examples: marriage law, driver's licenses, education
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the "Supremacy Clause" and where is it found?
Answer: Article VI — it declares the Constitution, federal laws, and treaties to be the supreme law of the land, taking precedence over conflicting state laws.
📝 Typical Exam Question
How many states were required to ratify the Constitution before it took effect?
Answer: 9 out of 13 states.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What document preceded the Constitution and why was it replaced?
Answer: The Articles of Confederation (1781). It was replaced because it created a weak national government with no power to tax, no executive branch, and no authority to regulate commerce between states.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the "Necessary and Proper Clause" and what powers does it grant?
Answer: Article I, Section 8, Clause 18 — gives Congress power to make all laws "necessary and proper" to carry out its enumerated powers. Also called the Elastic Clause because it stretches congressional authority.

The Federalist Papers

The Federalist Papers (1787–88) were 85 essays written by Alexander Hamilton, James Madison, and John Jay (under the pen name "Publius") to persuade New York to ratify the Constitution. Key essays include:

  • Federalist No. 10 (Madison) — dangers of factions; why a large republic protects against tyranny of the majority
  • Federalist No. 51 (Madison) — necessity of checks and balances; "ambition must be made to counteract ambition"
  • Federalist No. 78 (Hamilton) — defense of an independent judiciary and judicial review

Bill of Rights & Constitutional Amendments
▼

The Bill of Rights (Amendments 1–10, ratified 1791)

The Bill of Rights was proposed by James Madison and ratified in 1791. Anti-Federalists demanded it as a condition of ratifying the Constitution. It protects individual liberties from federal government infringement.

📝 Typical Exam Question
Which amendment extended citizenship and equal protection to all persons born or naturalized in the United States?
Answer: The 14th Amendment (1868) — it also includes the Due Process Clause that has been used to apply most Bill of Rights protections to state governments.
📝 Typical Exam Question
The 1st Amendment prohibits Congress from making laws that do what?
Answer: Establish a religion (Establishment Clause) or prohibit the free exercise of religion; abridge freedom of speech, press, assembly, or the right to petition the government.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What does "incorporation" mean in the context of the Bill of Rights?
Answer: Through the 14th Amendment's Due Process Clause, the Supreme Court has applied (incorporated) most Bill of Rights protections against state governments, not just the federal government.
📝 Typical Exam Question
Which amendment abolished slavery? Which gave women the right to vote?
Answer: 13th Amendment (1865) abolished slavery. 19th Amendment (1920) granted women's suffrage.

Topic 04
The Three Branches of Government
▼
⭐ Key Concept
The separation of powers was inspired by French philosopher Montesquieu's The Spirit of the Laws (1748). The Framers intentionally divided government into three branches to prevent any single person or group from gaining too much power.

🏛️ Legislative Branch (Congress) — Article I

🏠 House of Representatives

  • 435 members total
  • 2-year terms
  • Apportioned by state population
  • Must be 25 years old, 7-year citizen
  • Initiates all revenue (tax) bills
  • Has sole power of impeachment

🏛️ Senate

  • 100 members (2 per state)
  • 6-year terms (staggered)
  • Must be 30 years old, 9-year citizen
  • Confirms presidential appointments
  • Ratifies treaties (2/3 vote)
  • Conducts impeachment trials

🏢 Executive Branch (President) — Article II

  • Qualifications: Natural-born citizen, at least 35 years old, 14-year U.S. resident
  • Term: 4 years; limited to 2 terms by 22nd Amendment (1951)
  • Election: Via the Electoral College — electors chosen by states (number = senators + representatives)
  • Roles: Commander-in-Chief of the military; chief diplomat; chief executive; head of state
  • Powers: Veto legislation; grant pardons; nominate federal judges and cabinet officers; make executive agreements and treaties
  • Presidential Succession: Vice President → Speaker of the House → President Pro Tempore of the Senate → Cabinet officers in order of creation

⚖️ Judicial Branch (Federal Courts) — Article III

  • Supreme Court: 9 justices (1 Chief Justice + 8 Associate Justices); life tenure (during good behavior)
  • Appointment: Nominated by the President; confirmed by the Senate
  • Jurisdiction: Original jurisdiction in limited cases; appellate jurisdiction over lower courts
  • Judicial Review: Power to declare laws unconstitutional (Marbury v. Madison, 1803)
  • Court Hierarchy: U.S. District Courts → U.S. Courts of Appeals → U.S. Supreme Court

Checks and Balances — How They Work

BranchChecks on LegislativeChecks on ExecutiveChecks on Judicial
Congress — Override veto (2/3 vote); confirm appointments; control budget; impeach/remove Confirm judges; impeach judges; amend the Constitution; change court jurisdiction
President Veto bills; call special sessions; recommend legislation — Nominate judges; pardon power
Courts Declare laws unconstitutional Declare executive actions unconstitutional —
📝 Typical Exam Question
How many members are in the U.S. House of Representatives? How many are in the Senate?
Answer: House: 435 members. Senate: 100 members (2 per state).
📝 Typical Exam Question
What happens if a president vetoes a bill? How can Congress respond?
Answer: The bill does not become law. Congress can override the veto with a 2/3 vote in both the House and Senate.
📝 Typical Exam Question
Which branch of government has the power to declare war?
Answer: Congress (Article I). However, the President is Commander-in-Chief of the military and may deploy forces. The War Powers Act (1973) limits presidential military action without congressional approval to 60 days.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the process for impeachment?
Answer: The House of Representatives votes to impeach (simple majority). The Senate then holds a trial, presided over by the Chief Justice. A 2/3 Senate vote is required to convict and remove the official from office.

Topic 05
Federalism & State Government
▼

What Is Federalism?

Federalism is the division of power between the national (federal) government and state governments. The U.S. federal system was a compromise between those who wanted a strong national government and those who wanted states to remain powerful. Neither level of government is subordinate to the other in its own sphere.

🇺🇸 Delegated (Enumerated) Powers — Federal

  • Coin money
  • Declare war / raise armies
  • Regulate interstate & foreign commerce
  • Conduct foreign policy / make treaties
  • Run the postal service
  • Establish federal courts

🌴 Reserved Powers — States

  • Establish local governments
  • Regulate intrastate commerce
  • Conduct elections
  • Establish public schools
  • Marriage and divorce laws
  • Issue licenses (drivers, professional)

🤝 Concurrent Powers — Shared

  • Collect taxes
  • Build roads
  • Borrow money
  • Establish courts
  • Create and enforce laws
  • Spend for general welfare

🚫 Denied Powers — Neither Level

  • States cannot make treaties
  • States cannot coin money
  • Neither may grant titles of nobility
  • Neither may pass ex post facto laws
  • Neither may pass bills of attainder

Types of Federalism Over Time

1789–1865
Dual Federalism ("Layer Cake"): Federal and state governments operate separately in distinct spheres. States had broad powers; federal government was limited.
1933–1964
Cooperative Federalism ("Marble Cake"): Federal and state programs become intertwined. New Deal era grants-in-aid; shared goals and administration.
1964–1980
Creative / Coercive Federalism: Federal government sets conditions on grants; uses fiscal power to influence state policy. Great Society programs; unfunded mandates emerge.
1980–present
New Federalism / Devolution: Efforts to return power to states; block grants replacing categorical grants; state flexibility increased.

Interstate Relations — Article IV

  • Full Faith and Credit Clause: States must recognize the public acts, records, and court proceedings of other states (e.g., driver's licenses, birth certificates)
  • Privileges and Immunities Clause: States cannot discriminate against citizens of other states in basic rights
  • Extradition: States must return fugitives wanted in other states
  • Interstate Compacts: States may enter agreements with each other (with congressional approval for significant compacts)
📝 Typical Exam Question
Which amendment reserves powers not delegated to the federal government to the states or the people?
Answer: The 10th Amendment.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is a "block grant" and how does it differ from a "categorical grant"?
Answer: A block grant gives states money for a broad policy area with few restrictions (more flexibility). A categorical grant is given for a specific, narrowly-defined purpose with strict conditions on how the money must be spent.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What does the "Full Faith and Credit Clause" require?
Answer: States must recognize and honor the public records, acts, and court decisions of other states (e.g., a valid marriage in one state must be recognized in all states).

Topic 06
The Legislative Process — How a Bill Becomes Law
▼

Steps for a Bill to Become Law

Step 1
Introduction: A member of Congress introduces a bill. In the House, it is dropped in the "hopper." Bills can originate in either chamber, except revenue bills which must originate in the House.
Step 2
Committee Referral: The bill is sent to the appropriate standing committee. The committee may hold hearings, mark up (amend) the bill, pass it, or "table" (kill) it. Most bills die in committee.
Step 3
Floor Debate & Vote: The full chamber debates and votes on the bill. In the Senate, a bill can be filibustered — a senator may speak indefinitely to delay a vote. A cloture vote (60 senators) ends a filibuster.
Step 4
Other Chamber: The bill must pass the other chamber (House or Senate) in identical form. If it passes with changes, a Conference Committee reconciles the differences.
Step 5
Presidential Action: The President may: (1) Sign the bill — it becomes law; (2) Veto the bill — returned to Congress; (3) Take no action — if Congress is in session for 10 days, it becomes law; if Congress adjourns within 10 days, it is a "pocket veto."
Step 6
Veto Override: Congress can override a presidential veto with a 2/3 majority vote in both chambers. The bill then becomes law without the President's signature.

Types of Congressional Powers

  • Enumerated (Expressed) Powers: Explicitly listed in Article I, Sec. 8 (e.g., coin money, declare war, regulate commerce)
  • Implied Powers: Reasonably inferred from the Necessary and Proper Clause
  • Non-legislative Powers: Electoral duties, oversight of executive agencies, confirmation of appointments, ratification of treaties (Senate), amendment ratification

Congressional Leadership

🏠 House Leadership

  • Speaker of the House: Elected by full House; 2nd in line to presidency
  • Majority Leader: Manages floor agenda
  • Minority Leader: Leads opposition
  • Whips: Ensure party members vote together

🏛️ Senate Leadership

  • Vice President: President of the Senate; votes only to break ties
  • President Pro Tempore: Senior majority senator; presides in VP's absence
  • Majority/Minority Leaders: Control legislative agenda
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is a "filibuster" and how can it be stopped in the Senate?
Answer: A filibuster is a tactic where senators speak at length to delay or prevent a vote. It is ended by cloture — a vote of 60 senators to close debate.
📝 Typical Exam Question
Where must revenue (tax) bills originate?
Answer: All revenue bills must originate in the House of Representatives (Article I, Section 7).
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is a "pocket veto"?
Answer: If the President takes no action on a bill and Congress adjourns within 10 days of receiving it, the bill dies — a "pocket veto." No override is possible because Congress is not in session.

Topic 07
Elections & Voting Rights
▼

The Electoral College

The President of the United States is not elected by direct popular vote. Instead, the Framers created the Electoral College — a group of electors chosen by each state to formally cast votes for President and Vice President.

  • Total Electors: 538 (one per member of Congress = 435 House + 100 Senate + 3 for D.C. per the 23rd Amendment)
  • Majority Needed: 270 electoral votes to win the presidency
  • Winner-Take-All: Most states award all their electors to the popular vote winner in that state (Maine and Nebraska use proportional systems)
  • Contingent Election: If no candidate reaches 270, the House of Representatives chooses the President (one vote per state delegation), and the Senate chooses the Vice President
  • 12th Amendment (1804): Requires separate ballots for President and Vice President

Expansion of Voting Rights — Timeline

1789
Original Constitution: voting qualifications left to states; largely limited to white male property owners
1870
15th Amendment: Cannot deny vote based on race, color, or previous servitude; gave formerly enslaved men the right to vote
1920
19th Amendment: Women's suffrage — women granted the right to vote nationwide
1924
Native Americans granted citizenship (Indian Citizenship Act)
1964
24th Amendment: Abolished poll taxes in federal elections (disenfranchised poor voters, especially Black citizens in the South)
1965
Voting Rights Act of 1965: Prohibited discriminatory voting practices; federal oversight of elections in states with history of discrimination
1971
26th Amendment: Lowered voting age from 21 to 18 — driven by Vietnam War protests ("old enough to fight, old enough to vote")

Types of Primaries & Elections

  • Primary Election: Voters select a party's candidate for the general election; may be open (any voter) or closed (registered party members only)
  • General Election: Final election where voters choose among party nominees and independents
  • Caucus: Party members meet in groups to discuss and select candidates (e.g., Iowa caucuses)
  • Runoff Election: Held when no candidate receives the required percentage of votes in a primary
  • Special Election: Called to fill a vacant seat between regular elections
  • Recall Election: Allows voters to remove an elected official before their term ends

Congressional Elections

  • Midterm Elections: Held every 2 years between presidential elections; all 435 House seats and 1/3 of Senate seats are contested
  • Redistricting: Drawing of congressional district boundaries; occurs every 10 years after the census
  • Gerrymandering: Manipulating district boundaries to favor a party or group — named after Governor Elbridge Gerry of Massachusetts (1812)
📝 Typical Exam Question
How many electoral votes are needed to win the presidency?
Answer: 270 out of 538 total electoral votes.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What happens if no presidential candidate wins a majority of electoral votes?
Answer: The election goes to the House of Representatives, where each state delegation casts one vote to choose the President. This is called a contingent election. The Senate chooses the Vice President.
📝 Typical Exam Question
Which amendment gave 18-year-olds the right to vote?
Answer: The 26th Amendment (1971).
📝 Typical Exam Question
What was the purpose of the Voting Rights Act of 1965?
Answer: It prohibited racially discriminatory voting practices (such as literacy tests) and provided for federal oversight of elections in states and counties with a history of discrimination. It was meant to enforce the 15th Amendment.

Topic 08
Civil Rights & Equal Protection
▼

Civil Rights vs. Civil Liberties

🛡️ Civil Liberties

  • Freedoms that protect individuals from government
  • Primarily in the Bill of Rights
  • Examples: freedom of speech, right to bear arms, protection from unreasonable search
  • "What the government cannot do to you"

⚖️ Civil Rights

  • Rights that ensure equal treatment under the law
  • Rooted in the 14th Amendment's Equal Protection Clause
  • Examples: freedom from discrimination in housing, employment, voting
  • "What the government must do for you"

The Civil Rights Movement – Key Events

1954
Brown v. Board of Education: Supreme Court unanimously ruled segregated public schools unconstitutional — overturned Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)'s "separate but equal" doctrine
1955
Montgomery Bus Boycott: Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat; 381-day boycott desegregated Montgomery buses; elevated Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.
1963
March on Washington: 250,000+ marchers; Dr. King delivered "I Have a Dream" speech at the Lincoln Memorial
1964
Civil Rights Act of 1964: Banned discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin in employment and public accommodations
1965
Voting Rights Act of 1965: Outlawed discriminatory voting practices; followed "Bloody Sunday" on the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama
1968
Fair Housing Act: Prohibited discrimination in sale, rental, and financing of housing based on race, religion, national origin

Key Landmark Supreme Court Cases

CaseYearDecision
Marbury v. Madison1803Established judicial review — courts can strike down unconstitutional laws
McCulloch v. Maryland1819Upheld implied powers; federal law supreme over state law
Dred Scott v. Sandford1857Denied citizenship to Black Americans — later overturned by 13th & 14th Amendments
Plessy v. Ferguson1896Established "separate but equal" doctrine — upheld racial segregation
Brown v. Board of Education1954Overturned Plessy; segregated schools are unconstitutional
Miranda v. Arizona1966Suspects must be informed of their rights upon arrest (Miranda rights)
Roe v. Wade1973Established constitutional right to abortion — overturned by Dobbs v. Jackson (2022)
Tinker v. Des Moines1969Students retain 1st Amendment rights in school; "students don't shed rights at the schoolhouse gate"
Obergefell v. Hodges2015Legalized same-sex marriage nationwide under the 14th Amendment
📝 Typical Exam Question
What constitutional clause did the Supreme Court use to end school segregation in Brown v. Board of Education?
Answer: The Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What are "Miranda rights" and what case established them?
Answer: Miranda v. Arizona (1966). Police must inform suspects of: the right to remain silent; that anything said can be used against them; the right to an attorney; and that if they cannot afford one, one will be appointed.

Topic 09
Florida Government & Constitution
▼
⚠️ Florida-Specific Content: This section is especially important for the Florida Civics Literacy Exam, which places emphasis on Florida government, history, and civic engagement alongside federal civics content.

Florida's State Government Structure

Like the federal government, Florida's state government is divided into three branches:

🏛️ Legislative Branch

  • Florida Legislature — bicameral
  • Senate: 40 members, 4-year terms
  • House: 120 members, 2-year terms
  • Term limits: 8 consecutive years in each chamber
  • Meets in Tallahassee (the state capital)
  • Regular session: 60 days each year

🏢 Executive Branch

  • Governor: 4-year term; max 2 consecutive terms
  • Cabinet: Attorney General, Chief Financial Officer, Commissioner of Agriculture
  • Governor nominates state judges from list provided by Judicial Nominating Commissions
  • Can veto legislation and line-item veto budget items

Florida Constitution — Key Features

  • Current Constitution: Florida's Constitution of 1968 (the sixth Florida constitution)
  • Declaration of Rights: Article I — includes all rights in the U.S. Bill of Rights plus additional Florida protections (e.g., right to privacy, right to work)
  • Constitutional Amendments: Can be placed on the ballot by the Legislature (joint resolution), citizens' initiative (petition with valid signatures equal to 8% of votes cast in last presidential election in 2/3 of congressional districts), constitutional revision commission (every 20 years), taxation and budget reform commission, or court ruling
  • Approval Threshold: Constitutional amendments must receive at least 60% voter approval to pass
  • Right to Privacy: Florida's constitution explicitly guarantees a right to privacy — broader than the federal implied right

Florida Judicial System

  • Supreme Court: 7 justices; highest court; final authority on Florida law
  • District Courts of Appeal (5): Intermediate appellate courts; review trial court decisions
  • Circuit Courts (20 circuits): Major trial courts; handle felonies, civil cases over $30,000, family law, probate
  • County Courts: Handle misdemeanors, civil cases under $30,000, traffic violations
  • Merit Retention: Appellate judges face merit retention votes (not contested elections)

Florida History Highlights

1513
Juan Ponce de León arrives; named the land "La Florida" (Spanish for "Flowery" or "Land of Flowers")
1565
St. Augustine founded by Pedro Menéndez de Avilés — oldest continuously inhabited European settlement in the U.S.
1821
Spain cedes Florida to the United States (Adams-Onís Treaty)
1845
Florida admitted to the Union as the 27th state (March 3, 1845)
1861
Florida secedes from the Union; joins the Confederacy during the Civil War
1868
Florida readmitted to the Union after the Civil War
1969
Apollo 11 launches from Kennedy Space Center, Cape Canaveral — first Moon landing

Florida State Symbols

  • State Motto: "In God We Trust"
  • State Nickname: The Sunshine State
  • State Bird: Northern Mockingbird
  • State Flower: Orange Blossom
  • State Tree: Sabal Palm (Cabbage Palm)
  • State Song: "Swanee River (Old Folks at Home)" by Stephen Foster
  • Capital: Tallahassee
  • Largest City: Jacksonville
📝 Typical Exam Question
How many members are in the Florida Senate and House of Representatives?
Answer: Florida Senate: 40 members. Florida House of Representatives: 120 members.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What percentage of votes is required to pass a constitutional amendment in Florida?
Answer: 60% of votes cast on the amendment in a general election.
📝 Typical Exam Question
When was Florida admitted to the United States as a state?
Answer: March 3, 1845 — Florida became the 27th state.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the oldest continuously inhabited European settlement in the United States?
Answer: St. Augustine, Florida — founded in 1565 by Pedro Menéndez de Avilés under the Spanish crown.

Topic 10
U.S. Foreign Policy & National Security
▼

Foreign Policy Authority

Foreign policy power in the U.S. is shared between the executive and legislative branches, a principle called "invitation to struggle":

  • President: Commander-in-Chief; negotiates treaties; appoints ambassadors; recognizes foreign governments; executive agreements (no Senate approval needed)
  • Senate: Ratifies treaties (requires 2/3 vote); confirms ambassadors; declares war (shared with House)
  • Congress: Controls the budget for military and foreign aid; can pass legislation affecting trade and sanctions
  • War Powers Act (1973): Limits presidential military deployments to 60 days without congressional authorization (enacted over Nixon's veto after Vietnam)

Key Foreign Policy Doctrines & Events

1823
Monroe Doctrine: Declared Western Hemisphere off-limits to European colonization; U.S. would view interference as a threat to national security
1918
Wilson's 14 Points: Post-WWI framework for peace; included concept of self-determination and the League of Nations
1947
Truman Doctrine & Marshall Plan: U.S. commits to containing Soviet communism; economic aid to rebuild war-torn Europe
1947
National Security Act (1947): Created the CIA, NSC (National Security Council), and the Department of Defense; unified military command
1949
NATO Founded: North Atlantic Treaty Organization — collective defense alliance (Article 5: attack on one is attack on all)
1962
Cuban Missile Crisis: 13-day standoff between U.S. and Soviet Union over nuclear missiles in Cuba; resolved diplomatically by Kennedy and Khrushchev
1991
Cold War ends with dissolution of the Soviet Union; U.S. emerges as the sole superpower
2001
September 11 attacks: Led to the War on Terror, invasion of Afghanistan, USA PATRIOT Act, and creation of the Department of Homeland Security

Key International Organizations

  • United Nations (UN): Founded 1945; 193 member states; promotes peace, security, human rights, and development; Security Council (5 permanent members: U.S., UK, France, Russia, China) has veto power
  • NATO: Military alliance of 32 democracies; founded 1949; collective defense commitment
  • World Bank & IMF: International financial institutions providing loans and stabilization for developing nations
  • WTO (World Trade Organization): Governs international trade rules; resolves trade disputes
  • OAS (Organization of American States): Regional body for Western Hemisphere cooperation
📝 Typical Exam Question
What does the Monroe Doctrine state and when was it issued?
Answer: Issued in 1823 by President James Monroe — it declared that European powers could not establish new colonies in the Americas and that any attempt to extend their influence would be considered a threat to U.S. security.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the significance of Article 5 of the NATO Treaty?
Answer: Article 5 is the collective defense provision — an armed attack against one NATO member is considered an attack against all members, obligating mutual defense. It has only been invoked once, following the September 11, 2001 attacks.

Topic 11
Free Enterprise System & Economic Principles
▼

The American Free Enterprise System

The United States operates a mixed market economy based primarily on free enterprise (capitalism) principles, with some government regulation and social programs. Florida's constitution specifically protects the right to work and free enterprise.

✅ Characteristics of Free Enterprise

  • Private ownership of property and businesses
  • Profit motive drives production
  • Competition determines prices
  • Freedom of choice for consumers and producers
  • Limited government intervention

🏛️ Government's Role in the Economy

  • Enforce contracts and property rights
  • Regulate monopolies (antitrust laws)
  • Provide public goods (roads, defense)
  • Address externalities (pollution regulation)
  • Stabilize the economy (fiscal & monetary policy)

Key Economic Concepts

  • Supply and Demand: When supply increases, prices tend to fall; when demand increases, prices tend to rise; the market reaches equilibrium where supply equals demand
  • Gross Domestic Product (GDP): Total value of all goods and services produced in the country in one year — the primary measure of economic size
  • Inflation: General rise in price levels; reduces purchasing power; measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI)
  • Recession: Two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth; characterized by high unemployment and reduced economic activity
  • Fiscal Policy: Government use of spending and taxation to influence the economy (Congress and the President control this)
  • Monetary Policy: Control of the money supply and interest rates by the Federal Reserve (independent central bank)
  • Federal Reserve ("The Fed"): Central bank of the U.S.; controls interest rates and money supply; promotes maximum employment and stable prices
  • National Debt vs. Budget Deficit: A deficit is when annual spending exceeds revenue; the national debt is the accumulated total of all past deficits

The Federal Budget Process

  • The President submits a proposed budget to Congress each February
  • Congress passes a budget resolution and 12 appropriations bills
  • Mandatory Spending: Required by law — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, interest on the national debt (~70% of budget)
  • Discretionary Spending: Set annually by Congress — defense, education, transportation (~30% of budget)
  • Revenue comes primarily from income taxes, payroll taxes, and corporate taxes
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the role of the Federal Reserve in the U.S. economy?
Answer: The Federal Reserve is the central bank of the U.S. It conducts monetary policy by controlling interest rates and the money supply to promote maximum employment, stable prices, and moderate long-term interest rates.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What is the difference between fiscal policy and monetary policy?
Answer: Fiscal policy = government taxing and spending decisions (controlled by Congress and the President). Monetary policy = control of money supply and interest rates (controlled by the Federal Reserve, which is independent).

1776–1789

1861–1865

1865–1877

1877–1920

1929–1945

1945–1991

1991–present
Post-Cold War & Modern Era: U.S. as lone superpower; Gulf War; September 11 & War on Terror; Great Recession (2008); COVID-19 pandemic (2020)

Founding Fathers — Key Figures

  • George Washington: Commander-in-Chief of Continental Army; 1st President; set precedent of two-term limit; "Father of His Country"
  • John Adams: 2nd President; Federalist; helped draft Declaration; negotiated peace with Britain
  • Thomas Jefferson: Primary author of the Declaration of Independence; 3rd President; Louisiana Purchase; Democratic-Republican
  • James Madison: "Father of the Constitution"; co-wrote Federalist Papers; 4th President; proposed Bill of Rights
  • Alexander Hamilton: 1st Secretary of the Treasury; created national bank; strong Federalist; co-wrote Federalist Papers
  • Benjamin Franklin: Diplomat; inventor; delegate to Constitutional Convention; helped negotiate Treaty of Paris (1783)
  • John Jay: 1st Chief Justice of the Supreme Court; co-wrote Federalist Papers; negotiated Jay's Treaty with Britain
📝 Typical Exam Question
What was the significance of the Emancipation Proclamation and what did it actually do?
Answer: Issued January 1, 1863, it declared enslaved people in Confederate states "forever free." It did not free enslaved people in border states (which remained in the Union). It transformed the Civil War's purpose to include the abolition of slavery and prevented European powers from supporting the Confederacy.
📝 Typical Exam Question
Who wrote Common Sense and why was it important?
Answer: Thomas Paine wrote Common Sense (1776). It was one of the most widely read pamphlets in American history, arguing in plain language for independence from Britain and republican government. It helped turn colonial public opinion toward revolution.
📝 Typical Exam Question
What were the Reconstruction Amendments and what did each accomplish?
Answer: 13th Amendment (1865) — abolished slavery. 14th Amendment (1868) — granted citizenship to all born or naturalized in the U.S.; equal protection. 15th Amendment (1870) — prohibited denying the right to vote based on race.

Test-Taking Tips & High-Priority Review Items
▼

📌 Most Frequently Tested Topics

  • The three branches of government, their powers, and checks and balances
  • The Bill of Rights — what each of the first 10 amendments protects
  • The Electoral College process and required electoral votes (270)
  • How a bill becomes a law (including filibusters, veto, and override)
  • The 14th Amendment — citizenship, due process, equal protection
  • Key Supreme Court cases (especially Brown, Miranda, Marbury, and Tinker)
  • Florida-specific content: constitution, admission date, legislature structure
  • Constitutional amendments — especially Reconstruction Amendments (13th, 14th, 15th) and voting amendments (19th, 24th, 26th)
  • The Declaration of Independence — purpose, principles, and authorship
  • Federalism — types of powers and the Supremacy Clause

🧠 Memory Strategies

  • Amendments Trick: Amendments 1–10 = Bill of Rights; odd-numbered amendments after 10 often deal with electoral/governmental structure; amendments in the teens and twenties often deal with voting rights expansion
  • Articles 1-2-3: "LEJ" — Legislative, Executive, Judicial — in that order
  • Senate vs. House: Senate = "Seniority/6 years/60 cloture"; House = "Hustling/2 years/revenue bills"
  • Checks mnemonic: "PVC" — President vetoes, Veto can be overridden, Courts can strike down (judicial review)
  • Florida specifics: 40 senators, 120 house members, 60% for amendments, 27th state, March 3, 1845

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing the Bill of Rights (protects from federal government) with general civil rights laws
  • Thinking the President can declare war — only Congress can declare war
  • Confusing impeachment (House charges) with removal (Senate conviction)
  • Forgetting that treaty ratification requires 2/3 of the Senate (not a simple majority)
  • Mixing up enumerated powers (listed) vs. reserved powers (kept by states)
  • Thinking the Supreme Court has 10 or 12 justices — it has 9
  • Assuming the Emancipation Proclamation freed all enslaved people — it only applied to Confederate states
